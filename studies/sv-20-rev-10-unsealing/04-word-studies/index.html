    Word Studies¶

    From Word Studies Library¶

    bibliaridion (G974) — "little book"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G974-bibliaridion.md

    • Double diminutive: biblos → biblion → bibliaridion
    • 4 occurrences, ALL in Revelation 10 (vv.2, 8, 9, 10)
    • Confined to a single chapter — essentially a Johannine coinage
    • KJV: "little book" (3x), "a little book" (1x)
    • Key collocations: anoigo (open, 2x), katesthio (devour, 2x), glykys (sweet, 2x), pikraino (bitter, 2x)
    • No LXX occurrence — no direct Hebrew source
    • Parallels Ezekiel's megillah (H4039, "scroll/roll") in the eating-the-scroll motif
    • The diminutive form relative to biblion (G975) suggests a smaller document extracted from or representing a portion of a larger scroll

    biblion (G975) — "book/scroll"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G975-biblion.md

    • First diminutive of biblos; 28 unique verses, 34 tokens
    • Overwhelmingly concentrated in Revelation (18/28 = 64%)
    • In Revelation: used for the sealed scroll (Rev 5:1-9), the book of life (13:8, 17:8, 20:12, 21:27), and the book of prophecy (22:7, 22:18-19)
    • NOTE: In Rev 10:8, the heavenly voice calls the little book "to biblion to eneogmenon" (the book, the one having been opened) — shifting from bibliaridion (v.2) to biblion. This terminological shift may identify the little book as a portion of the larger biblion.
    • Hebrew source: sepher (H5612, 142 co-occurrences) — the standard OT scroll/book term
    • LXX bridge to chatham (H2856, sealing) with PMI 5.52

    anoigo (G455) — "to open"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G455-anoigo.md

    • 78 tokens across 75 verses; Revelation has highest concentration (25 occurrences, 6.2/100v)
    • Aorist dominates (71%); perfect tense at 17% — concentrated in Revelation's "already open" descriptions
    • Perfect passive participle cluster in Revelation: Rev 3:8 (open door), 4:1 (door opened in heaven), 10:2 and 10:8 (little book open), 19:11 (heaven opened) — ALL describe an entity whose opened state is a settled condition
    • Aorist active forms in the seal-breaking sequence (Rev 6:1-8:1): the Lamb performs discrete opening acts (enoixen, V-AAI-3S)
    • Hebrew source: pathach (H6605, 85 co-occurrences, PMI 31.1) — one of the strongest LXX bridges

    chronos (G5550) — "time"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5550-chronos.md

    • 49 verses across 15 books; denotes measured duration/elapsed time-span
    • Distinguished from kairos (G2540, appointed moment), aion (G165, age/epoch), hora (G5610, hour/moment)
    • Semantic categories: (1) extended duration, (2) fulfilled/completed time, (3) sovereign times of history, (4) pre-temporal, (5) brief remaining period
    • In Revelation (4 occurrences): Rev 2:21 (space to repent), 6:11 (little season), 10:6 (time no longer), 20:3 (little season)
    • All Revelation uses = a measured duration or allotted span
    • Rev 10:6: anarthrous chronos (qualitative — "time/delay as a concept"), ouketi (no longer — presupposes prior existence), estai (future — prospective declaration)

    kairos (G2540) — "appointed time"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2540-kairos.md

    • 79 occurrences; emphasizes the fitting, appointed, opportune moment
    • Distinguished from chronos (duration) — see WG-time.md for the contrast
    • NOT used in Rev 10:6. The oath terminates chronos (measured duration), not kairos (appointed occasion). This distinction matters: the angel declares the end of time-as-delay, not the end of appointed seasons.

    dei (G1163) — "it is necessary" (divine compulsion)¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1163-dei.md

    • 104 verses; impersonal verb expressing necessity/compulsion
    • KJV: "must" (55%), "ought" (25%)
    • Semantic categories: (1) Messianic necessity, (2) eschatological necessity, (3) theological necessity, (4) divine mission/prophetic compulsion, (5) ethical obligation, (6) cosmic salvific necessity
    • In Revelation (8 occurrences): Rev 1:1 (things which must come to pass), 4:1 (things which must be), 10:11 (thou must prophesy again), 11:5, 17:10, 20:3, 22:6
    • Rev 10:11: dei + accusative subject (se) + aorist infinitive (prophetuesai) = "you must prophesy" — present tense dei marks the necessity as currently operative; the aorist infinitive treats the prophesying as a complete, bounded task

    mysterion (G3466) — "mystery"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3466-mysterion.md

    • 27 verses across 10 books; from myo (to shut the mouth)
    • Concentrated in Pauline prison epistles (Col + Eph = 10/27)
    • Semantic categories: (1) mysteries of the kingdom, (2) God's pre-temporal secret now disclosed, (3) mystery of Christ among Gentiles, (4) eschatological/apocalyptic mysteries, (5) encoded symbolic meaning
    • Rev 10:7: "the mystery of God should be finished" — etelesthe (aorist passive of teleo) is a proleptic aorist viewing the finishing as already complete
    • Hebrew/Aramaic source: raz (H7328, 8 co-occurrences, PMI 25.20) — the primary bridge is Daniel 2 (Nebuchadnezzar's dream as raz revealed by God)

    sphragizo (G4972) — "to seal"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4972-sphragizo.md

    • 25 occurrences; Revelation has highest concentration (18, 4.5/100v)
    • Functions: physical sealing (security), authentication (confirming identity), spiritual marking (divine ownership)
    • Rev 10:4: sphragison (aorist active imperative) — "seal up" the seven thunders' words. This is the ONLY sealing act within Revelation's larger unsealing program.

    katasphragizo (G2696) — "to seal closely"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2696-katasphragizo.md

    • HAPAX LEGOMENON — only Rev 5:1
    • kata- prefix intensifies: to seal up completely
    • Perfect passive participle (V-RPP-ASN): the book has been sealed and remains in that sealed state
    • LXX parallel: Job 9:7 (God sealing the stars — same intensive compound)
    • Represents the MAXIMUM sealing point in the vocabulary arc

    telos (G5056) — "end/goal"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5056-telos.md

    • Connected to teleo (G5055, "to finish/complete") — the root of etelesthe in Rev 10:7 and tetelestai in John 19:30
    • The raz-mysterion chain terminates at Rev 10:7 with the same root as Christ's cross-cry: the mystery of God "was finished" (etelesthe)

    bronte (G1027) — "thunder"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1027-bronte.md

    • The seven thunders (Rev 10:3-4) speak with their own voices — personified as communicating entities

    Newly Generated Library Entries (Phase 3.5)¶

    omnyo (G3660) — "to swear"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3660-omnyo.md

    • 21 unique verses (27 tokens) across 7 NT books
    • Prolonged form of primary verb omo; to swear, take an oath
    • Aorist forms dominate (73%) — swearing treated as discrete completed act
    • KJV: exclusively "swear/sware/sworn" — no translation variation
    • Distribution: Matthew (8), Hebrews (6), Mark (2), Acts (2), Luke (1), James (1), Revelation (1 — Rev 10:6)
    • Semantic categories: (1) Jesus' prohibition of human oaths, (2) critique of casuistic temple oaths, (3) God's irrevocable oath to patriarchs, (4) God's oath of wrath/exclusion, (5) Melchizedek priesthood oath, (6) human rash oaths, (7) angelic/eschatological oath — Rev 10:6
    • Rev 10:6 is the ONLY Revelation occurrence — an angelic/eschatological oath unique in the corpus
    • Hebrew source: shaba (H7650, 143 co-occurrences, PMI 34.74) — near one-to-one bridge
    • Collocations: theos (God, 24%), horkos (oath, 19%), ouranos (heaven, 19%)

    shaba (H7650) — "to swear"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H7650-shaba.md

    • 175 unique verses (217 tokens) across 27 books
    • Denominative from sheba (H7651, "seven") — to seven oneself, to swear
    • Stems: Niphal 83% (reflexive, "bind oneself by oath"), Hiphil 17% (causative, "make someone swear")
    • Deuteronomy dominates (33 occurrences, 19%)
    • Dan 12:7: wayyishaba (Niphal.Wayyiqtol.3ms) — "and he sware"
    • Semantic categories: (1) divine oath to patriarchs, (2) divine oath of judgment/decree, (3) interpersonal covenant oath, (4) adjuration, (5) solemn charge, (6) personal vow, (7) false swearing
    • Dan 12:7 category: divine oath of decree — the angel swears by the Eternal One
    • LXX mapping: shaba → omnyo (G3660) with 143 co-occurrences — strongest oath-verb bridge
    • The root connection to "seven" (sheba) links etymologically to covenant-making through the number seven

    prophetes (G4396) — "prophet"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4396-prophetes.md

    • 143 verses (149 tokens) across 15 books
    • Compound: pro (before/forth) + phemi (to speak) — one who speaks forth
    • Synoptic Gospels + Acts = 89% of all tokens
    • Revelation: 8 occurrences (2.0/100v) — in eschatological witness category
    • Rev 10:7 "his servants the prophets" — prophets as recipients of God's advance declaration
    • Rev 10:11 uses the cognate verb propheteuo (G4395) — "thou must prophesy again"
    • Word family: propheteia (G4394, prophecy), propheteuo (G4395, to prophesy), prophetikos (G4397, prophetic)

    pikros (G4089) / pikraino (G4087) — "bitter / to make bitter"¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4089-pikros.md

    • G4089 pikros (adjective): 2 occurrences (both James 3:11, 14)
    • G4087 pikraino (verb): 4 occurrences — Col 3:19, Rev 8:11, Rev 10:9, Rev 10:10
    • G4088 pikria (noun): 4 occurrences — Acts 8:23, Rom 3:14, Eph 4:31, Heb 12:15
    • G4090 pikros (adverb): 2 occurrences — Matt 26:75, Luke 22:62 (Peter wept bitterly)

    Grammar Reference Data¶

    Rev 10:1-11 Passage Analysis¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/grammar-reference/passages/rev-10-1-11.md

    Four load-bearing grammatical features identified:

    1. eneogmenon (V-RPP-ASN) — Perfect passive participle of anoigo (G455) at vv.2, 8. Marks the little book as existing in a PERMANENTLY OPENED STATE. The tense describes condition, not event. Repeated twice for emphasis. Contrasts with the aorist active forms of the seal-breaking sequence (Rev 6-8).

    2. omosen (V-AAI-3S) — Aorist active indicative of omnyo (G3660) at v.6. Reports the oath as a completed act. The oath formula "omosen en to zonti eis tous aionas ton aionon" directly parallels Dan 12:7 LXX: "en to zonti eis ton aiona." Same verb, same preposition, same substantival participle.

    3. chronos ouketi estai — Future indicative negation. Anarthrous chronos (qualitative), ouketi (no longer — presupposes prior existence), estai (future — prospective). The oath content is future-oriented, not already accomplished.

    4. dei se palin prophetuesai — Impersonal necessity (dei, present) + accusative subject (se) + again (palin) + aorist infinitive (prophetuesai). The climactic commission. Palin modifies the infinitive (prophesy-again), not dei (it-is-necessary-again). The aorist infinitive (only aorist of propheteuo in NT) treats the commission as a complete, bounded task.

    Additional grammatical observations: - v.4: sphragison (aorist imperative of sphragizo) — seal up the thunders as a single decisive act - v.7: etelesthe (aorist passive of teleo) — proleptic/prophetic aorist: future event described as already completed - v.8: to biblion to eneogmenon — shift from bibliaridion to biblion; same perfect passive participle - v.9: legei (historic present) — vivid narrative device foregrounding the angel's speech - v.10: epikranthe (aorist passive of pikraino) — the belly was made bitter by the scroll's content - v.11: legousin (present active indicative 3rd PLURAL) — "they say," not "he said"; unexpressed plural subject

    Perfect Passive Participle (V-RPP)¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/grammar-reference/greek/participle-perfect-passive-state.md

    The V-RPP describes a STATE resulting from a prior completed action. The paradigmatic example: Rev 5:6 "a Lamb as it had been slain" (esphagmenon, V-RPP-ASN) — the Lamb carries the enduring condition of having-been-slain while standing alive. Applied to Rev 10:2: the little book carries the enduring condition of having-been-opened. The opening act is past; the open state persists throughout the chapter.

    Weqatal in Oath Apodosis¶

    Source: D:/bible/bible-studies/grammar-reference/hebrew/weqatal-apodosis-vow.md

    Relevant to Dan 12:7's oath structure. The weqatal (waw-consecutive + perfect) advances non-past discourse — future, modal, or consequent action. In oath/vow contexts it typically stands in the apodosis ("then..."). The oath formula in Dan 12:7 follows a different pattern (wayyiqtol narrative), but the commitment structure parallels oath-apodosis grammar.

    External Corpus Verification Notes¶

    Verification 1: Is the sealed-to-unsealed chain exclusively Danielic?¶

    The vocabulary arc (chatham/satham → sphragizo/katasphragizo → eneogmenon → me sphragises) traces exclusively through Daniel's sealed time prophecy into Revelation's opening and prohibition of re-sealing. ISA 8:16 seals teaching among disciples (preservation, not time-bound concealment). ISA 29:11-12 describes inability to read (spiritual blindness, not divine sealing with an unsealing mechanism). Neither provides a sealed-then-unsealed-at-the-time-of-the-end pattern. The structural uniqueness claim is supported by the biblical evidence.

    Verification 2: What does chronos ouketi estai mean?¶

    Three possibilities: (a) "no more prophetic time" = time prophecies cease, (b) "no more delay" = waiting period ends, (c) "time itself ceases" = cosmic end. The grammatical data supports (a) or (b): anarthrous chronos is qualitative (a category of time/delay), ouketi presupposes prior existence of that category, and the future estai makes it prospective. All four Revelation uses of chronos denote allotted spans/periods. The context (seventh trumpet, mystery finished) points to the cessation of prophetic waiting periods. Option (c) is unlikely since chronos in Revelation never denotes time-as-such but always a bounded duration.

    Verification 3: Does the sweet-then-bitter pattern create a distinct structural meaning?¶

    Ezekiel's scroll was sweet (3:3) but bitterness came AFTERWARD as emotional response (3:14, "I went in bitterness, in the heat of my spirit"). The bitterness is Ezekiel's personal reaction to the message he must deliver. In Rev 10:9-10, the angel PREDICTS the bitterness before consumption ("it shall make thy belly bitter"), and the experience confirms it ("my belly was bitter" — aorist passive, the scroll itself causes the bitterness). The modification makes bitterness intrinsic to the content rather than an emotional aftermath. This is a structural intensification, not merely a repetition of Ezekiel's pattern.

    Verification 4: Does the four-fold audience list match other Revelation lists?¶

    Rev 10:11's list (peoples, nations, tongues, kings) uniquely substitutes "kings" (basileusin) for "kindreds/tribes" (phylai) found in all other Revelation lists. This substitution may indicate the recommissioned prophecy specifically addresses political powers. The scope is universal in all seven lists.
