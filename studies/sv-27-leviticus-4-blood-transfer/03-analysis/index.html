    Verse Analysis¶

    Verse-by-Verse Analysis¶

    Leviticus 4:2¶

    Context: Opening clause for the entire chattat system. God speaks to Moses with instructions for Israel. Direct statement: "If a soul shall sin through ignorance (bishgagah) against any of the commandments of the LORD..." Original language: bishgagah (H7684, from shagah H7686) means inadvertent transgression, a mistake, an error. It defines the scope of the entire chapter. Every category that follows is governed by this term. Cross-references: Num 15:22-29 uses the same word for the same scope. Num 15:30-31 then explicitly excludes the opposite: "the soul that doeth ought presumptuously" (literally "with a high hand") has NO sacrifice. The chattat covers inadvertent sin only. Relationship to other evidence: This scope limitation is critical. The DOA in Lev 16:16 includes pesha (willful rebellion), which the daily chattat cannot process. The daily system was never designed to handle every category of sin through blood.

    Leviticus 4:5-7 (Path 1: Anointed Priest)¶

    Context: The blood procedure when the anointed priest sins. He takes the blood INTO the tabernacle. Direct statement: Three blood actions: (1) dip finger in blood, sprinkle seven times before the LORD before the veil (v.6); (2) put blood on horns of the incense altar (v.7a); (3) pour all blood at the base of the burnt offering altar (v.7b). Original language: Grammar reference (passages/lev-4-5-7.md) identifies a five-verb weqatal chain. The blood path uses nazah (Hiphil, causative sprinkling — fine, directed finger-application), nathan (put/place on altar horns), and shaphak (pour at base). nazah appears ONLY in Path 1. Its Hiphil stem marks the priest as deliberate instrumental agent. Cross-references: The same blood procedure appears for the congregation (Lev 4:16-18). Lev 16:14-15 uses the same nazah but goes deeper — past the veil, onto the mercy seat. Relationship to other evidence: This is one of only two offerer categories where blood physically enters the sanctuary building. For the other two categories (ruler, commoner), blood stays outside.

    Leviticus 4:16-18 (Path 1: Congregation)¶

    Context: The blood procedure when the whole congregation sins. Identical to the priest's procedure. Direct statement: Same three-step blood path: sprinkle seven times before the veil, put on incense altar horns, pour at burnt offering altar base. Original language: Same verbs as vv.5-7. The procedure is textually identical. Cross-references: Both priest and congregation use a bull (expensive). Both have their carcasses burned outside the camp (vv.12, 21). Both follow Path 1. Relationship to other evidence: The congregation, though they never physically enter the sanctuary, follows Path 1 because their sin is covenantal — it threatens the covenant relationship itself (see sanc-06 analysis of covenantal vs. individual proximity).

    Leviticus 4:20¶

    Context: The result formula for the congregation's sin offering. Direct statement: "the priest shall make an atonement for them, and it shall be forgiven them." Original language: kaphar (Piel) + nislach (Niphal of salach, divine passive). Same formula as every other category. Cross-references: Identical to Lev 4:26, 4:31, 4:35. The formula does not vary by offerer category. Relationship to other evidence: The graduated blood procedure produces identical forgiveness. The variation is about where contamination reaches in the sanctuary, not about whether or how much the offerer is forgiven.

    Leviticus 4:25 (Path 2: Ruler)¶

    Context: The blood procedure when a ruler sins. Blood stays in the courtyard. Direct statement: Two blood actions only: (1) put blood on horns of the burnt offering altar; (2) pour blood at its base. No sprinkling before the veil. No entry into the building. Original language: Only nathan and shaphak. No nazah. The Hiphil causative sprinkling that characterizes Path 1 is absent. The blood stays at the courtyard altar. Cross-references: Same procedure for the common person (Lev 4:30, 34). Relationship to other evidence: This is the textual fact the study exists to address. For categories 3 and 4, blood never enters the sanctuary building. The objection that "blood never enters for individuals" is correct. But the question is whether blood-into-the-building is the only way sin reaches the sanctuary. It is not.

    Leviticus 4:26¶

    Context: Result formula for the ruler. Direct statement: "the priest shall make an atonement for him as concerning his sin, and it shall be forgiven him." Original language: Same kaphar + nislach. Identical to v.20. Relationship to other evidence: Proves the graduated procedure is about contamination depth, not forgiveness degree.

    Leviticus 4:30-31, 34-35 (Path 2: Common Person)¶

    Context: Blood procedure and result formula for the common person. Direct statement: Same as ruler: blood on burnt offering altar horns, poured at base. Same result: kaphar + nislach. Original language: Same verbs. No nazah. Blood stays in courtyard. Relationship to other evidence: Confirms the pattern. For the most common category of sin offering — individual sin by a commoner — blood never enters the building.

    Leviticus 6:26¶

    Context: The law of the sin offering — who eats it and where. Direct statement: "The priest that offereth it for sin shall eat it: in the holy place shall it be eaten, in the court of the tabernacle of the congregation." Original language: akal (H398, Qal) — the priest SHALL eat. This is a command, not an option. Cross-references: Lev 6:29 reinforces: "All the males among the priests shall eat thereof: it is most holy." Relationship to other evidence: Establishes the positive rule that Path 2 sin offerings must be eaten by the priest. The next verse (6:30) provides the negative complement.

    Leviticus 6:30¶

    Context: The mutual exclusion rule — the pivot of the two-path system. Direct statement: "No sin offering, whereof any of the blood is brought into the tabernacle of the congregation to reconcile withal in the holy place, shall be eaten: it shall be burnt in the fire." Original language: Blood brought inside (bo, Hophal — "is caused to come in") → flesh burned. Blood NOT brought inside → priest eats (v.26). Cross-references: Moses confirms the same rule from the opposite direction in Lev 10:18: "the blood of it was not brought in within the holy place: ye should indeed have eaten it." Relationship to other evidence: This verse creates a perfect binary. Every chattat follows one of two mutually exclusive paths. Neither path is optional. The system is designed so that sin ALWAYS reaches the sanctuary — either through blood or through the priest.

    Leviticus 10:16-17¶

    Context: After Nadab and Abihu's death. Moses discovers the sin-offering goat was burned when it should have been eaten. Direct statement: Moses' angry question: "Wherefore have ye not eaten the sin offering in the holy place, seeing it is most holy, and God hath given it you to bear the iniquity of the congregation, to make atonement for them before the LORD?" Original language: Grammar reference (passages/lev-10-16-20.md) identifies the dual infinitive chain: laset (nasa, Qal InfCon — "to bear") + lekapper (kaphar, Piel InfCon — "to make atonement"). Both are governed by nathan ("God gave it to you FOR these purposes"). Eating = bearing iniquity = making atonement. These are not separate functions but one integrated action. Cross-references: Moses' anger (emphatic cognate infinitive darosh darash — "he diligently sought") proves this is not optional or symbolic. The eating was essential. Relationship to other evidence: This verse is the strongest text for the second transfer mechanism. If priestly eating is "bearing the iniquity of the congregation," then sin reaches the sanctuary through the priest's person when blood does not enter the building.

    Leviticus 10:18¶

    Context: Moses' explanation of WHY eating was required. Direct statement: "Behold, the blood of it was not brought in within the holy place: ye should indeed have eaten it in the holy place, as I commanded." Original language: Hophal of bo (huva — "was brought in," causative passive). The blood was NOT brought inside. BECAUSE it was not brought inside, eating was mandatory. Cross-references: This is the explicit statement of the Lev 6:30 rule applied to a specific case. Blood outside → eat. Blood inside → burn. Relationship to other evidence: Moses' reasoning establishes that eating is the ALTERNATIVE to blood-entry, not an additional optional step. The two mechanisms are complementary halves of one system.

    Leviticus 15:31¶

    Context: Summary verse closing the chapter on bodily discharges. Follows detailed purity laws for men and women. Direct statement: "Thus shall ye separate the children of Israel from their uncleanness; that they die not in their uncleanness, when they defile my tabernacle that is among them." Original language: The key phrase is betamme'am (Piel InfCon of tame + 3mp suffix) — "when they CAUSE TO BE UNCLEAN." The Piel stem is causative. Israel's uncleanness actively causes God's tabernacle (mishkani — "MY dwelling-place," first-person possessive) to be defiled. The tabernacle is described as asher betokham — "which is in their midst." The defilement happens because the tabernacle DWELLS AMONG a contaminated people. Cross-references: Num 19:13 and 19:20 confirm the same principle with a specific case (corpse contamination). Both use the Piel of tame for the person defiling the tabernacle/sanctuary at a distance. Relationship to other evidence: This is the third transfer mechanism — ambient defilement. No blood is involved. No priestly eating. The community's unresolved impurity reaches the sanctuary by proximity. This fills the tumot category in Lev 16:16 that neither blood nor eating can fully account for.

    Numbers 19:13¶

    Context: Laws about corpse contamination and the water of purification. Direct statement: "Whosoever toucheth the dead body of any man that is dead, and purifieth not himself, defileth the tabernacle of the LORD; and that soul shall be cut off from Israel." Original language: "defileth" is the Piel of tame — same causative stem as Lev 15:31. The person does not physically enter the tabernacle. They defile it from wherever they are in the community. The penalty is karet (covenantal cutting off). Cross-references: Num 19:20 repeats: "he hath defiled the sanctuary of the LORD." Uses miqdash (sanctuary) instead of mishkan (tabernacle) — both terms, same principle. Relationship to other evidence: Proves that sanctuary defilement occurs WITHOUT any sacrificial channel. A person who fails to purify never brings a sacrifice — yet they defile the sanctuary. This contamination has no daily remedy. Only the DOA addresses it.

    Numbers 19:20¶

    Context: Restatement of the penalty for failure to purify after corpse contact. Direct statement: "the man that shall be unclean, and shall not purify himself, that soul shall be cut off from among the congregation, because he hath defiled the sanctuary of the LORD." Original language: Piel of tame again. "Sanctuary of the LORD" (miqdash YHWH) — the sacred space is polluted by someone who never approached it. Relationship to other evidence: Doubles the textual witness for ambient defilement alongside Num 19:13 and Lev 15:31.

    Numbers 15:30-31¶

    Context: Laws about inadvertent vs. deliberate sin. Follows the chattat provisions for inadvertent community sin (Num 15:22-29). Direct statement: "the soul that doeth ought presumptuously... the same reproacheth the LORD; and that soul shall be cut off." v.31: "Because he hath despised the word of the LORD... that soul shall utterly be cut off; his iniquity shall be upon him." Original language: "presumptuously" = beyad ramah ("with a high hand"). This is deliberate, defiant sin. No sacrifice is prescribed. The penalty is karet. Cross-references: Contrast with every opening of Lev 4 (bishgagah, "through ignorance"). The daily chattat addresses only inadvertent sin. Relationship to other evidence: The daily system has a built-in scope limitation. Pesha (willful rebellion) is excluded from the daily chattat. Yet pesha appears in the DOA formula (Lev 16:16). The DOA covers what the daily cannot.

    Leviticus 16:16¶

    Context: The purpose statement of the DOA blood ministry. Direct statement: "And he shall make an atonement for the holy place, because of the uncleanness of the children of Israel, and because of their transgressions in all their sins: and so shall he do for the tabernacle of the congregation, that remaineth among them in the midst of their uncleanness." Original language: Three categories with three prepositions: mittumot (from the uncleannesses — causal min + tumah fp), umipish'eihem (from their rebellions — min + pesha mp), lekhol-chatto'tam (with respect to all their sins — lamed specification + chattat). Grammar reference (passages/lev-16-14-16.md) confirms the structure. The closing clause uses the participle hashshokhen (shakhan — "the one dwelling") — God dwells in their midst surrounded by their tumot. Cross-references: Lev 16:21 shifts the first term: avonot replaces tumot. Same scope, different angle (guilt vs. contamination). Relationship to other evidence: The tumot category is the key. It includes ambient defilement (Lev 15:31, Num 19:13,20), bodily emissions (Lev 15), corpse contamination (Num 19), and all unresolved impurity. Much of this NEVER passed through a blood channel. The DOA addresses it anyway. This proves the system does NOT require blood-into-the-building for every sin to reach the sanctuary.

    Leviticus 16:19¶

    Context: The altar cleansing during the DOA. Direct statement: "he shall sprinkle of the blood upon it with his finger seven times, and cleanse it, and hallow it from the uncleanness of the children of Israel." Original language: Two verbs: taher (Piel — "cleanse," addressing the tame/tahor axis) and qadash (Piel — "hallow/sanctify," addressing the qodesh/chol axis). Both axes of the Lev 10:10 framework are addressed. Relationship to other evidence: The altar has accumulated contamination during the year — from both blood deposits and ambient defilement. The DOA restores it on both axes.

    Leviticus 16:30¶

    Context: Summary statement of the DOA's effect. Direct statement: "For on that day shall the priest make an atonement for you, to cleanse you, that ye may be clean from all your sins before the LORD." Original language: Three-verb chain (grammar reference passages/lev-16-30.md): kaphar (Piel.Impf — the act) → taher (Piel.InfCon — the process) → taher (Qal.Impf — the achieved state). The stem shift within taher encodes God acting (Piel) producing human state change (Qal). Relationship to other evidence: salach does not appear here. The annual formula is kaphar+taher, not kaphar+salach. Different result from the daily. The daily forgives the sinner. The annual cleanses the sanctuary and the people.

    Leviticus 17:11¶

    Context: The theological rationale for blood sacrifice. Direct statement: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood: and I have given it to you upon the altar to make an atonement for your souls: for it is the blood that maketh an atonement for the soul." Original language: dam (blood) carries nephesh (life). kaphar is the function. Relationship to other evidence: Blood is central to atonement. But this verse does not say blood is the ONLY carrier of sin to the sanctuary. The system uses multiple channels.

    Hebrews 9:22-23¶

    Context: The author of Hebrews summarizing the Levitical blood principle and applying it to heaven. Direct statement: v.22: "almost all things are by the law purged with blood; and without shedding of blood is no remission." v.23: "It was therefore necessary that the patterns of things in the heavens should be purified with these; but the heavenly things themselves with better sacrifices than these." Original language: katharizesthai (G2511, present passive infinitive of katharizo) — the specific LXX word for taher. The author uses DOA vocabulary for heavenly purification. Cross-references: Heb 9:12 (Christ entered once, by his own blood). Heb 9:24 (into heaven itself, in God's presence for us). Relationship to other evidence: Hebrews 9:22's "almost" acknowledges exceptions (the flour offering of Lev 5:11-13). Blood is the primary medium but not the only one. The "heavenly things" needing purification parallels the earthly sanctuary absorbing contamination.

    Hebrews 13:11-12¶

    Context: Exhortation to go "outside the camp" with Christ. Direct statement: v.11: "the bodies of those beasts, whose blood is brought into the sanctuary by the high priest for sin, are burned without the camp." v.12: "Wherefore Jesus also, that he might sanctify the people with his own blood, suffered without the gate." Original language: The author identifies Christ's sacrifice with the Path 1 chattat specifically — the highest-grade offering whose blood enters the sanctuary and whose body is burned outside the camp. Relationship to other evidence: Hebrews sees Christ fulfilling the Path 1 (deepest penetration) chattat, not the Path 2 (courtyard only) version. This is typologically appropriate — Christ's blood enters the heavenly sanctuary directly (Heb 9:12).

    Patterns Identified¶

    • Pattern 1: The two-path system is exhaustive and mutually exclusive. Every chattat follows one of two paths (blood inside + flesh burned, OR blood outside + priest eats). Lev 6:30 and Lev 10:18 together prove this. The system leaves no sin-offering unprocessed. Supported by: Lev 4:6-7, 4:16-18, 4:25, 4:30, 6:26, 6:30, 10:17-18.

    • Pattern 2: The result formula is invariant across all categories. kaphar + nislach appears identically for priest (4:20), congregation (4:20), ruler (4:26), commoner (4:31, 4:35). The procedure varies; the forgiveness does not. Supported by: Lev 4:20, 4:26, 4:31, 4:35, 5:10, 5:13.

    • Pattern 3: The sanctuary absorbs defilement through multiple channels, not blood alone. Blood (Lev 4:6-7), priestly eating (Lev 10:17), and ambient defilement (Lev 15:31, Num 19:13, 19:20) all contribute. The DOA addresses all three through its tumot category (Lev 16:16). Supported by: Lev 4:6, 10:17, 15:31, 16:16, Num 19:13, 19:20.

    • Pattern 4: The daily chattat has a scope limitation that the DOA overcomes. The daily covers only shegagah (inadvertent sin, Lev 4:2). Pesha (deliberate rebellion) has no daily sacrifice (Num 15:30-31). The DOA covers pesha (Lev 16:16). The annual service resolves what the daily cannot. Supported by: Lev 4:2, Num 15:30-31, Lev 16:16, 16:21.

    Word Study Integration¶

    The Hebrew verb stems reveal distinctions invisible in English. The Hiphil of nazah (causative sprinkling) appears only in Path 1, marking deliberate priestly agency directing blood toward the veil. The Piel of tame (causative defilement) in Lev 15:31 and Num 19:13 marks the community actively causing sanctuary contamination at a distance. The Niphal of salach (divine passive forgiveness) in the result formula marks God as the unnamed agent. The Qal Infinitive Construct of nasa in Lev 10:17 marks priestly sin-bearing as the PURPOSE of eating, not a side effect. These stem distributions are from the canonical word studies library.

    Cross-Testament Connections¶

    Hebrews reads the chattat system typologically. Christ's sacrifice corresponds to the Path 1 (highest-grade) chattat: blood enters the sanctuary (Heb 9:12), body destroyed outside the camp (Heb 13:11-12). The "heavenly things" needing purification (Heb 9:23) extends the Levitical principle that the sanctuary absorbs contamination. The vocabulary bridge (taher → katharizo) connects the DOA cleansing to the heavenly purification.

    Difficult or Complicating Passages¶

    Leviticus 4 itself never uses tame or tumah¶

    The defilement-transfer function of the chattat blood procedure is inferred from surrounding texts (Lev 15:31, 16:16, Num 19:13,20), not stated within Leviticus 4 itself. This means the blood-defilement connection is a systematic inference, not a direct statement in the chattat legislation.

    Aaron's exception (Lev 10:19-20)¶

    Aaron refused to eat and Moses accepted his reasoning. If eating is essential for sin transfer, did the system fail that day? The text records the exception but does not say the principle is wrong — Moses' prior anger (v.16) proves the principle stands. The exception is for extraordinary grief, not a new rule.

    EGW's two-mechanism model vs. the Bible's three¶

    EGW (GC 418.1) identifies blood and priestly eating as the two transfer mechanisms. She does not mention ambient defilement. The Bible adds a third (Lev 15:31, Num 19:13,20). This is a supplement, not a contradiction. EGW describes the sacrificial channels. The text also describes a non-sacrificial channel. Both are accurate within their scope.

    The flour chattat (Lev 5:11-13)¶

    A chattat with no animal and no blood, for the poorest offerers. The kaphar+nislach formula still applies. This complicates the blood-only transfer model. Heb 9:22's "almost all things" may acknowledge such exceptions.

    Preliminary Synthesis¶

    The weight of evidence points clearly: Leviticus 4 does NOT teach that individual sin offerings transferred sin into the sanctuary via blood for every offerer category. The text distinguishes four categories, only two of which send blood inside the building. For the other two (ruler, commoner — the most common cases), blood stays at the courtyard altar. Sin reaches the sanctuary through the priest's eating (Lev 10:17) and through ambient defilement (Lev 15:31). The DOA resolves all accumulated defilement, including the tumot category that entered without blood (Lev 16:16). The system uses multiple channels, not one.
