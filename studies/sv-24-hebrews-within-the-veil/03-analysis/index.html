    Verse Analysis¶

    Verse-by-Verse Analysis¶

    Exodus 26:31-33 — The Veil's Construction and Purpose¶

    Context: God instructs Moses on building the tabernacle. These verses define what the veil (poreketh) is and what it does. Direct statement: "Thou shalt make a vail... with cherubims shall it be made" (v.31). "The vail shall divide unto you between the holy place and the most holy" (v.33). Original language: The verb hivdilah (Hiphil of badal, "to separate") is the same verb used in Genesis 1 for God's creation-level acts of separation — light from darkness, waters from waters. The veil performs a boundary act of the same kind. Poreketh (H6532) is an active participle meaning "separatrix" — the thing that separates (H6532 word study, canonical). The cherubim woven into it echo the cherubim guarding Eden (Gen 3:24). Cross-references: Heb 9:3 describes this same curtain as "the second katapetasma." 2 Chr 3:14 confirms the temple's inner veil is also called poreketh. Relationship to other evidence: This passage establishes the foundational vocabulary. Poreketh always and only means the inner dividing curtain.

    Exodus 26:36-37 — The Entrance Screen¶

    Context: Immediately after the veil instructions, Moses receives instructions for a different curtain. Direct statement: "Thou shalt make an hanging for the door of the tent." Original language: The word here is macak (H4539), not poreketh. Macak means a screen or covering, from the root cakak ("to cover"). All 25 OT occurrences of macak designate entrance screens or protective coverings — never the inner dividing veil (H4539 word study, canonical). Relationship to other evidence: This distinction is critical. Hebrew preserves a clear two-word system: poreketh = inner veil; macak = outer screen. But the LXX translates both as katapetasma (G2665). This collapse of two Hebrew words into one Greek word is the root of the "which veil?" question in Hebrews.

    Leviticus 16:2 — "Within the Veil" in the OT¶

    Context: God warns Aaron not to enter the Most Holy Place whenever he chooses. Direct statement: "Come not at all times into the holy place within the vail before the mercy seat... that he die not." Original language: The phrase is mibbeyth lapparoketh — "from the interior of the curtain." This is the exact Hebrew construction behind Heb 6:19's "within the veil." The spatial referent is completely clear: behind the poreketh, before the mercy seat, in the Most Holy Place. Cross-references: The same phrase recurs in Lev 16:12 and 16:15. Every occurrence means the MHP. Relationship to other evidence: This is the OT linguistic base for Heb 6:19. When a Hebrews reader familiar with the Levitical background hears "within the veil," the natural referent is the space behind the poreketh — the MHP.

    Leviticus 16:12 — Incense Within the Veil¶

    Context: The high priest brings incense into the Most Holy Place on the Day of Atonement. Direct statement: "Bring it within the vail." Original language: Again mibbeyth lapparoketh. The action is entry into the MHP with incense. Relationship to other evidence: Confirms the consistent OT pattern. "Within the veil" = inside the MHP.

    Leviticus 16:15 — Blood Within the Veil¶

    Context: The high priest brings the sin offering blood into the Most Holy Place. Direct statement: "Bring his blood within the vail, and do with that blood as he did with the blood of the bullock, and sprinkle it upon the mercy seat." Original language: mibbeyth lapparoketh again. Blood is brought behind the inner curtain and applied to the mercy seat (kapporeth, H3727). Cross-references: Heb 9:7 — "Into the second went the high priest alone once every year, not without blood." Heb 9:12 — Christ entered "by his own blood." Relationship to other evidence: This is the DOA entry pattern that Hebrews draws upon. Christ's entry "within the veil" parallels the high priest's blood-entry on the DOA.

    Leviticus 16:17 — No Man in the Tabernacle¶

    Context: Access restriction during the high priest's MHP ministry. Direct statement: "There shall be no man in the tabernacle of the congregation when he goeth in to make an atonement in the holy place, until he come out." Original language: The high priest enters monos — alone. No one follows. Cross-references: Heb 9:7 echoes this: "the high priest alone (monos)." But Heb 6:20 calls Jesus prodromos — "forerunner." A forerunner implies others follow. This is a deliberate reversal of the Lev 16 restriction. Relationship to other evidence: The prodromos/monos contrast signals that Christ's entry is not simply repeating the DOA pattern but transforming it.

    Numbers 18:7 — Priestly Service Within the Veil¶

    Context: God defines the scope of priestly authority. Direct statement: "Thou and thy sons with thee shall keep your priest's office for every thing of the altar, and within the veil; and ye shall serve." Original language: "Within the veil" (mibbeyth lapparoketh) again designates the MHP as the scope of high priestly service. Relationship to other evidence: This verse connects priestly service with the space behind the veil, reinforcing the sanctuary-service reading of Heb 6:19-20.

    Genesis 14:18-20 — Melchizedek as King-Priest¶

    Context: Abraham returns from battle. Melchizedek meets him. Direct statement: "Melchizedek king of Salem brought forth bread and wine: and he was the priest of the most high God" (v.18). Original language: Melchizedek holds two titles simultaneously: melek (king) and kohen (priest). The name itself means "king of righteousness" (malki-tsedeq). He is also "king of Salem" (melek-shalem) = king of peace. Cross-references: Heb 7:1-2 interprets both names: "King of righteousness... King of peace." Psa 110:4 connects the Melchizedek priesthood to the Davidic enthronement. Relationship to other evidence: Melchizedek is the biblical precedent for combining royal authority (sitting) with priestly function (serving). This dual identity is the key to resolving the sitting-while-serving tension in Hebrews.

    Psalm 110:1 — The Enthronement Command¶

    Context: A Davidic psalm. YHWH speaks to David's "Lord." Direct statement: "The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at my right hand, until I make thine enemies thy footstool." Original language: shev (yashav, Qal Imperative 2ms) — a direct command: "Sit!" This is an enthronement order. The verb means to sit, dwell, remain on a throne. "Until I make thine enemies thy footstool" (ad-ashiyth) sets a temporal horizon — the sitting continues during the subduing of enemies. Cross-references: Quoted or alluded to in Heb 1:3,13; 8:1; 10:12-13; 12:2. The most-quoted OT verse in the NT (over 20 times). Relationship to other evidence: This is the source text for every Hebrews "sat down" statement. Christ's sitting is obedience to a divine command, not a cessation of activity.

    Psalm 110:4 — Eternal Priesthood by Oath¶

    Context: Same psalm, same oracle, same individual addressed. Direct statement: "The LORD hath sworn, and will not repent, Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedek." Original language: nishba (Niphal of shava, "he swore") + lo yinnachem (Niphal of nacham, "will not repent") — an irrevocable divine oath. kohen le-olam — "priest forever." The priesthood has no endpoint. Cross-references: Quoted in Heb 5:6,10; 6:20; 7:17,21. The author of Hebrews treats v.1 (sitting) and v.4 (priesting) as describing the same person at the same time — they are verses in the same oracle. Relationship to other evidence: This is the decisive text. The same psalm that commands sitting also declares eternal priesthood. The two are not sequential phases — they are concurrent realities within one divine oracle.

    Psalm 110:2-3 — Rule Amid Enemies¶

    Context: Between the enthronement (v.1) and the priesthood (v.4). Direct statement: "The LORD shall send the rod of thy strength out of Zion: rule thou in the midst of thine enemies. Thy people shall be willing in the day of thy power." Original language: "Rule" (redeh, Qal Imperative) = a command to exercise dominion while enemies still exist. Relationship to other evidence: This describes active rule during the "until" period of v.1. The enthroned one is not idle — he rules from his seat.

    Psalm 110:5-7 — Judgment and Victory¶

    Context: The climax of the psalm. Direct statement: "The Lord at thy right hand shall strike through kings in the day of his wrath. He shall judge among the heathen." Relationship to other evidence: The psalm moves from enthronement (v.1) through priesthood (v.4) to judgment (vv.5-6). This mirrors the Hebrews progression: Christ sat down (enthronement) as a priest forever (ongoing ministry) expecting until enemies are subdued (judgment/consummation).

    Zechariah 6:12-13 — The Branch as King-Priest¶

    Context: A prophetic oracle about a future figure called "the Branch" (tsemach). Joshua the high priest receives a crown in a symbolic act. Direct statement: "He shall build the temple of the LORD; and he shall bear the glory, and shall sit and rule upon his throne; and he shall be a priest upon his throne: and the counsel of peace shall be between them both" (v.13). Original language: yashav (Qal Perfect 3ms) = "he will sit" + mashal (Qal Perfect 3ms) = "he will rule" + hayah kohen (= "he will be a priest"). The prophetic perfect depicts future events as already accomplished. The word al-kis'o ("upon his throne") appears twice — once for the sitting/ruling and once for the priesting. Same throne, same person, both functions. Cross-references: The counsel of peace "between them both" (bein shneyhem) points to two offices unified in one person. The temple-building connects to Heb 3:3 (Jesus as builder) and Heb 8:2 (the true tabernacle "which the Lord pitched"). Relationship to other evidence: This is the clearest OT text combining sitting, ruling, and priesting simultaneously in one individual on one throne. It directly answers the question: Can someone sit on a throne AND serve as priest? Zechariah says yes.

    Hebrews 1:3 — The Opening Enthronement¶

    Context: The grand opening of Hebrews, declaring the Son's superiority. Direct statement: "When he had by himself purged our sins, sat down on the right hand of the Majesty on high." Original language: poiesamenos katharismon (aorist middle participle, "having made purification") → ekathisen (aorist active, "sat down"). The sequence: completed sacrifice → enthronement. The aorist participle is antecedent in time to the main verb. Cross-references: Echoes Psa 110:1. The "right hand" language comes directly from the psalm. Relationship to other evidence: This is the first "sat down" statement in Hebrews. It links the completed sacrifice (purging sins) to the enthronement. But it says nothing about cessation of ministry — only that the sacrifice is finished.

    Hebrews 1:13 — Psalm 110:1 Quoted Directly¶

    Context: Conclusion of the angels-comparison section. Direct statement: "But to which of the angels said he at any time, Sit on my right hand, until I make thine enemies thy footstool?" Original language: kathou (kathemai, present imperative) — "be seated" / "keep sitting." The Hebrews author quotes Psa 110:1 using kathemai (the state of being seated) rather than kathizo (the act of sitting down). This emphasizes the ongoing posture, not just the initial act. Relationship to other evidence: The "until" clause carries directly into Heb 10:13: "from henceforth expecting till his enemies be made his footstool." The sitting is DURING the subduing, not AFTER it.

    Hebrews 4:14-16 — Access Through the Great High Priest¶

    Context: Transitional passage introducing Christ's high priesthood. Direct statement: "We have a great high priest, that is passed into the heavens, Jesus the Son of God" (v.14). "Let us therefore come boldly unto the throne of grace" (v.16). Original language: dielelythota (perfect active participle of dierchomai, "having passed through") — Christ has passed through the heavens. The perfect tense means his heavenly presence is an ongoing state. The destination is a "throne of grace" (thronos tes charitos) — a throne that dispenses mercy and help. This is a throne of active ministry, not a retired seat. Cross-references: Heb 6:19-20 (access within the veil); Heb 10:19-22 (boldness to enter). Relationship to other evidence: This passage combines heavenly location with ongoing ministry ("mercy and grace to help in time of need") and active access for believers ("come boldly"). The throne is not closed — it is open and dispensing.

    Hebrews 6:13-17 — God's Oath to Abraham¶

    Context: The author argues from God's immutable promises to establish the basis for hope. Direct statement: "When God made promise to Abraham, because he could swear by no greater, he sware by himself" (v.13). God confirmed "the immutability of his counsel" by an oath (v.17). Relationship to other evidence: The oath language directly connects to Psa 110:4 ("The LORD hath sworn and will not repent") and Heb 7:21 (Christ's priesthood confirmed by oath). The "two immutable things" (promise + oath) ground the hope that enters "within the veil" in vv.18-19.

    Hebrews 6:18-19 — Hope as Anchor Within the Veil¶

    Context: The climax of the hope-and-promise argument. The transition point into sanctuary theology. Direct statement: "We might have a strong consolation, who have fled for refuge to lay hold upon the hope set before us: Which hope we have as an anchor of the soul, both sure and stedfast, and which entereth into that within the veil." Original language: eiserchmenen (present middle/passive participle of eiserchomai, "entering") — the hope is personified as actively entering the interior space. to esoteron tou katapetasmatos — "the interior of the veil." esoteron (G2082, comparative: "more inner") is substantivized by the article. The genitive tou katapetasmatos specifies what the inner space is relative to (G2082 word study, canonical; grammar reference on Greek comparative adjective). Critical question — which veil? The comparative form of esoteros does not automatically mean "innermost of two compartments." It designates the interior relative to the veil named. Since katapetasma in Greek covers both the inner and outer curtain (G2665 word study: LXX maps both H6532 poreketh and H4539 macak to katapetasma), the grammar alone does not resolve the question. But the OT background tips the scales: every Levitical "within the veil" (mibbeyth lapparoketh) means inside the MHP (Lev 16:2,12,15). The Hebrews author is using sanctuary language his readers would recognize. Cross-references: Heb 9:3 ("the second katapetasma") shows the author knew the ambiguity. He did not add a qualifier here, which may mean he trusted the phrase's OT resonance to convey MHP. Relationship to other evidence: The anchor metaphor is unique (G45 word study). Hope lodges in the heavenly sanctuary as an anchor lodges in the seabed. The believers' security is anchored in the place where Christ ministers.

    Hebrews 6:20 — The Forerunner Entered¶

    Context: Immediately follows the anchor/veil image. Direct statement: "Whither the forerunner is for us entered, even Jesus, made an high priest for ever after the order of Melchisedec." Original language: prodromos (G4274, hapax — only NT use) = "one who runs ahead." hyper hemon = "on our behalf." eiselthen (aorist of eiserchomai) = "entered" (completed act). genomenos archiereus (aorist participle + noun) = "having become a high priest." eis ton aiona = "forever/for the age." kata ten taxin Melchisedek = "according to the order of Melchizedek." Theological import: A forerunner implies others will follow. This is fundamentally different from the DOA model where the high priest enters alone (Lev 16:17; Heb 9:7 monos). Prodromos transforms the access model: not solitary entry with annual repetition but inaugural entry with permanent access for all who follow (G4274 word study, canonical). Cross-references: Heb 10:19-20 fulfills the prodromos promise: believers now have "boldness to enter." Heb 2:10 calls Jesus archegos ("captain/author") — another "pioneer" word. Relationship to other evidence: The Melchizedek designation at the end of this verse transitions into the Melchizedek exposition of chapter 7. The forerunner entered "within the veil" as a king-priest, not merely as a Levitical high priest.

    Hebrews 7:1-3 — Melchizedek Introduced¶

    Context: The Melchizedek exposition begins. The author interprets Genesis 14. Direct statement: "This Melchisedec, king of Salem, priest of the most high God... first being by interpretation King of righteousness, and after that also King of Salem, which is, King of peace; without father, without mother, without descent, having neither beginning of days, nor end of life; but made like unto the Son of God; abideth a priest continually." Original language: The author draws theology from both the name (Melchi-zedek = king of righteousness) and the title (king of Salem = king of peace). The silence of Genesis about Melchizedek's genealogy is read as typologically significant: no recorded beginning or end makes him a type of Christ's eternal priesthood. Relationship to other evidence: The dual identity — king AND priest — is the foundation for understanding how Christ can sit (royal) and serve (priestly) at the same time.

    Hebrews 7:11 — Why Another Priesthood?¶

    Context: The argument for a change of priesthood. Direct statement: "If therefore perfection were by the Levitical priesthood... what further need was there that another priest should rise after the order of Melchisedec, and not be called after the order of Aaron?" Relationship to other evidence: The Levitical priesthood's inability to bring "perfection" (teleiosis) is part of the first-covenant inadequacy that chapters 8-9 develop. The Melchizedek priesthood replaces it because it can do what the Levitical could not.

    Hebrews 7:17 — Psalm 110:4 Quoted¶

    Context: Within the argument for Christ's superior priesthood. Direct statement: "Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchisedec." Relationship to other evidence: This is the third quotation of Psa 110:4 (after 5:6 and 6:20). Repetition signals importance. The "forever" directly contradicts the idea that Christ's priesthood has phases that end.

    Hebrews 7:21 — The Oath That Secures Everything¶

    Context: Comparison between the Levitical priests (no oath) and Christ (with oath). Direct statement: "The Lord sware and will not repent, Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchisedec." Original language: Fourth use of Psa 110:4 in Hebrews. The emphasis falls on the oath — what God has sworn, he cannot revoke. Relationship to other evidence: This oath connects back to 6:13-18 (God's oath to Abraham) and forward to 6:19-20 (the hope secured by the oath enters within the veil).

    Hebrews 7:25 — Intercession as Ongoing Ministry¶

    Context: The climax of the Melchizedek priesthood argument. Direct statement: "Wherefore he is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him, seeing he ever liveth to make intercession for them." Original language: entynchanein (present active infinitive, "to make intercession") — ongoing, continuous activity (G1793 word study, canonical). panteles ("to the uttermost" / "completely") — salvation is comprehensive. pantote zon ("ever living") — his life is perpetual. Cross-references: Rom 8:34 — "at the right hand of God, who also maketh intercession for us." The intercession happens from the seated position. Relationship to other evidence: This verse proves Christ is not idle after sitting. He "ever liveth" to intercede. The sitting (Heb 1:3; 8:1; 10:12) and the interceding (7:25) are concurrent, not sequential.

    Hebrews 8:1-2 — The Thesis Statement¶

    Context: The author's own summary of his argument: "This is the sum" (kephalaion). Direct statement: "We have such an high priest, who is set on the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in the heavens; a minister of the sanctuary, and of the true tabernacle, which the Lord pitched, and not man." Original language: ekathisen (aorist, "sat down") in v.1 + leitourgos (G3011, "minister/public servant") in v.2. The author deliberately places these in the same sentence. The one who SAT DOWN IS a leitourgos. Sitting does not exclude ministry — the author combines them as his thesis statement. Cross-references: ton hagion ("of the sanctuary") uses the ambiguous neuter plural (G39 word study). In this context, it most likely means the heavenly sanctuary as a whole — "the true tabernacle which the Lord pitched." Relationship to other evidence: This is the single most important verse for the sitting-while-serving question. The author of Hebrews himself states that the enthroned Christ IS a minister. If sitting meant cessation of priestly work, this sentence would be self-contradictory.

    Hebrews 8:3-5 — Necessity of Offering¶

    Context: Directly follows the thesis statement. Direct statement: "Every high priest is ordained to offer gifts and sacrifices: wherefore it is of necessity that this man have somewhat also to offer" (v.3). "Who serve unto the example and shadow of heavenly things" (v.5). Relationship to other evidence: v.3 says Christ must have "somewhat to offer" — the heavenly priestly role is not empty. v.5 establishes the type-antitype relationship: earthly = shadow; heavenly = reality.

    Hebrews 8:6-13 — Better Covenant, Better Ministry¶

    Context: Transition from sanctuary to covenant. Quotes Jeremiah 31:31-34. Direct statement: "He hath obtained a more excellent ministry, by how much also he is the mediator of a better covenant" (v.6). "In that he saith, A new covenant, he hath made the first old" (v.13). Relationship to other evidence: The covenant framework overlaps with the sanctuary framework. The "first" covenant (v.7) and the "first tabernacle" (9:2,6,8) use the same adjective (protos), which creates the interpretive ambiguity in 9:8.

    Hebrews 9:1-2 — The First Tabernacle Described¶

    Context: The author describes the earthly sanctuary's structure. Direct statement: "The first covenant had also ordinances of divine service, and a worldly sanctuary. For there was a tabernacle made; the first, wherein was the candlestick, and the table, and the shewbread; which is called the sanctuary (hagia)." Original language: he prote skene ("the first tabernacle") — the first compartment containing lampstand, table, and showbread. This is labeled hagia (neuter plural) — "the sanctuary" or "the holy place." Relationship to other evidence: The numbering system begins: first tabernacle = first compartment = Holy Place. The second follows in v.3.

    Hebrews 9:3 — The Second Veil and the Holiest¶

    Context: Continuing the sanctuary description. Direct statement: "And after the second veil, the tabernacle which is called the Holiest of all." Original language: meta to deuteron katapetasma ("after the second veil") — the author explicitly numbers the veils: there is a first and a second katapetasma. This proves the author knew katapetasma could refer to either curtain. Hagia hagion ("Holy of Holies") — the superlative Hebrew construction in Greek. Cross-references: Exo 26:31-37 shows two curtains: poreketh (v.31, inner) and macak (v.36, outer). The LXX renders both as katapetasma, but the Hebrews author adds the ordinal "second" to distinguish them. Relationship to other evidence: This is the key to interpreting Heb 6:19. The author knows there are two katapetasmata. When he writes "within the veil" in 6:19 without a number, is the default the second (inner) veil based on the OT background? Or is it deliberately unspecified?

    Hebrews 9:4-5 — Contents of the Most Holy Place¶

    Context: Describing what is behind the second veil. Direct statement: "The golden censer, and the ark of the covenant... wherein was the golden pot that had manna, and Aaron's rod that budded, and the tables of the covenant; and over it the cherubims of glory shadowing the mercyseat (hilasterion)." Original language: hilasterion (G2435) — the mercy seat / place of propitiation. This is the throne of grace (cf. Heb 4:16) — God's presence localized between the cherubim. Relationship to other evidence: The mercy seat is the destination of the "within the veil" language in Lev 16:2. If Heb 6:19's "within the veil" echoes Leviticus, it points toward the mercy seat — toward God's very presence.

    Hebrews 9:6-7 — Two-Level Priestly Access¶

    Context: The author describes two patterns of priestly access. Direct statement: "The priests went always into the first tabernacle, accomplishing the service of God. But into the second went the high priest alone once every year, not without blood." Original language: eis men ten proten skenen (v.6) — "into the first tabernacle" (present tense: ongoing, continual). eis de ten deuteran (v.7) — "into the second" (the noun skene is implied). hapax tou eniautou ("once a year") + monos ("alone") + ou choris haimatos ("not without blood"). Cross-references: Lev 16:2,12,15,17 — the DOA entry pattern. Relationship to other evidence: The author sets up a contrast: many priests / one high priest; continual / once a year; first compartment / second compartment. This entire pattern is about to be declared obsolete by 9:8.

    Hebrews 9:8 — The Way Not Yet Manifest¶

    Context: The Holy Spirit's lesson from the two-tabernacle arrangement. Direct statement: "The Holy Ghost this signifying, that the way into the holiest of all was not yet made manifest, while as the first tabernacle was yet standing." Original language: ten ton hagion hodon ("the way of the holy places") — access to God's presence. me pephaneroosthai (perfect passive infinitive, "had not been revealed"). tes protes skenes echousees stasin (genitive absolute: "the first tabernacle having standing/status"). Critical ambiguity: Does "the first tabernacle" here mean (a) the first compartment (architectural reading: the Holy Place physically blocked access to the MHP) or (b) the first-covenant system (covenantal reading: the old-covenant order as a whole prevented access to God's full presence)? stasis can mean physical "standing" (spatial) or legal/institutional "standing" (status). The author has been using prote skene for the first compartment in vv.2 and 6, which favors the architectural reading. But v.9 shifts to "a figure for the time then present" and v.10 to "carnal ordinances, imposed until the time of reformation" — clearly temporal/covenantal language. The answer may be: both. The architectural barrier symbolized the covenantal barrier. The first compartment's existence as a block to the second was itself the parable about the first-covenant system blocking full access to God. Cross-references: Heb 10:19-20 — the way IS now open. Relationship to other evidence: Whether "first tabernacle" is spatial or covenantal affects whether Hebrews thinks in two-apartment categories or two-covenant categories. The text appears to use the spatial arrangement as a vehicle for the covenantal point — both models operate together.

    Hebrews 9:9-10 — The Time of Reformation¶

    Context: The earthly sanctuary was a temporary illustration. Direct statement: "Which was a figure for the time then present... imposed on them until the time of reformation (diorthosis)." Relationship to other evidence: diorthosis ("setting right") points forward to the new-covenant era. The old-covenant sanctuary arrangement was provisional, not permanent.

    Hebrews 9:11-12 — Christ's Entry¶

    Context: The author now describes what Christ accomplished. Direct statement: "Christ being come an high priest of good things to come, by a greater and more perfect tabernacle, not made with hands... neither by the blood of goats and calves, but by his own blood he entered in once into the holy place, having obtained eternal redemption." Original language: meizonos ("greater," comparative) and teleioteras ("more perfect," comparative) — the heavenly sanctuary surpasses the earthly. eiselthen ephapax eis ta hagia — "entered once for all into ta hagia." The ta hagia here could mean the entire heavenly sanctuary (consistent with 9:24 "into heaven itself") or the MHP specifically (consistent with DOA typology). The grammar does not resolve this (G39 word study, canonical). Cross-references: Heb 9:24 equates the destination with "heaven itself" and "the presence of God." Heb 9:25 compares Christ's entry to the high priest's annual entry (DOA pattern). Relationship to other evidence: ta hagia in v.12 parallels "heaven itself" in v.24, suggesting the author is thinking of the whole heavenly sphere rather than a compartment within it.

    Hebrews 9:13-14 — Blood of Christ vs. Animal Blood¶

    Context: Argument from lesser to greater. Direct statement: "How much more shall the blood of Christ... purge your conscience from dead works to serve the living God?" Relationship to other evidence: The blood's effect is on the conscience — a moral/spiritual result that the earthly sacrifices could not achieve (v.9).

    Hebrews 9:15-22 — Covenant Dedication With Blood¶

    Context: The author connects covenant inauguration with blood. Direct statement: "For this cause he is the mediator of the new testament" (v.15). "Neither the first testament was dedicated (enkekainistai) without blood" (v.18). Original language: enkekainistai (perfect passive of enkainizo, G1457) — the first covenant was "inaugurated" with blood. This is the same verb used in 10:20 for the "new and living way" that Christ inaugurated (G1457 word study, canonical). Relationship to other evidence: This is one of the most important passages for the phased-ministry question. The inauguration vocabulary connects Christ's ascension entry to a covenant-opening ceremony — NOT to the Day of Atonement. The earthly sanctuary had a distinct inauguration phase (Exo 40:9-11, Moses anointing the tabernacle and all its vessels; Lev 8-9, the seven-day consecration of priests culminating in the inaugural service). This inauguration occurred BEFORE the daily tamid began (Exo 40:29, the first burnt offering), and the daily tamid ran throughout the year BEFORE the annual DOA (Lev 16). Three distinct phases in the earthly system: inauguration → daily service → annual DOA. Daniel 9:24's sixth purpose — "anoint the most Holy" (mashach qodesh qodashim) — uses inauguration language for the heavenly sanctuary. The Hebrews author picks up this same thread: enkainizo describes what happened at the ascension, not what happens at the antitypical DOA. Christ's entry "within the veil" at the ascension inaugurated the heavenly sanctuary ministry — the same way Moses's anointing inaugurated the earthly ministry in Exo 40.

    Hebrews 9:23 — Heavenly Things Purified¶

    Context: The type-antitype purification argument. Direct statement: "It was therefore necessary that the patterns of things in the heavens should be purified with these; but the heavenly things themselves with better sacrifices than these." Cross-references: sv-22 established that this connects to Dan 8:14 as complementary vocabulary: Hebrews uses katharizo (purification); Daniel uses nitsdaq (vindication). Both describe aspects of the same heavenly event. Relationship to other evidence: The heavenly things require purification — this implies something more than a one-time inauguratory act. The DOA typology (purification of the sanctuary from accumulated defilement) is in view.

    Hebrews 9:24 — Into Heaven Itself¶

    Context: The author's most direct statement about WHERE Christ went. Direct statement: "Christ is not entered into the holy places made with hands, which are the figures of the true; but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us." Original language: eis auton ton ouranon — "into heaven itself." emphanisthenai to prosopo tou theou — "to appear before the face of God" (G4314 word study). antitypa ton alethinon — the earthly sanctuary is the "copy" (antitypos) of the "true" (alethinos). Cross-references: Heb 8:2 ("the true tabernacle"); Heb 8:5 ("shadow of heavenly things"). Relationship to other evidence: The author equates Christ's destination with "heaven itself" and "God's presence" — not a specific compartment but the divine presence. This favors reading ta hagia in 9:12 as the whole heavenly sanctuary rather than one compartment.

    Hebrews 9:25-26 — Not Often, But Once¶

    Context: Contrast between Christ's single entry and the high priest's annual entries. Direct statement: "Nor yet that he should offer himself often, as the high priest entereth into the holy place every year with blood of others; for then must he often have suffered since the foundation of the world: but now once in the end of the world hath he appeared to put away sin by the sacrifice of himself." Relationship to other evidence: The comparison is between repeated annual entries (DOA pattern) and Christ's single, definitive appearance. But the point is about the sacrifice, not about which compartment. Christ does not need to repeat his offering.

    Hebrews 9:27-28 — Death, Judgment, Second Coming¶

    Context: The conclusion of chapter 9's argument. Direct statement: "As it is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment: so Christ was once offered to bear the sins of many; and unto them that look for him shall he appear the second time without sin unto salvation." Relationship to other evidence: The sequence — death → judgment → second appearance — mirrors the three-phase model established in the series insights: cross (once offered) → intercession/judgment (the interval) → return (second time).

    Hebrews 10:1-4 — Shadow vs. Reality¶

    Context: The inadequacy of animal sacrifices. Direct statement: "The law having a shadow of good things to come, and not the very image of the things, can never with those sacrifices which they offered year by year continually make the comers thereunto perfect." Relationship to other evidence: The word "shadow" (skia) connects to 8:5. The earthly system is a shadow; Christ's ministry is the reality it pointed to.

    Hebrews 10:5-10 — Christ's Body as Offering¶

    Context: Psalm 40 quoted to explain Christ's self-offering. Direct statement: "A body hast thou prepared me" (v.5). "By the which will we are sanctified through the offering of the body of Jesus Christ once for all" (v.10). Relationship to other evidence: The "once for all" (ephapax) offering connects to the "sat down" in v.12. The sacrifice is complete and unrepeatable.

    Hebrews 10:11-12 — Standing vs. Sitting¶

    Context: The climactic contrast in the standing/sitting argument. Direct statement: "Every priest standeth daily ministering and offering oftentimes the same sacrifices, which can never take away sins: but this man, after he had offered one sacrifice for sins for ever, sat down on the right hand of God." Original language: hesteken (perfect active, "stands" — ongoing characteristic state) + leitourgon (present participle, "ministering") + prospheron (present participle, "offering") vs. prosenkengas (aorist participle, "having offered") + ekathisen (aorist, "sat down"). The tense contrast is sharp: the Levitical priest stands in an ongoing state of incomplete ministry; Christ, having completed his offering (aorist), sat down (aorist). Critical question: Does "sat down" mean priestly work is finished? The context specifies what is finished: offering sacrifices. The standing priest stands because "the same sacrifices can never take away sins" — his work is never done because it is never effective. Christ sat down because his one sacrifice was effective. The sitting signals the completion of the OFFERING, not the cessation of all priestly activity. Heb 7:25 ("ever liveth to make intercession") and 8:1-2 (sat down + leitourgos) confirm that ministry continues after the sitting. Relationship to other evidence: Series insight #16 established this precise reading. The horn's counterfeit reinstalls a system of standing priests, reversing the "sat down."

    Hebrews 10:13 — Expecting Until...¶

    Context: Immediately after "sat down." Direct statement: "From henceforth expecting till his enemies be made his footstool." Original language: ekdechomenos (present middle participle, "expecting/waiting") — ongoing activity after the sitting. heos (conjunction, "until") — the same temporal boundary as Psa 110:1. Relationship to other evidence: This verse proves the sitting is not the endpoint. There is a period of waiting/expecting that follows the enthronement. The enemies are not yet subdued. Something is still happening.

    Hebrews 10:14 — Perfected and Being Sanctified¶

    Context: The completed sacrifice's ongoing effect. Direct statement: "By one offering he hath perfected for ever them that are sanctified." Original language: teteleiooken (perfect active, "has perfected" — completed with ongoing result) vs. hagiazomenous (present passive participle, "being sanctified" — ongoing process). The deliberate tense contrast mirrors the standing/sitting contrast: the sacrifice's legal effect is complete (perfect tense) but its experiential application is ongoing (present tense). atone-12 identified this as a key finding. Relationship to other evidence: The ongoing sanctification is consistent with ongoing priestly ministry. The sacrifice is done; the application is not.

    Hebrews 10:19-20 — Boldness Through the Veil¶

    Context: The practical application of everything in chapters 7-10. Direct statement: "Having therefore, brethren, boldness to enter into the holiest by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way, which he hath consecrated for us, through the veil, that is to say, his flesh." Original language: parrhesian (G3954, "boldness/confidence") — the new-covenant access that the old system never offered (G3954 word study, canonical). eisodon ton hagion — "entrance of the holy places." enekainisen (aorist of enkainizo, G1457) — "he inaugurated" a new and living way (G1457 word study). dia tou katapetasmatos — "through the veil." tout' estin tes sarkos autou — "that is, his flesh." Gender mismatch in the apposition: katapetasmatos is neuter; sarkos is feminine. The identification "veil = his flesh" crosses grammatical genders, signaling a theological/metaphorical connection rather than a strict grammatical one. The veil is not literally Christ's body but access goes through the veil by means of Christ's broken body. Cross-references: Heb 6:19 (entering within the veil); Heb 9:8 (the way not yet manifest — now it is). Relationship to other evidence: This fulfills the prodromos promise of 6:20. Christ entered as forerunner; now believers follow with boldness. The way is "new" (prosphaton) and "living" (zosan) — permanently open, not sealed after use.

    Hebrews 10:21-22 — Drawing Near¶

    Context: Exhortation following the access declaration. Direct statement: "Having an high priest over the house of God; let us draw near with a true heart in full assurance of faith." Relationship to other evidence: The "high priest over the house of God" echoes 3:1-6. The access is ongoing — "let us draw near" is present tense.

    Hebrews 10:23-25 — Hold Fast, Consider, Assemble¶

    Context: Practical exhortation. Direct statement: "Let us hold fast the profession of our faith without wavering; for he is faithful that promised" (v.23). "So much the more, as ye see the day approaching" (v.25). Relationship to other evidence: "The day approaching" implies an eschatological horizon — consistent with the "until" of Psa 110:1 and Heb 10:13.

    Hebrews 12:2 — Seated at God's Right Hand¶

    Context: The "great cloud of witnesses" exhortation. Direct statement: "Looking unto Jesus the author and finisher of our faith; who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame, and is set down at the right hand of the throne of God." Original language: kekathiken (perfect active, "has sat down / is seated") — this is the ONLY Hebrews "sitting" passage in the perfect tense. All others are aorist. The perfect emphasizes the resulting ongoing state: Christ is currently seated. archegos ("author/originator") parallels prodromos ("forerunner") — both portray Christ as the pioneer. Relationship to other evidence: The perfect tense here and the aorists elsewhere create a unified picture: Christ sat down (completed act, aorist) and remains seated (ongoing state, perfect).

    Mark 16:19 — Received Up and Sat Down¶

    Context: The ascension. Direct statement: "He was received up into heaven, and sat on the right hand of God." Relationship to other evidence: The simplest ascension-to-enthronement statement. No compartment mentioned — just "heaven" and "the right hand of God."

    Acts 1:9-12 — The Ascension Narrative¶

    Context: The disciples watch Jesus ascend. Direct statement: "He was taken up; and a cloud received him out of their sight" (v.9). "This same Jesus... shall so come in like manner" (v.11). Relationship to other evidence: The ascension is the event that places Christ in the heavenly sanctuary. The return promise creates the temporal frame — Christ is in heaven between ascension and return, which is the period of his heavenly ministry.

    Romans 8:34 — Right Hand and Intercession Together¶

    Context: Paul's argument for the believer's security. Direct statement: "It is Christ that died, yea rather, that is risen again, who is even at the right hand of God, who also maketh intercession for us." Original language: The sequence: died → risen → right hand → intercedes. Four activities. The intercession happens from the right-hand position. Sitting and interceding are simultaneous. Cross-references: Heb 7:25 ("ever liveth to make intercession"); Heb 8:1-2 (sat down + minister). Relationship to other evidence: Paul confirms the same picture Hebrews presents: enthronement does not end ministry.

    Colossians 3:1 — Seek Things Above¶

    Context: Ethical exhortation based on Christ's heavenly position. Direct statement: "Seek those things which are above, where Christ sitteth on the right hand of God." Original language: kathemai (present participle) — "is sitting" — the ongoing state of being seated. Relationship to other evidence: Confirms the picture: Christ is currently in the seated position, which is a position of authority, not retirement.

    Ephesians 3:12 — Boldness and Access¶

    Context: Paul on the mystery of Christ. Direct statement: "In whom we have boldness and access with confidence by the faith of him." Original language: parrhesian (same word as Heb 10:19) + prosagogen (access, from prosago, "to bring near") — confident access to God through Christ. Cross-references: Heb 4:16; 10:19. Relationship to other evidence: Confirms the access theology of Hebrews from a Pauline angle.

    Patterns Identified¶

    Pattern 1: Sitting Signals Completed OFFERING, Not Completed MINISTRY¶

    Every "sat down" passage in Hebrews explicitly connects the sitting to the completion of the sacrificial offering: - Heb 1:3 — "when he had by himself purged our sins, sat down" - Heb 8:1 — the "sum" combines sitting with being a leitourgos (minister) - Heb 10:12 — "after he had offered one sacrifice for sins for ever, sat down" - Heb 12:2 — "endured the cross... is set down"

    The contrast in 10:11-12 is specifically about offering: standing priest offers repeatedly; Christ offered once and sat down. But 7:25 ("ever liveth to make intercession") and 8:1-2 (sat down + minister) prove ministry continues. The sitting marks the end of sacrifice, not the end of all priestly activity.

    Supporting verses: Heb 1:3; 7:25; 8:1-2; 10:11-12; 12:2; Rom 8:34.

    Pattern 2: The LXX Conflation Creates the Ambiguity, and the Author Knew It¶

    The Hebrew OT preserves a clear two-word system: poreketh = inner veil; macak = outer screen. The LXX collapses both into katapetasma. The Hebrews author reveals his awareness of this collapse by specifying "the second katapetasma" in 9:3. He numbers the veils because the Greek word alone does not distinguish them.

    In 6:19, no number is given. This could mean the author trusted the OT "within the veil" resonance (always MHP in Leviticus) to carry the reference, or it could mean the referent is broader — the heavenly sanctuary as a whole, entered through whatever separates the earthly from the heavenly.

    Supporting verses: Exo 26:31,36; Lev 16:2,12,15; Heb 6:19; 9:3; 10:20.

    Pattern 3: Prodromos Transforms the Access Model¶

    The DOA high priest enters alone (Lev 16:17; Heb 9:7 monos). No one follows. But Christ enters as prodromos — a forerunner who implies followers. This is not merely a repetition of the DOA pattern but a transformation of it.

    The transformation unfolds across Hebrews: - 6:20 — Christ enters as forerunner - 10:19-20 — Believers now have boldness to enter the same space - 4:16 — "Come boldly unto the throne of grace" - 10:22 — "Let us draw near"

    The DOA pattern provides the typology (blood, high priest, veil, presence of God); the prodromos concept transforms the access rules (from restricted to open, from solitary to communal).

    Supporting verses: Lev 16:17; Heb 4:16; 6:20; 9:7; 10:19-22.

    Pattern 4: Psalm 110 Integrates What Seems Contradictory¶

    Hebrews draws on both Psa 110:1 (sit) and 110:4 (priest forever) as describing the same person simultaneously. The psalm itself places both in one oracle. Zechariah 6:13 makes the combination explicit: "sit and rule upon his throne; and shall be a priest upon his throne."

    Every Hebrews "sat down" passage alludes to Psa 110:1. Every Melchizedek priesthood passage alludes to Psa 110:4. The author never separates them into sequential phases. In fact, 8:1-2 places them in one sentence.

    Supporting verses: Psa 110:1,4; Zech 6:13; Gen 14:18; Heb 1:3,13; 5:6; 6:20; 7:17,21; 8:1-2; 10:12-13.

    Pattern 5: The Author Moves Freely Between Spatial and Covenantal Language¶

    Hebrews does not commit exclusively to either a spatial model (two compartments) or a covenantal model (two eras). It uses both:

    Spatial: 9:2-3 (first and second tabernacle); 9:6-7 (two levels of priestly access); 6:19 (within the veil) Covenantal: 8:7-13 (first and second covenant); 9:9-10 (figure for the time then present); 9:15 (mediator of the new testament) Both at once: 9:8 (first tabernacle having standing — architectural and institutional); 10:9 ("he taketh away the first, that he may establish the second" — covenant language using ordinals that echo tabernacle language)

    The author is comfortable using physical sanctuary language as a vehicle for covenant theology. He does not force a choice between the two.

    Supporting verses: Heb 8:7,13; 9:1-10; 10:9,19-20.

    Word Study Integration¶

    The most consequential word study finding is the katapetasma conflation. The Hebrew Bible uses two precise words (poreketh/macak) that the LXX collapsed into one (katapetasma). This single fact explains most of the interpretive difficulty. When the Hebrews author writes katapetasma, modern readers cannot automatically determine which curtain he means — but his original readers, steeped in the LXX, would have heard the word with its full Levitical resonance.

    The esoteros comparative form adds a layer. Grammar analysis shows that Koine spatial comparatives function as simple positional adjectives. Esoteros means "inner" or "interior" — it does not force "innermost of two." The phrase to esoteron tou katapetasmatos means "the interior of the veil" — the space on the other side of whatever curtain is named. The question shifts entirely to which veil.

    The prodromos word study reveals a fundamental change in the access model. Every DOA text emphasizes exclusion (Lev 16:2 "come not"; 16:17 "no man"; Heb 9:7 "alone"). Prodromos reverses this: the first one through the door implies others will follow. The word itself refutes the idea that Christ's entry is simply the antitype of the annual DOA entry, which was solitary and restricted.

    The histemi/kathizo contrast is revealing when the tenses are examined. Histemi in Heb 10:11 is perfect — standing as an ongoing characteristic state. Kathizo in 10:12 is aorist — sitting as a completed act. But the present participles (leitourgon, prospheron) in v.11 are parallel to the present infinitive (entynchanein) in 7:25. Standing + present participle of ministering = Levitical pattern. Sitting + present infinitive of interceding = Melchizedek pattern. The posture changes; the activity continues in a different form.

    The ta hagia (G39) ambiguity is the single most important lexical issue after katapetasma. All 10 NT uses are in Hebrews. Context must decide the referent each time. In 9:12, parallel with 9:24 ("heaven itself"), it likely means the heavenly sanctuary generally. In 10:19, parallel with 6:19 ("within the veil"), the MHP resonance is stronger. The author may be intentionally fluid — the heavenly sanctuary does not map neatly onto the earthly compartment system.

    Cross-Testament Connections¶

    The OT-to-NT connections run through three main channels:

    1. Psalm 110 → Hebrews' Christology. Psalm 110:1 is the source of every "right hand" and "sat down" statement in Hebrews. Psalm 110:4 is the source of every Melchizedek priesthood statement. The Hebrews author reads these as simultaneous descriptions of one person's heavenly role. What the psalm declares, Hebrews applies.

    2. Leviticus 16 → Hebrews' Sanctuary Typology. The DOA pattern (high priest, blood, veil, mercy seat, alone, once a year) provides the framework for understanding Christ's entry. But Hebrews modifies every element: not animal blood but his own blood (9:12); not into the handmade sanctuary but into heaven itself (9:24); not alone (monos) but as forerunner (prodromos, 6:20); not annually but once for all (ephapax, 9:12).

    3. Zechariah 6:13 → The King-Priest Synthesis. Zechariah's Branch prophecy is the clearest OT statement that sitting-ruling and priesting can coexist in one person on one throne. While Hebrews does not directly quote Zech 6:13, its Melchizedek typology draws on the same concept: a figure who is simultaneously king and priest. Genesis 14:18 establishes the prototype; Psalm 110 provides the prophecy; Zechariah 6 provides the explicit combination; Hebrews provides the fulfillment.

    The LXX serves as the bridge between testaments. The poreketh→katapetasma mapping carries the veil theology from Hebrew to Greek. The kohen→hiereus mapping carries the priesthood theology (TR-kohen-hiereus, canonical). The qodesh→hagios mapping carries the sanctuary holiness vocabulary (TR-qodesh-hagios, canonical). Each mapping generally preserves semantic continuity, but the katapetasma conflation introduces a genuine ambiguity that the Hebrew text did not have.

    Difficult or Complicating Passages¶

    Hebrews 9:8 — First Tabernacle Having Standing¶

    This is the most difficult interpretive crux in the study. If "first tabernacle" means the first compartment (Holy Place), then its "standing" is an architectural barrier: while the Holy Place exists as a separate space, access to the MHP is blocked. This supports a two-compartment spatial reading. If "first tabernacle" means the first-covenant system, then its "standing" is an institutional barrier: while the old-covenant order was in effect, access to God's full presence was blocked. This supports a two-era temporal reading.

    The text seems to support both readings because the author has been using "first tabernacle" architecturally in vv.2 and 6, but then pivots to covenantal language in vv.9-10. The architectural arrangement is itself the parable about the covenantal situation. The Holy Spirit's "signifying" (v.8) is that the physical structure teaches a spiritual lesson. This means the spatial and temporal models are not competing alternatives — the spatial model is the vehicle for the temporal lesson.

    Hebrews 10:20 — The Veil as Christ's Flesh¶

    The apposition "through the veil, that is to say, his flesh" is theologically dense and grammatically unusual (neuter katapetasmatos ↔ feminine sarkos). Three readings are possible: (a) the veil IS his flesh (identification — Christ's body is the barrier that was broken to grant access); (b) access goes through the veil BY MEANS OF his flesh (instrumental — his incarnation/death is the means of passing through); (c) the veil was the first-covenant barrier and Christ's flesh was the means by which it was removed. The gender mismatch suggests the identification is not strict — it is a theological equivalence, not a grammatical one. The veil that once blocked access was rendered passable by Christ's sacrificed body. This does not require identifying the veil as one compartment or the other — it locates the breakthrough in the cross.

    Hebrews 9:12 — ta hagia as Destination¶

    When the author says Christ "entered into ta hagia," the reader expects a clear answer: which part? But the author provides none. In 9:24 (just twelve verses later), he equates the destination with "heaven itself" and "the presence of God." This either means ta hagia = the whole heavenly sphere (not a compartment), or it means the specific MHP compartment is equated with God's presence. The ambiguity may be intentional: the heavenly sanctuary is not a scaled-up version of the earthly tabernacle with discrete rooms. It is the reality the earthly structure pointed to. Trying to assign "ta hagia" to a specific compartment may be imposing earthly architecture onto a heavenly reality.

    The "Until" of Psalm 110:1 / Hebrews 10:13¶

    "Sit at my right hand until I make thine enemies thy footstool." The "until" (ad in Hebrew, heos in Greek) creates a temporal horizon that the text does not fill. What happens after the enemies are subdued? Does the sitting end? Hebrews does not say. It focuses on the DURING — the period between enthronement and final victory. This is the period of ongoing priestly ministry (7:25), ongoing sanctification (10:14), and ongoing hope (6:19). The "until" prevents reading the sitting as the final state of things. Something more comes after.

    Preliminary Synthesis¶

    The weight of evidence points toward a both/and reading rather than either/or:

    On the veil question: The Hebrew background strongly associates "within the veil" with the MHP (poreketh = inner veil in every OT occurrence). The author's awareness of the Greek ambiguity (proven by 9:3's "second katapetasma") suggests he used the phrase deliberately. But the heavenly sanctuary may not map onto the earthly compartments as neatly as the question assumes. Heb 9:24 equates Christ's destination with "heaven itself" — God's unmediated presence. The veil language conveys access to God's presence rather than a specific room assignment.

    On the sitting question: Sitting signals completed offering (10:11-12), not completed ministry. Heb 8:1-2 explicitly combines sitting with ministering. Heb 7:25 confirms ongoing intercession. Psalm 110 and Zechariah 6:13 establish the OT precedent for simultaneous sitting and priesting. The standing/sitting contrast is about the quality of the sacrifice, not about whether priestly work continues.

    On the spatial vs. temporal question: The author uses both models. The spatial model (two compartments) provides the typological framework. The temporal model (two covenants) provides the theological argument. The architecture teaches the theology. The "first tabernacle having standing" (9:8) is the hinge where both models converge.

    On the forerunner question: Prodromos transforms the DOA access model from solitary to inaugural. Christ's entry is not simply the antitype of the annual DOA entry — it opens permanent access for all who follow. This argues against reading Heb 6:19-20 as merely describing a once-a-year MHP entry. It describes an entry that stays open.

    What is established with high confidence: 1. Christ sat down because his sacrifice is complete and effective. 2. Christ's priestly ministry continues from the seated position (intercession, 7:25; minister of the sanctuary, 8:2). 3. Psalm 110 and Zechariah 6:13 establish that sitting and priesting are simultaneous. 4. The OT "within the veil" always means the MHP, but the Greek katapetasma is ambiguous. 5. Hebrews uses both spatial and covenantal models without requiring a choice between them. 6. The forerunner concept opens access rather than restricting it.

    What remains less certain: 1. Whether Heb 6:19's katapetasma specifically denotes the inner veil or the boundary between earth and heaven. 2. Whether the author envisions Christ in a specific compartment or in "heaven itself" as a whole. 3. How precisely the earthly two-compartment structure maps onto the heavenly reality.
