# Colossians 2:14-17 and the Ceremonial Law at the Cross

## Question
What was "nailed to the cross" in Colossians 2:14? What is the cheirographon tois dogmasin ("handwriting of ordinances")? Does this include the Day of Atonement? If the ceremonial law was abolished at the cross, how can its antitype (the heavenly judgment/DOA) begin in 1844?

## Summary Answer
The cheirographon tois dogmasin ("handwriting of ordinances") nailed to the cross was the ceremonial regulatory code written by Moses' hand — not the moral law and not the heavenly realities those ceremonies pointed to. The Greek vocabulary is decisive: cheirographon (G5498, hapax) means a hand-written document, while the Decalogue was written by God's finger (Exo 31:18); dogma (G1378) is never used for the Decalogue in any NT passage. Paul identifies the abolished items as "a shadow (skia) of things to come; but the body (soma) is of Christ" (Col 2:17). Abolishing the shadow confirms that the substance has arrived — it does not destroy the substance. The earthly Day of Atonement was a shadow; the heavenly judgment/purification (Heb 9:23; Dan 7:9-10; Dan 8:14) is the substance. Hebrews 9:23 states with logical necessity that "the heavenly things themselves" require purification "with better sacrifices" — this stands on its own basis, independent of whether the earthly ceremony continues.

## Analysis

### I. What Was Nailed to the Cross — The Cheirographon Identified

Paul uses three Greek terms to identify what was cancelled at the cross, and each points away from the moral law.

The first is cheirographon (G5498), a hapax legomenon — it appears only in Col 2:14 in the entire NT. The word is a compound of cheir ("hand") and grapho ("to write"). It means, literally, "something hand-written." In Hellenistic legal usage, a cheirographon was a signed note of indebtedness — a document written in the debtor's own hand acknowledging a debt. The canonical word study confirms this meaning.

This matters because the Decalogue was NOT hand-written. Exodus 31:18 states it was "written with the finger of God." Deuteronomy 9:10 repeats: "tables of stone, written with the finger of God." The authorship is divine, not human. By contrast, the ceremonial law was explicitly hand-written: "Moses had made an end of writing the words of this law in a book" (Deu 31:24). Moses wrote it by hand, delivered it to the Levites, and instructed them to place it "in the side of the ark of the covenant of the LORD your God, that it may be there for a witness against thee" (Deu 31:26). The phrase "for a witness against thee" matches Paul's description of the cheirographon as "against us" (kath' hēmōn) and "contrary to us" (hypenantios hēmin, G5227).

The second key term is dogmasin (G1378, Dat Pl N), rendered "ordinances" in the KJV. The canonical word study documents all five NT occurrences of dogma: Caesar's census decree (Lk 2:1), Caesar's decrees (Acts 17:7), the Jerusalem Council's decisions (Acts 16:4), the ordinances abolished in Ephesians 2:15, and the ordinances nailed in Colossians 2:14. In none of these five occurrences does dogma refer to the Decalogue or the moral law. It always means an official decree, regulation, or directive. Paul had better words available if he meant the moral law — nomos (G3551, 197 NT occurrences) or entolē (G1785, 71 occurrences). He chose dogma deliberately.

The third term is hypenantios (G5227), "contrary/opposed." The cheirographon was "against us" and "contrary to us." Paul would not describe the moral law in these terms — he calls it "holy, and the commandment holy, and just, and good" (Rom 7:12) and "spiritual" (Rom 7:14). The law that is "against" and "contrary" is the ceremonial regulatory code that burdened believers with requirements the sacrificial system could never permanently resolve.

Three verbs describe what Christ did with this document: exaleipsas ("having blotted out," G1813, Aor Act Ptcp), ērken ek tou mesou ("has taken away from the midst," G142, Perf Act Ind — the perfect tense marks completed action with ongoing result), and prosēlōsas ("having nailed," G4338, Aor Act Ptcp, another hapax). The imagery is vivid: the debt record was erased, removed, and publicly nailed to the cross — a Roman practice for displaying cancelled documents. The cancellation is complete and permanent.

The prior study laws-abolished-at-cross traced this same identification across all seven NT abolition passages and found the same result in every case: what is abolished, nailed, changed, or removed is always identified as ceremonial — never as the Decalogue. This study confirms that finding through closer examination of Col 2:14's specific vocabulary.

### II. The Shadow/Substance Distinction — Col 2:17 as the Linchpin

Col 2:16-17 is the bridge between "what was nailed" (v.14) and "why it no longer binds" (v.17). The conjunction oun ("therefore") in v.16 links the freedom from judgment to the nailing of v.14: BECAUSE the cheirographon was nailed, THEREFORE no one should judge regarding "meat, or drink, or holyday, or new moon, or sabbath days."

Verse 17 then explains the nature of these items: "Which are a shadow (skia) of things to come; but the body (soma) is of Christ." The Greek parsing reveals several important features.

The relative pronoun ha is neuter plural, referring to ALL items listed in v.16 — the entire ceremonial package. The word skia (G4639) is used in the typological sense — the same sense found in Hebrews 8:5 ("serve unto the example and shadow of heavenly things") and Hebrews 10:1 ("the law having a shadow of good things to come"). The canonical word study confirms that in ALL three theological uses of skia in the NT, the word applies exclusively to the ceremonial system. No NT passage calls the Decalogue or the weekly Sabbath a "shadow."

The phrase tōn mellontōn ("of the things coming") uses the present active participle of mello (G3195) — "things in the process of coming." This is significant. The shadow pointed not just to the cross but to the entire range of realities in Christ's ministry that were still unfolding. The canonical word study for mello confirms this forward-looking sense. Hebrews 10:1 uses the identical construction: mellontōn agathōn, "good things to come." These "good things" include Christ's ongoing priestly ministry (Heb 8:1-2; 9:24), his intercession (Heb 7:25), and the heavenly purification (Heb 9:23).

The contrast with soma (G4983, "body/substance") is the critical move. The ceremonies were the shadow; Christ is the substance. The shadow is the outline cast by a solid object — remove the shadow, and the solid object remains. This is not an inference; it is the nature of what a shadow IS. A shadow depends on the substance, not the other way around. When Paul says the ceremonial system was "a shadow of things to come, but the body is of Christ," he identifies the ceremonies as derivative and temporary, and Christ's reality as original and permanent.

### III. Abolishing the Shadow Confirms the Substance — The Type/Antitype Logic

This is the study's central contribution. The objection runs: "If the ceremonial law (including the Day of Atonement) was abolished at the cross, then its antitype cannot begin in 1844." The objection confuses the shadow with the substance, the type with the antitype.

Every type/antitype pair in the NT demonstrates the same pattern: the earthly type ceases, and the heavenly antitype continues.

**The Passover lamb was abolished — Christ's sacrifice continues.** "Christ our passover is sacrificed for us" (1 Cor 5:7). No one argues that because the Passover lamb is no longer killed, Christ's sacrifice has no effect. The opposite is true: the type ceased because the antitype arrived.

**The Levitical priesthood was abolished — Christ's priesthood continues forever.** "But this man, because he continueth ever, hath an unchangeable priesthood. Wherefore he is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him, seeing he ever liveth to make intercession for them" (Heb 7:24-25). The Levitical priests died; Christ's priesthood is permanent. The type ended; the antitype endures.

**The earthly sanctuary service was abolished — Christ's heavenly ministry continues.** "We have such an high priest, who is set on the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in the heavens; a minister of the sanctuary, and of the true tabernacle, which the Lord pitched, and not man" (Heb 8:1-2). "Christ is not entered into the holy places made with hands, which are the figures of the true; but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us" (Heb 9:24). The earthly sanctuary was "made with hands" — a copy. The heavenly is "the true." The copy ceased; the original continues.

**The earthly Day of Atonement was abolished — the heavenly judgment/purification continues.** This follows the same pattern. The DOA was part of the ceremonial calendar (Lev 23:27-32), described as a "sabbath of rest" (Lev 23:32) on a fixed calendar date — one of the ceremonial sabbaths listed in Col 2:16. It was a shadow (Col 2:17) that pointed to the heavenly purification described in Hebrews 9:23 and the judgment scene of Daniel 7:9-10. The earthly ceremony ceased at the cross. The heavenly reality it foreshadowed was not diminished but confirmed.

The logic is simple: shadows are produced by real objects. When you remove the shadow (by turning off the light, by ending the ceremonial system), the real object does not disappear. The real object was always there — the shadow was just a projection of it. Ending the projection confirms the existence of the real object.

### IV. Hebrews 9:23 — The Heavenly Things Require Their Own Purification

Hebrews 9:23 is the single most important verse for this question because it states the heavenly purification as a logical necessity independent of the earthly type.

The verse opens with Anankē oun — "It was THEREFORE NECESSARY." The word anankē (G318) means compulsion, necessity, constraint. The logical connector oun ("therefore") draws the conclusion from what precedes. Verse 22 established the principle: "without shedding of blood is no remission." Verse 23 applies the principle: if the copies (hupodeigmata) of the heavenly things needed purification with animal blood, then the heavenly things THEMSELVES (auta de ta epourania) need purification with better sacrifices (kreittoisin thysiais).

The argument is a fortiori — from the lesser to the greater. If the earthly required purification, how much more the heavenly. This logical necessity does not depend on whether the earthly ceremony continues. It depends on the nature of the heavenly sanctuary itself and the need for blood in remission. The heavenly sanctuary accumulated the moral claims of Christ's mediation (as sanc-22 established), and those claims require resolution. That resolution is the antitypical Day of Atonement.

The vocabulary confirms the connection to Leviticus 16. The verb katharizesthai (G2511, Pres Pass Inf, "to be purified") is the same verb used in the LXX for the Levitical purification in Lev 16:19,30 — the Greek equivalent of taher (H2891). The author of Hebrews deliberately chose Day of Atonement language for the heavenly event. He did not use dikaioō (the word behind Dan 8:14's nitsdaq); he used katharizō — purification language, not vindication language. Hebrews 9:23 describes the priestly mechanism; Daniel 8:14 describes the judicial outcome. They are two angles on the same reality.

Verse 24 then explains WHERE this purification occurs: "Christ is not entered into the holy places made with hands, which are the figures (antitypa, G499) of the true; but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us." The earthly was the antitype (the copy); the heavenly is "the true" (ta alēthina). Christ entered the heavenly sanctuary — the original that Moses' tabernacle copied (Heb 8:5; Exo 25:40). His ministry there is the substance of what the earthly ministry shadowed.

### V. The Temporal Dimension — mellontōn and the Ongoing Fulfillment

An important detail in Col 2:17 is the phrase "things to come" (tōn mellontōn). The present active participle of mello (G3195) describes realities in the process of arriving. The ceremonies did not merely point to the cross — they pointed to everything in Christ's ministry.

Hebrews 10:1 uses the identical construction: "the law having a shadow of good things to come" (mellontōn agathōn). The "good things to come" are then identified in the surrounding context: Christ's one sacrifice (10:10,12,14), his ongoing intercession at God's right hand (10:12-13), the perfecting of the sanctified (10:14), and the new covenant with laws written on hearts (10:16). These are not all past events — some continue into the present and future.

This means the shadow system pointed beyond the cross. It pointed to Christ's entire priestly ministry — his sacrifice (Heb 9:12), his entrance into the heavenly sanctuary (Heb 9:24), his intercession (Heb 7:25), and the purification of the heavenly things (Heb 9:23). The Day of Atonement, as the climactic annual event in the ceremonial calendar, foreshadowed the climactic event in Christ's heavenly ministry — the judgment and purification that Dan 7:9-10 and Dan 8:14 describe.

### VI. The Convergence of Daniel and Hebrews

The prior studies in the sv and sanc series established three independent lines of evidence that converge on the heavenly antitypical Day of Atonement:

**Daniel 7:9-10** describes a heavenly judgment scene: "the judgment was set, and the books were opened." Thrones are placed, the Ancient of days sits, the Son of man approaches. This is a court proceeding with cosmic scope — "thousand thousands ministered unto him, and ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him." The judgment involves books of record, corresponding to the earthly DOA's examination and resolution of accumulated sin records (Lev 16:16,30).

**Daniel 8:14** provides the timing and the outcome: "Unto two thousand and three hundred days; then shall the sanctuary be cleansed [vindicated]." The Hebrew nitsdaq (Niphal of tsadaq) means "to be vindicated, declared righteous" — a forensic term. The sanctuary is not merely cleansed but judicially cleared. This connects to Dan 7's judgment scene as the outcome of that proceeding.

**Hebrews 9:23** provides the mechanism: the heavenly things require purification with better sacrifices. The vocabulary (katharizō) ties directly to the Day of Atonement typology (taher in Lev 16). The logical necessity (anankē) makes this purification an inherent requirement of the type/antitype relationship.

These three passages — from three different biblical authors in three different genres (apocalyptic vision, apocalyptic prophecy, theological epistle) — converge on the same reality: a heavenly event corresponding to the earthly Day of Atonement. The earthly ceremony was the shadow. The heavenly event is the substance. Abolishing the shadow does not prevent the substance from operating; it confirms that the substance is real and active.

## Word Studies

The original-language evidence is decisive at every point in this study.

**cheirographon (G5498, canonical):** Hand-written document. The Decalogue was God-written. These are different documents. The cheirographon is the Mosaic ceremonial code.

**dogma (G1378, canonical):** Never used for the Decalogue in any NT passage. Always civil decrees or ceremonial regulations. Specifies the content of the cheirographon as ceremonial ordinances.

**skia (G4639, canonical):** All three theological NT uses apply exclusively to the ceremonial system. The shadow category belongs to ceremonies, not to the moral law.

**soma (G4983, canonical):** In Col 2:17, "the body/substance is of Christ" — the reality that the shadow pointed to. The substance belongs to Christ and operates through his ministry.

**mello (G3195, canonical):** The "things to come" are realities in the process of arriving — extending beyond the cross to Christ's entire heavenly ministry.

**katharizo (G2511, canonical):** Levitical purification vocabulary deliberately used for the heavenly purification in Heb 9:23. Connects the heavenly event directly to the DOA typology.

**exaleipho (G1813, fresh):** "To blot out/erase." Used for the cheirographon (Col 2:14), for sins (Acts 3:19), and for names in the book of life (Rev 3:5). Consistently describes permanent erasure of a written record.

**proseloo (G4338, fresh):** Hapax. "To nail." The cancelled document is publicly displayed on the cross — a completed, permanent cancellation.

## Difficult Passages

### Col 2:17 — Does mellontōn mean "things still future" or "things that arrived at the cross"?
If "things to come" means only the cross event (now past), there would be no basis for future antitypical fulfillment. However, the present participle is from Paul's own temporal standpoint and the context in Hebrews uses the same phrase (mellontōn agathōn, Heb 10:1) for realities that include Christ's ongoing priestly ministry. Heb 9:28 explicitly states there are still future events: Christ "shall appear the second time." The "things to come" extend through the entire range of Christ's redemptive work.

### Heb 9:23 — "better sacrifices" (plural) vs. Christ's one sacrifice
Hebrews elsewhere emphasizes Christ's single offering (Heb 10:12,14). The plural in 9:23 could suggest multiple heavenly sacrifices, which would contradict the one-sacrifice emphasis. The best reading, as sanc-22 established, is that the plural reflects the typological perspective — one sacrifice viewed as fulfilling what ALL the earthly sacrifices (inauguration, daily, and annual) pointed to. The single sacrifice has multiple applications corresponding to the multiple earthly ceremonies.

### The objection: "Abolishing the DOA means there is no DOA antitype"
This confuses the map with the territory. The earthly DOA was a map — a shadow, a copy, a pattern (all words the NT uses). The heavenly judgment/purification is the territory — the real landscape the map described. When you no longer need the map (because you have arrived at the destination), the destination does not disappear. You put away the map BECAUSE you have reached the reality. Heb 10:9 states this explicitly: "He taketh away the first, that he may establish the second." The type is removed SO THAT the antitype can stand.

### Hebrews 8:13 — "ready to vanish away"
The old covenant arrangement (including the DOA ceremony) was decaying and vanishing. But the bookend structure of Heb 8-10 shows that what vanishes is the sacrificial system (defined in Heb 9:1-10). What continues is "my laws" on hearts (Heb 8:10; 10:16) and Christ's heavenly priestly ministry (Heb 8:1-2; 9:24). The heavenly reality does not vanish with the earthly shadow.

## Conclusion

The Bible's answer to the objection — "if the ceremonial law was abolished, how can its antitype begin in 1844?" — is built into the type/antitype framework itself.

What was nailed to the cross was the cheirographon tois dogmasin — a hand-written document of ceremonial ordinances (Col 2:14). The Greek vocabulary proves this beyond reasonable doubt: cheirographon means "hand-written" while the Decalogue was God-written (Exo 31:18); dogma is never used for the Decalogue in any NT passage (canonical word study, 5 occurrences); the items listed are "shadow" (skia), and skia is applied exclusively to the ceremonial system in all three NT theological uses (Col 2:17; Heb 8:5; Heb 10:1).

The Day of Atonement was part of this ceremonial shadow system (Lev 23:27-32). It ceased at the cross along with the rest of the ceremonial calendar. But its cessation does not eliminate the heavenly reality it foreshadowed — any more than the cessation of the Passover lamb eliminates Christ's sacrifice, or the cessation of the Levitical priesthood eliminates Christ's priesthood, or the cessation of the earthly sanctuary service eliminates Christ's heavenly ministry.

Hebrews 9:23 states the heavenly purification as a logical necessity (anankē) grounded in the type/antitype relationship itself: if the earthly copies required purification, then the heavenly originals require purification with better sacrifices. This stands on its own basis, independent of whether the earthly ceremony continues. The heavenly purification is not derived from the earthly DOA; both are derived from the same divine plan — the earthly was the shadow, and the heavenly is the substance.

Daniel 7:9-10 describes the heavenly judgment. Daniel 8:14 provides the timing and outcome. Hebrews 9:23 provides the mechanism. Three witnesses, three genres, one event: the antitypical Day of Atonement in the heavenly sanctuary. The earthly shadow pointed to it. The heavenly reality fulfills it. Abolishing the shadow confirms the substance.
