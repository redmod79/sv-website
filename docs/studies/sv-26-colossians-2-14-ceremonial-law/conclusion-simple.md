# Was the Sanctuary System Nailed to the Cross?
## A Plain-English Summary

Most people have heard that Jesus "fulfilled the law" when he died on the cross. But what exactly was fulfilled — and what continued? Colossians 2:14 says something specific was "nailed to his cross." A closer look at what Paul actually wrote, and how it connects to Hebrews and Daniel, gives a surprisingly clear answer.

---

## What Did Paul Actually Say?

> "Blotting out the handwriting of ordinances that was against us, which was contrary to us, and took it out of the way, nailing it to his cross." — Colossians 2:14

The phrase "handwriting of ordinances" is a translation of two Greek words: *cheirographon* (hand-written document) and *dogmasin* (ordinances/decrees). Both of these words are significant.

A *cheirographon* was a legal term in the ancient world — think of it as a signed debt note, written in the debtor's own hand. The key detail is that it was hand-written. This matters because the Ten Commandments were *not* hand-written by a human. Exodus 31:18 says they were "written with the finger of God." The ceremonial law, however — the rules about sacrifices, feast days, and temple rituals — was written by Moses' hand (Deuteronomy 31:24). That's the document that fits the description.

The word *dogma* (translated "ordinances") is also telling. Paul uses it five times in the New Testament, and not once does it refer to the Ten Commandments. It always refers to official decrees or ceremonial regulations. If Paul had meant the moral law, he had better words available. He chose *dogma* deliberately.

Paul also calls this document "against us" and "contrary to us." He would never describe the moral law that way — he calls the commandments "holy, and just, and good" (Romans 7:12). What was "against" the people was the burden of a sacrificial system that could never permanently fix the sin problem. That burden was cancelled at the cross.

---

## What Kind of Thing Was Cancelled?

The very next verses tell us.

> "Which are a shadow of things to come; but the body is of Christ." — Colossians 2:17

Paul calls the cancelled items — feast days, new moons, sabbaths of the ceremonial calendar — a **shadow**. The real thing (the "body," or substance) belongs to Christ.

This is a crucial distinction. A shadow is cast by something solid. Remove the shadow, and the solid object does not disappear — it was the real thing all along. The shadow depended on the substance, not the other way around.

Every time the New Testament uses this word "shadow" to describe the ceremonial system, the point is the same: the ceremonies were a projected outline of Christ's real ministry. When Jesus arrived, the outline was no longer needed. But the reality the outline traced did not end — it intensified.

---

## The Pattern: Type and Antitype

The Bible follows a consistent pattern when it talks about the ceremonies and Christ. In every case, the earthly ceremony ends — but the heavenly reality it pointed to continues.

**The Passover lamb:** The lamb was no longer sacrificed after Christ died. No one concludes from this that Christ's sacrifice has no ongoing effect. The opposite is true. The type (the lamb) ceased because the antitype (Christ's sacrifice) arrived.

**The Levitical priesthood:** The earthly priests died and were replaced. Christ's priesthood never ends.

> "He is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him, seeing he ever liveth to make intercession for them." — Hebrews 7:25

**The earthly sanctuary:** The Jerusalem temple was eventually destroyed. But the author of Hebrews says Christ entered something greater — the original, not the copy.

> "Christ is not entered into the holy places made with hands, which are the figures of the true; but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us." — Hebrews 9:24

The earthly sanctuary was a model of the heavenly one. The model was set aside; the original continues.

**The Day of Atonement:** The same logic applies. The annual ceremony on earth ended at the cross. But the heavenly event it foreshadowed did not end — it was confirmed.

---

## The Heavenly Sanctuary Requires Its Own Purification

This is where Hebrews 9:23 becomes essential. It does not merely imply a heavenly Day of Atonement — it states one as a logical necessity.

> "It was therefore necessary that the patterns of things in the heavens should be purified with these; but the heavenly things themselves with better sacrifices than these." — Hebrews 9:23

The word translated "necessary" (*anankē*) means compulsion — something that cannot be otherwise. The argument runs like this: if the earthly copies of heavenly things needed to be purified with animal blood, then the heavenly originals need purification with something better. This conclusion does not depend on whether the earthly ceremony is still happening. It depends on the nature of the heavenly sanctuary itself.

The author of Hebrews deliberately chose Day of Atonement language here. The Greek verb for "purified" (*katharizō*) is the same word used in the Greek Old Testament for the Levitical purification on the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16:19, 30). The connection is intentional — the heavenly event is the substance of what the earthly ceremony shadowed.

---

## What Daniel Adds

Two passages in Daniel describe this heavenly event from a different angle.

> "A fiery stream issued and came forth from before him: thousand thousands ministered unto him, and ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him: the judgment was set, and the books were opened." — Daniel 7:10

This is a heavenly court scene — thrones placed, the Ancient of Days seated, books opened, judgment conducted. It corresponds to the earthly Day of Atonement, which was the annual moment when Israel's accumulated sin records were examined and resolved (Leviticus 16:16, 30).

> "And he said unto me, Unto two thousand and three hundred days; then shall the sanctuary be cleansed." — Daniel 8:14

Daniel 8:14 gives the timing and the outcome: after 2,300 days, the sanctuary is cleansed — or more precisely, vindicated. The Hebrew word (*nitsdaq*) carries the sense of being declared right in a legal proceeding. This is the judicial outcome of the court scene in Daniel 7.

Three passages — Colossians, Hebrews, Daniel — written by three different authors in three different styles, all point to the same event: a heavenly counterpart to the earthly Day of Atonement.

---

## Answering the Objection

People sometimes raise this challenge: "If the Day of Atonement was part of the ceremonial law that was abolished at the cross, how can there be a heavenly Day of Atonement starting in 1844?"

The answer is that this objection confuses the shadow with the substance. The earthly Day of Atonement was the shadow. The heavenly judgment and purification is the substance. Abolishing the shadow does not abolish the substance — it confirms it.

Think of it this way: if you put away a road map because you have arrived at your destination, the destination does not disappear. You put away the map precisely because the real place is now where you are. Hebrews 10:9 puts it plainly: "He taketh away the first, that he may establish the second." The type is removed so the antitype can stand.

The earthly Passover lamb was abolished — Christ's sacrifice stands.
The earthly priesthood was abolished — Christ's priesthood stands.
The earthly sanctuary was abolished — Christ's heavenly ministry stands.
The earthly Day of Atonement was abolished — the heavenly judgment stands.

> "For the law having a shadow of good things to come, and not the very image of the things, can never with those sacrifices which they offered year by year continually make the comers thereunto perfect." — Hebrews 10:1

The ceremonies pointed forward to "good things to come." Those good things include not only the cross, but Christ's entire ongoing ministry in the heavenly sanctuary — his intercession, his mediation, and the final resolution of the sin question.

---

## What This Means for the Series

This study serves as a foundation for the broader Daniel 8:14 and Sanctuary Vindication series. One of the most common objections to a post-cross heavenly sanctuary ministry is that the ceremonial law — including the Day of Atonement — was abolished at Calvary, leaving no basis for its antitype.

This study answers that objection directly: the abolition of the type is precisely what the type/antitype framework requires. The earthly ceremony was always meant to be temporary — a hand-drawn map pointing to a real destination. When Christ entered the heavenly sanctuary, the map was no longer needed. But the destination, the heavenly ministry, the judgment, and the purification described in Daniel and Hebrews — those are not shadows. They are the substance the shadows were always pointing toward.

The case for a heavenly antitypical Day of Atonement does not rest on the continuation of the earthly ceremony. It rests on three independent witnesses: Daniel 7 (the judgment scene), Daniel 8:14 (the timing and outcome), and Hebrews 9:23 (the logical necessity of heavenly purification). All three stand on their own, regardless of what happened to the earthly system at the cross.
