# Verse Analysis

## Verse-by-Verse Analysis

### Daniel 9:1-2
**Context:** Daniel, living in Babylon during the first year of Darius the Mede (539/538 BC), reads Jeremiah's prophecy about 70 years of exile.
**Direct statement:** "I Daniel understood by books the number of the years, whereof the word of the LORD came to Jeremiah the prophet, that he would accomplish seventy years in the desolations of Jerusalem."
**Original language:** Daniel "understood" (biyn, H995 — the same word Gabriel used in 8:16 and will use again in 9:23). He reads Jeremiah's 70 years as literal years — not as encoded or symbolic. This is the control case: prophetic time in a non-apocalyptic genre is taken at face value.
**Cross-references:** Jer 25:11-12 and Jer 29:10 are the source texts Daniel reads. 2 Chr 36:21 ties the 70 years to sabbatical-year compensation (Lev 26:34-35). The land "kept sabbath" for 70 years because Israel neglected 490 years of sabbatical rests.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse sets up the entire 70-weeks oracle. Daniel's prayer arises from reading about 70 years. Gabriel's answer of 70 weeks (= 490 years) mirrors the 490 missed sabbatical years that caused the 70-year exile. The numbers lock together.

### Daniel 9:3-19 (Daniel's Prayer)
**Context:** Daniel prays a corporate confession — "we have sinned, we have committed iniquity, we have done wickedly, we have rebelled." He uses all four sin-terms found in Lev 16.
**Direct statement:** Key prayer elements: confession of national sin (v.5), acknowledgment of covenant curse (v.11 — "the curse is poured upon us, and the oath that is written in the law of Moses"), appeal to God's righteousness (v.16 — "according to all thy righteousness"), and petition for the city and the temple (v.19).
**Original language:** The Deuteronomic covenant-curse language in v.11 ("the curse is poured upon us") echoes Lev 26 and Deu 28. Daniel frames the exile as covenant judgment.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:11's "the oath that is written in the law of Moses" points to Lev 26:34-43 — the sabbatical-year punishment. Dan 9:16's appeal to God's tsedaqah (righteousness) anticipates the answer in v.24: God will "bring in everlasting righteousness."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Daniel prays about sin and exile; Gabriel answers with a timeline for ending sin and bringing righteousness. The six infinitives of v.24 answer the prayer point by point.

### Daniel 9:20-23 (Gabriel's Arrival)
**Context:** While Daniel is still praying, Gabriel arrives "about the time of the evening oblation" — the tamid service. Gabriel identifies himself as "the man Gabriel, whom I had seen in the vision (chazon) at the beginning."
**Direct statement:** "At the beginning of thy supplications the commandment came forth, and I am come to shew thee; for thou art greatly beloved: therefore understand the matter (dabar), and consider the vision (mar'eh)."
**Original language:** "Consider the vision" uses biyn + mar'eh — the exact same commission from Dan 8:16. Gabriel is resuming unfinished business. The word mar'eh (not chazon) points specifically to the time prophecy (see sv-05, sv-12).
**Cross-references:** Dan 8:16 "make this man to understand the vision (mar'eh)" → Dan 8:27 "none understood the mar'eh" → Dan 9:23 "understand... consider the mar'eh." The biyn-chain tracks Gabriel's incomplete mission (established in sv-12).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse proves Dan 9's oracle is the answer to Dan 8's unresolved question. Gabriel returns to explain the mar'eh — the time element.

### Daniel 9:24
**Context:** The opening line of the 70-weeks oracle. Gabriel announces the scope and purpose.
**Direct statement:** "Seventy weeks are determined upon thy people and upon thy holy city, to finish the transgression, and to make an end of sins, and to make reconciliation for iniquity, and to bring in everlasting righteousness, and to seal up the vision and prophecy, and to anoint the most Holy."
**Original language:** Six telic (purpose) infinitives, all dependent on nechtak:
1. lekalle happesha — "to finish the transgression" (Piel of kalah, H3615)
2. ulehatem chatta'ot — "to seal up/complete sins" (Hiphil of tamam — Qere reading)
3. ulekapper avon — "to atone for iniquity" (Piel of kaphar, H3722)
4. ulehabi tsedeq olamim — "to bring in everlasting righteousness" (Hiphil of bo)
5. velachtom chazon venabi — "to seal/authenticate vision and prophet" (Qal of chatham, H2856)
6. velimshoch qodesh qodashim — "to anoint the holy of holies" (Qal of mashach, H4886)

The word nechtak (H2852) is a hapax legomenon — occurs only here in the entire OT. It is Niphal Perfect 3ms of chathak, "to cut off." The verb's root meaning is physical severance: cutting a portion from a larger whole. The 70 weeks are "cut off" from something bigger. Since Gabriel's mission is to explain the mar'eh (the 2300 erev boqer), the larger period from which the 70 weeks are cut is the 2300.

The sin-removal triad (pesha, chattaah, avon) matches the three terms in Lev 16:16,21 — the only place all three appear together. Cross-testament parallels confirm this: Dan 9:24 shares kaphar + qodesh + avon + pesha with Lev 16:16 (parallel score 0.427).

chatham in "seal up the vision and prophecy" carries the authentication sense (see sv-11). The fulfillment of the 70 weeks stamps the prophetic time system as genuine.

qodesh qodashim ("holy of holies") refers to the heavenly sanctuary (see sv-08). This phrase never refers to a person anywhere in the OT.

**Cross-references:** Lev 16:16 (sin-removal triad), Exo 30:10 (annual atonement with kaphar + qodesh + avon), Exo 40:9-11 (mashach + tabernacle inauguration), Isa 53:11 (righteous servant justifies many — same tsadaq root).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This single verse ties together DOA vocabulary (sv-02, sv-04), chazon/mar'eh tracking (sv-05, sv-12), heavenly sanctuary inauguration (sv-08), and the sealing system (sv-11). It is the bridge verse between the earthly DOA and the heavenly vindication.

### Daniel 9:25
**Context:** The timeline begins.
**Direct statement:** "Know therefore and understand, that from the going forth of the commandment to restore and to build Jerusalem unto the Messiah the Prince shall be seven weeks, and threescore and two weeks: the street shall be built again, and the wall, even in troublous times."
**Original language:** min motsa dabar — "from the going forth of the word/commandment." motsa (H4161) in the decree sense means an authoritative pronouncement going forth from power. The terminus a quo is a specific decree.

mashiyach nagiyd — "Messiah the Prince." Two absolute-state nouns in apposition. mashiyach (H4899) = "anointed one," used only twice as "Messiah" (here and v.26). nagiyd (H5057) = divinely designated leader who stands at the front.

The division: 7 + 62 = 69 weeks (483 prophetic days = 483 years). The first 7 weeks (49 years) cover the rebuilding period ("in troublous times"). The next 62 weeks (434 years) extend to the Messiah.

rechob wechăruwts — "the street and the wall/moat." charuwts (H2742) from the root meaning "cut/incised" — a defensive trench, not a stone wall. Together with rechob (open plaza), this represents full urban infrastructure: both public spaces and defensive fortifications. Only a decree that authorized full civil restoration (not just temple-building) fits this description.

**Cross-references:** Ezra 7:11-26 (Artaxerxes' decree granting judicial authority — the only decree matching "restore and build"). Ezra 1:1-4 (Cyrus — temple only). Neh 2:1-8 (Nehemiah — walls only, no civil authority). Ezra 6:14 groups all three under one "commandment."
**Relationship to other evidence:** The starting point question is decisive. The decree must authorize both RESTORATION (civil/judicial authority) and BUILDING (physical construction). Only Artaxerxes' decree in Ezra 7 (457 BC) includes both: "set magistrates and judges" (Ezra 7:25) + building the city. Cyrus authorized the temple. Nehemiah got permission for walls. Only Ezra carried the mandate to restore the judicial system.

### Daniel 9:26
**Context:** After the 69 weeks.
**Direct statement:** "And after threescore and two weeks shall Messiah be cut off, but not for himself: and the people of the prince that shall come shall destroy the city and the sanctuary; and the end thereof shall be with a flood, and unto the end of the war desolations are determined."
**Original language:** yikkaret mashiyach — "Messiah shall be cut off." Niphal Imperfect of karath (H3772). karath in the Niphal is the standard formula for judicial/divine cutting off — it means violent death with covenantal overtones. "But not for himself" (ve'eyn lo) — literally "and nothing to/for him." He dies not for his own benefit but for others.

am nagiyd habba — "the people of the prince that shall come." A different nagiyd from v.25. The Messiah-Prince of v.25 is mashiyach nagiyd. The prince in v.26 is associated with the people who destroy Jerusalem — Rome's armies in AD 70.

necheretset shomemot — "desolations are determined." charats (H2782) in the Niphal participle = God's irrevocable decree. The desolation of Jerusalem after the 70 weeks is part of the divine plan.

**Cross-references:** Isa 53:8 — "he was cut off out of the land of the living: for the transgression of my people was he stricken." The same karath + pesha + substitution pattern. Mat 27:50-51 — the veil rent at the crucifixion marks the end of the sacrificial system.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The two "cutting" verbs in vv.24-26 (chathak + karath) create a deliberate echo. chathak cuts TIME (v.24 — severing the 70 weeks from the 2300). karath cuts a PERSON (v.26 — the Messiah's death). Both are Niphal passives — both actions are done BY God, not by human initiative.

### Daniel 9:27
**Context:** The 70th week.
**Direct statement:** "And he shall confirm the covenant with many for one week: and in the midst of the week he shall cause the sacrifice and the oblation to cease, and for the overspreading of abominations he shall make it desolate, even until the consummation, and that determined shall be poured upon the desolate."
**Original language:** vehigbiyr beriyth — "he shall confirm/strengthen the covenant." This is Hiphil Perfect 3ms of gabar (H1396) with waw-consecutive. gabar means "to be strong, prevail." The Hiphil = "cause to prevail" or "strengthen." This is NOT karath beriyth ("cut a new covenant") — it is strengthening an EXISTING covenant. The WG-covenant library entry confirms: karath beriyth = new covenant, gabar beriyth = strengthen existing one. KJV's "confirm" for gabar appears nowhere else — this is the only occurrence.

yashbiyt zebach uminchah — "he shall cause the sacrifice and the oblation to cease." Hiphil of shabath (H7673) — causative cessation. zebach (sacrifice) + minchah (H4503, grain offering) represent the entire sacrificial system. The Hiphil form means the subject actively causes this cessation.

chatsi hashabuwa — "in the midst of the week." chatsi = half, midpoint. The crucifixion occurs at the midpoint of the 70th week.

The 3ms subject of this verse is grammatically ambiguous — it could refer back to mashiyach (v.25-26) or nagiyd habba (v.26). But the sustained Messianic subject from v.25 through v.26 ("Messiah the Prince... Messiah shall be cut off") makes the Messiah the natural antecedent. And the action described (confirming a covenant, causing sacrifice to cease) fits the Messiah's work, not a destroying prince's.

**Cross-references:** Heb 10:11-12 — the standing/sitting contrast. Earthly priests stand daily; Christ sat down after offering one sacrifice. Col 2:14 — "nailing it to his cross." Matt 27:50-51 — the veil rent.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The Messiah "confirms" (gabar) the covenant — He strengthens it, not replaces it. This fits the Abrahamic and Davidic covenant promises being fulfilled through Christ's ministry and sacrifice. Isa 42:6 and 49:8 describe the Messiah as given "for a covenant of the people."

### Numbers 14:34
**Context:** The spies have returned from 40 days of exploring Canaan. Israel rebels. God pronounces punishment.
**Direct statement:** "After the number of the days in which ye searched the land, even forty days, each day for a year, shall ye bear your iniquities, even forty years, and ye shall know my breach of promise."
**Original language:** yom lashshanah yom lashshanah — literally "a day for the year, a day for the year." This is the distributive-lamed construction: le- + definite article + noun = "per/for each." The grammar library (distributive-lamed-ratio.md) confirms: the le- encodes the ratio operator; the framing cardinals (40 days / 40 years) encode the ratio value. The 1:1 ratio (one day = one year) is explicit and divinely declared.
**Cross-references:** Ezek 4:6 uses the identical construction ("I have appointed thee each day for a year"). These are the only two verses in the OT with the yom lashshanah formula.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the DECLARATION text — God Himself establishes the day-for-year ratio. It is not an interpretive method invented by expositors. It is a divinely stated principle applied in judgment contexts.

### Ezekiel 4:4-6
**Context:** God commands Ezekiel to lie on his side for a symbolic number of days representing years.
**Direct statement:** "I have laid upon thee the years of their iniquity, according to the number of the days, three hundred and ninety days: so shalt thou bear the iniquity of the house of Israel... I have appointed thee each day for a year."
**Original language:** yom lashshanah — identical construction to Num 14:34. Same distributive lamed, same definite article, same 1:1 ratio. Two different prophets, different centuries, same divinely commanded ratio.
**Cross-references:** Num 14:34 (the only other occurrence of this exact formula).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Ezekiel and Numbers together form the biblical basis for the day-year principle. Both are performative — God commands the ratio as a decree, not as a suggestion. The grammar library confirms: the day-year formula is "declared" in these two texts and "applied" in the Daniel/Revelation cluster.

### Genesis 29:27-28
**Context:** Jacob completes his 7-year service for Leah. Laban says: "Fulfil her week."
**Direct statement:** "Fulfil her week, and we will give thee this also for the service which thou shalt serve with me yet seven other years."
**Original language:** shabuwa here = the 7-day bridal week. But the context pairs it with "seven other years." The word's fundamental meaning is "a sevened period" — the unit depends on context.
**Cross-references:** Dan 10:2-3 where Daniel adds yamim to shabuim ("weeks of days") to specify literal 7-day weeks. Dan 9:24 uses shabuim WITHOUT yamim — the absence signals a different kind of "sevened period."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse demonstrates shabuwa's flexibility. The H7620 library entry confirms: shabuwa is from sheba (seven), literally "sevened." Context determines whether the sevened period is days, years, or something else. The absence of yamim in Dan 9:24 is significant against the presence of yamim in Dan 10:2-3.

### Daniel 10:2-3
**Context:** Daniel mourns for three weeks after understanding the mar'eh.
**Direct statement:** "In those days I Daniel was mourning three full weeks [shabuim yamim — weeks of days]."
**Original language:** shabuim yamim — Daniel adds "days" to clarify these are literal day-weeks. The same author, in the same book, uses two different constructions: shabuim yamim (10:2-3, literal day-weeks) vs. shabuim alone (9:24, year-weeks). This is the strongest internal evidence from the book itself.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:24 (shabuim without yamim).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This yamim distinction is the grammatical linchpin. If shabuim always meant literal day-weeks, Daniel would have no reason to add yamim in 10:2-3. The addition proves he knew the word was ambiguous and wanted to specify. When he did NOT add yamim in 9:24, he intended a different unit.

### Ezra 7:7-9, 11-13, 25-26
**Context:** The seventh year of Artaxerxes I (457 BC). Ezra receives a letter from the king authorizing the Jewish return.
**Direct statement:** Key clause: "And thou, Ezra, after the wisdom of thy God, that is in thine hand, set magistrates and judges, which may judge all the people that are beyond the river" (v.25).
**Original language:** The decree grants civil and judicial authority — not just permission to build the temple. This is what makes it unique among the three Persian decrees.
**Cross-references:** Ezra 1:1-4 (Cyrus: temple only), Ezra 6:1-14 (Darius: confirms Cyrus), Neh 2:1-8 (Nehemiah: walls only).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Dan 9:25 says "from the going forth of the commandment to RESTORE and to BUILD Jerusalem." The word "restore" (shuwb, H7725) implies bringing back something that was lost — not just physical structures but governmental function. Only Artaxerxes' decree in Ezra 7 authorized the restoration of Jewish self-governance.

### Ezra 6:14
**Context:** The temple is completed.
**Direct statement:** "And they builded, and finished it, according to the commandment of the God of Israel, and according to the commandment of Cyrus, and Darius, and Artaxerxes king of Persia."
**Original language:** The Hebrew groups all three kings under a single "commandment" (ta'am). Uriah Smith argued this shows all three decrees form one composite command. The text can be read either way — as three separate decrees unified by divine purpose, or as three phases of one ongoing authorization.
**Cross-references:** Isa 44:28 (God's naming of Cyrus as the instrument of restoration).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Whether the decree is composite or singular, 457 BC remains the critical date because that is when the RESTORATION component (judicial authority) was added. The temple authorization alone (538 BC) does not satisfy Dan 9:25's "restore AND build."

### Ezra 1:1-4
**Context:** Cyrus' decree, 538 BC.
**Direct statement:** "The LORD God of heaven hath given me all the kingdoms of the earth; and he hath charged me to build him an house at Jerusalem."
**Original language:** The decree authorizes temple-building. No mention of restoring civil government, appointing judges, or rebuilding the city.
**Cross-references:** 2 Chr 36:22-23 (parallel account), Isa 44:28 (prophetic naming of Cyrus).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This decree is necessary but insufficient. It starts the physical return but does not match "restore and build Jerusalem." The city's governmental function requires the additional authority granted in Ezra 7.

### Nehemiah 2:1-8
**Context:** The 20th year of Artaxerxes (444 BC). Nehemiah requests permission to rebuild Jerusalem's walls.
**Direct statement:** "If it please the king... send me unto Judah, unto the city of my fathers' sepulchres, that I may build it."
**Original language:** Nehemiah receives letters for safe passage and timber for walls and gates. No mention of judicial authority, civil governance, or national restoration.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:25 requires "restore AND build."
**Relationship to other evidence:** The 444 BC date has been proposed as an alternative starting point (notably by Anderson's dispensationalist calculation using 360-day "prophetic years"). But Nehemiah's request covers only physical construction — walls and gates — not the "restore" component. And 444 BC + 483 years does not reach Jesus' baptism in AD 27 without non-standard calendar adjustments.

### Leviticus 26:34-35, 43
**Context:** The covenant-curse chapter. God warns what will happen if Israel disobeys.
**Direct statement:** "Then shall the land enjoy her sabbaths, as long as it lieth desolate... because it did not rest in your sabbaths, when ye dwelt upon it."
**Original language:** The land compensates for missed sabbatical rests during the exile.
**Cross-references:** 2 Chr 36:21 ("to fulfil threescore and ten years"), Jer 25:11-12 (the 70-year prophecy Daniel read).
**Relationship to other evidence:** 70 years of exile compensates for 490 missed sabbatical years (one every 7 years for approximately 490 years). Gabriel's 70 weeks (= 490 years) mirrors this number exactly. The punishment (490 years of neglect → 70 years of exile) is answered by the remedy (70 weeks = 490 years → Messianic fulfillment).

### 2 Chronicles 36:21
**Context:** The Babylonian exile.
**Direct statement:** "To fulfil the word of the LORD by the mouth of Jeremiah, until the land had enjoyed her sabbaths: for as long as she lay desolate she kept sabbath, to fulfil threescore and ten years."
**Original language:** This verse ties three threads together: Jeremiah's 70-year prophecy, the sabbatical-year compensation from Lev 26, and the actual exile.
**Cross-references:** Lev 26:34-35, Jer 25:11-12, Dan 9:2.
**Relationship to other evidence:** 2 Chr 36:21 is the historical confirmation that the 70 years of exile corresponded to the 490 missed sabbatical years. This creates the numeric framework that Gabriel uses in Dan 9:24.

### Jeremiah 25:11-12
**Context:** Jeremiah prophesies the Babylonian captivity.
**Direct statement:** "These nations shall serve the king of Babylon seventy years. And it shall come to pass, when seventy years are accomplished, that I will punish the king of Babylon."
**Original language:** shib'im shanah — "seventy years." Plain shanah (year), no apocalyptic encoding, no seal command, no unusual time unit. This is the CONTROL CASE established by the grammar reference (jer-25-11-12-seventy-years.md): a prophetic time-prediction that is NOT day-for-year.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:2 (Daniel reads this text literally), Jer 29:10 (promise of return).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Jeremiah's 70 years prove that day-for-year is genre-triggered, not universally applied. When a prophet uses plain shanah in a non-apocalyptic context, the number means what it says. The day-year principle applies only when specific signals are present: unusual time units, sealing commands, apocalyptic genre.

### Jeremiah 29:10
**Context:** God's promise to the exiles.
**Direct statement:** "After seventy years be accomplished at Babylon I will visit you, and perform my good word toward you, in causing you to return to this place."
**Original language:** The verb male ("accomplish/fill") appears — the same concept as Mark 1:15's pleroo. Time is "filled up" when it reaches its divinely appointed completion.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:2, 2 Chr 36:21.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Daniel was reading this very promise when Gabriel arrived. The 70-year promise was being fulfilled; Gabriel then announced the 70-weeks extension.

### Mark 1:14-15
**Context:** Jesus begins His public ministry in Galilee after John's imprisonment.
**Direct statement:** "The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand: repent ye, and believe the gospel."
**Original language:** peplerōtai ho kairos — "THE time HAS BEEN fulfilled." Two key features: (1) Perfect tense — completed action with present relevance. The prophetic clock has already reached its destination. (2) kairos with the article — "THE appointed time," not just any time. Jesus declares a specific prophetic period complete.
**Cross-references:** Gal 4:4 ("the fulness of the time"), Eph 1:10 ("the fulness of times"), Dan 9:25 (the endpoint of the 69 weeks).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the verification point. If 457 BC + 483 years = AD 27, and Jesus begins His ministry in AD 27 declaring "the time is fulfilled," the calculation is confirmed. The Greek perfect tense (peplerōtai) does not say "the time is now filling up" but "the time HAS BEEN fulfilled" — it is already complete. The prophetic clock arrived at its mark.

### Galatians 4:4
**Context:** Paul explains Christ's arrival in the fullness of time.
**Direct statement:** "But when the fulness of the time was come, God sent forth his Son, made of a woman, made under the law."
**Original language:** to pleroma tou chronou — "the fullness of THE time." Here Paul uses chronos (duration) rather than kairos (appointed moment). The divine timetable reached its completion. ēlthen (aorist of erchomai) = "came" — a definite historical arrival.
**Cross-references:** Mark 1:15, Eph 1:10.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Both Mark 1:15 and Gal 4:4 say the same thing with different vocabulary: God's prophetic clock reached its appointed terminus. The Son arrived at the pre-calculated moment.

### Luke 3:1-3, 21-23
**Context:** Luke provides the most detailed chronological framework for Jesus' baptism.
**Direct statement:** "In the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar" — six rulers named for cross-verification. "Jesus himself began to be about thirty years of age."
**Original language:** Luke names six overlapping officials: Tiberius (emperor), Pilate (governor), Herod Antipas (tetrarch), Philip (tetrarch), Lysanias (tetrarch), and Annas/Caiaphas (high priests). This cluster of names creates a precise historical crosshair.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:25 (the endpoint of the 69 weeks = Messiah's appearance).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Tiberius' 15th year = AD 27 by inclusive reckoning from his co-regency with Augustus beginning in AD 12, or AD 28-29 from sole reign in AD 14. Jesus was "about thirty" — consistent with a birth around 4 BC. The Luke 3 synchronism is the best external verification of the 457 BC + 483 = AD 27 calculation.

### Ephesians 1:10
**Context:** Paul describes God's eternal plan.
**Direct statement:** "That in the dispensation of the fulness of times he might gather together in one all things in Christ."
**Original language:** to pleroma tōn kairōn — "the fullness of THE times" (plural). The gathering of all things in Christ is the culmination of multiple appointed seasons, not just one.
**Cross-references:** Gal 4:4, Mark 1:15.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Paul treats prophetic time as something that fills up to a divinely set measure. The 70 weeks are one such "fullness."

### Isaiah 53:5, 8, 10-11
**Context:** The Suffering Servant prophecy.
**Direct statement:** "He was cut off out of the land of the living: for the transgression of my people was he stricken" (v.8). "By his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many" (v.11).
**Original language:** nigzar — "cut off" (Niphal of gazar, a synonym of karath). The same cutting-off concept as Dan 9:26's yikkaret. "For the transgression (pesha) of my people" — the same pesha that Dan 9:24 says will be "finished." yatsdiq rabbim — "shall justify many" (Hiphil of tsadaq) — the same tsadaq root as Dan 9:24's tsedeq olamim and Dan 8:14's nitsdaq.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:26, Dan 9:24, Dan 12:3 (matsdiqey — "turn many to righteousness").
**Relationship to other evidence:** Isaiah 53 is the fullest portrait of what Dan 9:26 states in compressed form. The Servant is "cut off" (Dan 9:26), bears "transgression" (Dan 9:24), and through His knowledge "justifies many" (Dan 9:24's "bring in everlasting righteousness"). The vocabulary connections are tight.

### Matthew 27:50-51
**Context:** The crucifixion.
**Direct statement:** "Jesus, when he had cried again with a loud voice, yielded up the ghost. And, behold, the veil of the temple was rent in twain from the top to the bottom."
**Original language:** The veil tearing from top to bottom signals divine action (top-down, not bottom-up). The barrier between the holy and most holy place is removed — the earthly sacrificial system is terminated at the moment of Christ's death.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:27 ("cause the sacrifice and the oblation to cease"), Heb 10:11-12.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the "midst of the week" event. Dan 9:27 says "he shall cause the sacrifice and the oblation to cease." The veil tearing is God's visible declaration that the sacrificial system has reached its end — its purpose is fulfilled in the death of the Antitype.

### Hebrews 10:11-14
**Context:** The writer of Hebrews contrasts the earthly priesthood with Christ's.
**Direct statement:** "Every priest standeth daily ministering... which can never take away sins: But this man, after he had offered one sacrifice for sins for ever, sat down on the right hand of God."
**Original language:** The standing/sitting contrast (see sv-09, Series Insight #16): histēmi (perfect active, ongoing state) vs. kathizō (aorist active, completed act). The earthly priest stands because his work is never done. Christ sat down because His sacrifice is complete.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:27 (sacrifice ceases), Col 2:14.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This passage explains the mechanics of Dan 9:27's "cause the sacrifice and the oblation to cease." The sacrificial system ceases not by destruction but by fulfillment. The Antitype has come; the type has served its purpose.

### Colossians 2:14
**Context:** Paul describes what happened at the cross.
**Direct statement:** "Blotting out the handwriting of ordinances that was against us, which was contrary to us, and took it out of the way, nailing it to his cross."
**Original language:** exaleipsas to cheirographon — "blotting out the handwriting." The record of ordinances is removed. This is the legal dimension of the sacrifice's cessation.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:27, Eph 2:15.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Another angle on the "midst of the week" event. The ordinances (ceremonial law pointing forward to Christ) are "nailed to his cross." They cease because they have been fulfilled.

### Isaiah 42:6
**Context:** God speaks to His Servant.
**Direct statement:** "I the LORD have called thee in righteousness, and will hold thine hand, and will keep thee, and give thee for a covenant of the people, for a light of the Gentiles."
**Original language:** The Messiah IS the covenant — not just the covenant-maker. He embodies and enacts the covenant relationship.
**Cross-references:** Isa 49:8, Dan 9:27 (gabar beriyth — the Messiah strengthens the existing covenant).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Dan 9:27's "he shall confirm (gabar) the covenant with many for one week" describes the Messiah strengthening the Abrahamic/Davidic covenant through His ministry, death, and the early church's gospel work. He is not cutting a new treaty; He is fulfilling and strengthening the existing promises.

### Isaiah 49:8
**Context:** God addresses the Servant.
**Direct statement:** "In an acceptable time have I heard thee, and in a day of salvation have I helped thee: and I will preserve thee, and give thee for a covenant of the people."
**Original language:** "An acceptable time" (eth ratson) — a divinely chosen moment. "Day of salvation" (yom yeshuah) — the appointed rescue.
**Cross-references:** 2 Cor 6:2 (Paul quotes this: "now is the accepted time; now is the day of salvation"), Isa 42:6, Dan 9:27.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The "acceptable time" language connects to the 70 weeks' precision. God's timing is not random — the Messiah arrives at the pre-calculated moment.

### Acts 7:54-60; 8:1-4
**Context:** Stephen's martyrdom and the resulting scattering.
**Direct statement:** Stephen is stoned. "At that time there was a great persecution against the church which was at Jerusalem; and they were all scattered abroad."
**Original language:** The scattering forces the gospel outward from Jerusalem into Judaea and Samaria (Acts 1:8 pattern).
**Cross-references:** Acts 10:34-45 (gospel to Cornelius), Acts 13:46 (formal turn to Gentiles).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Stephen's martyrdom (approximately AD 34) marks the approximate end of the 70th week — the point at which the gospel mission formally extends beyond Israel. The 70 weeks were "upon thy people and upon thy holy city" (Dan 9:24). When the period expires, the exclusive Jewish focus ends.

### Acts 10:34, 38, 44-45
**Context:** Peter's visit to Cornelius — the first Gentile convert.
**Direct statement:** "God anointed Jesus of Nazareth with the Holy Ghost and with power" (v.38). "On the Gentiles also was poured out the gift of the Holy Ghost" (v.45).
**Original language:** Peter references Jesus' anointing at baptism (Acts 10:38 = Luke 3:22 = Dan 9:25's "Messiah the Prince"). The Spirit falling on Gentiles demonstrates that the exclusive Jewish period has ended.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:24 ("upon thy people"), Acts 13:46.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The Cornelius episode shows the covenant being "confirmed with many" (Dan 9:27) — including Gentiles now, after the 70th week.

### Acts 13:46
**Context:** Paul and Barnabas in Antioch Pisidia.
**Direct statement:** "It was necessary that the word of God should first have been spoken to you: but seeing ye put it from you... lo, we turn to the Gentiles."
**Original language:** "First" (prōton) — the gospel goes to Israel first, then to the Gentiles.
**Cross-references:** Rom 1:16 ("to the Jew first, and also to the Greek"), Dan 9:24 ("upon thy people").
**Relationship to other evidence:** The formal turning to the Gentiles confirms the transition point. The 70 weeks were "upon thy people" — when they expired, the gospel was no longer exclusively Jewish.

### Daniel 8:14
**Context:** The sanctuary question.
**Direct statement:** "Unto two thousand and three hundred days; then shall the sanctuary be cleansed."
**Original language:** erev boqer — "evening morning." The unusual time unit from which the 70 weeks are "cut off" (sv-10). If the 70 weeks share a starting point with the 2300, then 457 BC + 2300 = 1844.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:24 (chathak — "cut off" from this period), Dan 8:26 (mar'eh is the time prophecy).
**Relationship to other evidence:** The 70 weeks function as a verification key for the 2300. The subset (70 weeks) is testable — its fulfillment can be checked against history. If the math works on the testable subset, it authenticates the same method applied to the parent period.

### Daniel 12:4, 9
**Context:** The sealing command.
**Direct statement:** "Shut up the words, and seal the book, even to the time of the end" (v.4). "The words are closed up and sealed till the time of the end" (v.9).
**Original language:** v.4 uses chatham (seal — concealment sense). v.9 uses satham (close up) + chatham together. sv-11 distinguished these: satham = concealment, chatham can mean either concealment or authentication depending on context. Here both are concealment-seals.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:24 where chatham means authentication (the 5th infinitive: "seal up the vision and prophecy").
**Relationship to other evidence:** The contrast between Dan 9:24's chatham (authentication) and Dan 12:4's chatham (concealment) is key. The 70 weeks authenticate the prophetic system (Dan 9:24). The broader vision is sealed for concealment until the time of the end (Dan 12:4). Same word, different function — context determines which sense.

### Revelation 5:5-9
**Context:** The heavenly throne room. A sealed book. Only the Lamb can open it.
**Direct statement:** "Thou art worthy to take the book, and to open the seals thereof: for thou wast slain."
**Original language:** The Lamb's death qualifies Him to unseal. This is Series Insight #6: the same event (Messiah's death, Dan 9:26) both authenticates the prophecy and qualifies the Lamb to unseal it.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:26 (Messiah cut off), Dan 12:4 (sealed book).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Authentication and unsealing are linked through the cross. The 70 weeks' fulfillment (including the Messiah's death) is the authentication event. The Lamb's worthiness to unseal (Rev 5) comes from the same death. The same act serves both functions.

## Patterns Identified

### Pattern 1: The Cutting Motif
Three "cutting" verbs run through Dan 9:24-27, each performing a different kind of severance.
- **chathak** (v.24) — cuts TIME. The 70 weeks are severed from the 2300. Hapax. Niphal passive.
- **karath** (v.26) — cuts a PERSON. The Messiah is violently killed. Niphal passive.
- **charats** (vv.26-27) — cuts FATE. Desolations are irrevocably determined. Niphal participle.

All three are passive forms — all three severances are done by God. The progression is: God cuts a time period, the Messiah is cut off within it, and the consequences are cut as an irrevocable decree. Supported by: Dan 9:24 (nechtak), Dan 9:26 (yikkaret, necheretset), Dan 9:27 (venecheratsah), Isa 53:8 (nigzar — another cutting synonym).

### Pattern 2: The 490 Mirror
The number 490 appears three times in the biblical narrative, each time connecting sin, exile, and restoration.
- **490 missed sabbatical years** — Israel's disobedience over ~490 years of monarchy (Lev 26:34-35).
- **70-year exile** = compensation for those 490 years (2 Chr 36:21).
- **70 weeks = 490 years** — God's plan to resolve sin and bring restoration (Dan 9:24).

The punishment is answered by the remedy using the same number. Supported by: Lev 26:34-35, 2 Chr 36:21, Jer 25:11-12, Dan 9:2, Dan 9:24.

### Pattern 3: The Day-Year Declaration and Verification
The day-year principle follows a three-step logic: declared, signaled, verified.
- **Declared:** God establishes the 1:1 ratio in Num 14:34 and Ezek 4:6 using the identical yom lashshanah formula.
- **Signaled:** Daniel's apocalyptic time prophecies use unusual units (erev boqer, shabuim without yamim) that mark them as candidates for day-year conversion. The yamim distinction (Dan 10:2-3 vs. 9:24) is the internal signal.
- **Verified:** The 70 weeks' historical fulfillment confirms the conversion. 457 BC + 483 years = AD 27. Jesus declares "the time is fulfilled" (Mark 1:15).

Supported by: Num 14:34, Ezek 4:6, Dan 9:24, Dan 10:2-3, Mark 1:15, Gal 4:4.

### Pattern 4: The Six Infinitives as Sin-Removal and Restoration
Dan 9:24's six purpose clauses divide into a 3+3 structure: three address sin, three establish what replaces it.
- Sin-removal: finish transgression (pesha), seal up sins (chattaah), atone for iniquity (avon).
- Positive establishment: bring in everlasting righteousness (tsedeq olamim), authenticate vision and prophet (chatham), anoint the most holy (mashach qodesh qodashim).

This mirrors the DOA logic: the annual service BOTH removes defilement AND restores the sanctuary to its original state. The 70 weeks accomplish at the antitypical level what the DOA accomplishes ritually. Supported by: Dan 9:24, Lev 16:16,21 (same sin-triad), Lev 16:30 (taher — cleansing result), Dan 8:14 (nitsdaq — vindication result).

### Pattern 5: Strengthening vs. Cutting a Covenant
Dan 9:27 uses gabar beriyth ("strengthen the covenant"), not karath beriyth ("cut a new covenant"). This distinction is deliberate.
- karath beriyth = cutting a NEW covenant (Gen 15:18, Jer 31:31). 87 of karath's 360 occurrences are in this formula.
- gabar beriyth = strengthening an EXISTING covenant. Only Dan 9:27 uses this formula. gabar means "to prevail, be strong."

The Messiah does not start something new in the 70th week — He completes something God already promised. Supported by: Dan 9:27 (gabar beriyth), Gen 29:27 (word group context), Isa 42:6, Isa 49:8 (Messiah AS the covenant), Heb 10:11-14 (fulfillment, not replacement).

## Word Study Integration

The original-language data reshapes three key areas of the English reading:

**1. "Determined" in Dan 9:24 means "cut off."** English readers see "seventy weeks are determined" and think "decreed." The Hebrew chathak (H2852) is a hapax that means physical severance. The 70 weeks are not merely decreed — they are cut from a larger whole. This single word is the bridge between the 70 weeks and the 2300. Without the Hebrew, the connection is invisible.

**2. "Confirm" in Dan 9:27 means "strengthen."** English readers see "confirm the covenant" and might think of ratifying a new agreement. The Hebrew gabar (H1396) means "cause to prevail" or "make strong." The contrast with karath (the standard covenant-making verb, used in v.26 for the Messiah being "cut off") reveals that the subject is not creating a new covenant but strengthening an existing one. This eliminates the interpretation that a future Antichrist makes a 7-year treaty.

**3. "The time is fulfilled" (Mark 1:15) carries prophetic weight.** The Greek peplerōtai (Perfect Passive Indicative of pleroo, G4137) says the time "has been fulfilled" — completed action, present state. kairos (G2540) with the article means "THE appointed time." Jesus is not making a vague statement about readiness; He is declaring a specific prophetic period complete.

The canonical word studies (H2852-chathak, H1396-gabar, G4137-pleroo, G2540-kairos) from the shared library provide verified semantic ranges that confirm these readings.

## Cross-Testament Connections

**1. Dan 9:24 ↔ Leviticus 16:16** — The sin-removal triad (pesha, chattaah, avon) in Dan 9:24 matches the three-term formula of the Day of Atonement in Lev 16:16. Cross-testament parallels confirm this with a 0.427 hybrid score sharing kaphar + qodesh + avon + pesha. The 70 weeks' work is framed in DOA language — what the annual cleansing did ritually, the Messianic work does permanently.

**2. Dan 9:26 ↔ Isaiah 53:8** — "Messiah shall be cut off" (karath) parallels "he was cut off out of the land of the living" (gazar). Both use passive cutting-off verbs. Both specify the death is substitutionary — "not for himself" (Dan 9:26), "for the transgression of my people" (Isa 53:8). The tsadaq connection continues: Isaiah 53:11's "justify many" (yatsdiq) uses the same root as Dan 9:24's "everlasting righteousness" (tsedeq) and Dan 8:14's "vindicated" (nitsdaq).

**3. Num 14:34 / Ezek 4:6 → Dan 9:24 → Mark 1:15** — The day-year principle is declared in the OT (yom lashshanah), applied in Daniel's apocalyptic prophecy (shabuim without yamim), and verified in the NT (peplerōtai ho kairos). The NT confirmation closes the loop: the OT principle works when applied to Daniel's numbers.

**4. Dan 9:27 → Mat 27:50-51 → Heb 10:11-14** — "Cause the sacrifice and the oblation to cease" is fulfilled when the veil tears (Mat 27:51), and explained theologically in Hebrews: the earthly priests stood daily because their work was never done; Christ sat down because His sacrifice was complete. The cessation is not destruction but fulfillment.

**5. Dan 9:26 → Rev 5:5-9** — The Messiah's death (Dan 9:26) both authenticates the prophecy and qualifies the Lamb to unseal the sealed book (Rev 5:5-9). Authentication and unsealing are linked through the cross.

## Difficult or Complicating Passages

### 1. The Antecedent of "he" in Dan 9:27
The grammar reference notes that the 3ms subject of vehigbiyr in v.27 is grammatically ambiguous. It could refer to mashiyach (the Messiah from vv.25-26) or to nagiyd habba ("the prince that shall come" from v.26). Dispensationalist interpreters take "he" as a future prince (Antichrist) who makes a 7-year treaty with Israel. The Messianic reading takes "he" as the Messiah who strengthens the existing covenant through His ministry and death.

The weight of evidence favors the Messianic reading: (1) gabar beriyth implies strengthening an existing covenant, not cutting a new treaty; (2) the sustained Messianic subject from vv.25-26 is the natural antecedent; (3) the action described (causing sacrifice to cease) fits Christ's work, not a political treaty. But the grammatical ambiguity is real. The text does not resolve it by naming the subject explicitly.

### 2. The Crucifixion Date
The "midst of the week" places the crucifixion 3.5 years after the 69th week's terminus (AD 27). This points to spring AD 31 (or AD 30 depending on the reckoning). But scholarly consensus ranges from AD 30 to AD 33 based on different Passover calculations, the Pilate chronology, and astronomical data for the Passover moon. The most commonly cited dates are AD 30, 31, and 33.

This does not undermine the 70-weeks framework — all proposed dates fall within the 70th week (AD 27-34). But the precision of "in the midst" depends on which crucifixion date is correct. The AD 31 date (spring, 3.5 years after autumn AD 27) produces the tightest fit.

### 3. The Tiberius Co-Regency Question
Luke 3:1 dates Jesus' baptism to "the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar." If counted from Tiberius' sole reign (August AD 14), his 15th year = AD 28/29 — slightly late for a AD 27 baptism. If counted from a co-regency with Augustus (beginning around AD 12), his 15th year = AD 27, matching the calculation.

The co-regency is attested but debated among historians. The text does not specify which reckoning Luke uses. The calculation works best with the co-regency dating, but this is not the only evidence — the mathematical chain from 457 BC stands on its own through Ezra 7's dating.

### 4. Ezra 6:14's "Composite Commandment" Reading
Ezra 6:14 lists Cyrus, Darius, and Artaxerxes under one "commandment." Uriah Smith argued this shows the starting point should be viewed as a composite decree rather than a single moment. But the Hebrew ta'am can mean "decree" or "command" in a general sense. Whether three distinct decrees constitute one composite starting point, or whether only the final (457 BC) decree satisfies "restore and build," is an interpretive question the text does not settle definitively.

The critical point is that 457 BC works mathematically AND textually. It is the only date that produces the correct terminus AND includes the "restore" (judicial authority) component.

### 5. The End of the 70th Week
The 70th week ends approximately AD 34. But no single event in AD 34 is explicitly identified in the text as the terminus. Stephen's martyrdom (Acts 7), the scattering of believers (Acts 8:1-4), and the gospel reaching Gentiles (Acts 10-11, Acts 13:46) all occur in this period. The transition from exclusively Jewish mission to universal gospel is gradual, not a single dateable event.

Dan 9:24 says the 70 weeks are "upon thy people and upon thy holy city." The expiration of this period does not mean God abandons Israel but that the exclusive Jewish focus of the prophecy ends. The gospel goes "to the Jew first, and also to the Greek" (Rom 1:16).

## Preliminary Synthesis

The evidence converges on a clear picture.

**The starting point** is Artaxerxes' decree in Ezra 7 (457 BC) — the only decree that authorizes both restoration of civil government and rebuilding of the city, matching Dan 9:25's "restore and build."

**The day-year principle** is declared in Num 14:34 and Ezek 4:6, signaled by the yamim distinction in Daniel, and verified by historical fulfillment. The 70 weeks use shabuim without yamim, distinguishing them from the literal "weeks of days" in Dan 10:2-3.

**The calculation** works: 457 BC + 483 years = AD 27 (accounting for no year zero). Jesus begins His ministry in AD 27 declaring "the time is fulfilled" (Mark 1:15).

**The 70th week** events match the Messianic reading: the covenant is "strengthened" (gabar, not karath); the sacrifice ceases "in the midst" (the crucifixion); the gospel extends beyond Israel by the week's end.

**The verification key function** is the series' distinctive contribution: the 70 weeks are "cut off" (chathak) from the 2300. They share a starting point. If the day-year conversion works on the testable 490-year subset (which it does — AD 27 confirms it), then the same conversion applied to the full 2300 is authenticated. The 70 weeks function as both a Messianic prophecy AND a mathematical proof that the day-year encoding is correct.

The difficult passages (antecedent ambiguity, crucifixion date, Tiberius reckoning, composite decree, gradual 70th-week terminus) are real but do not undermine the core findings. The mathematical precision is confirmed by multiple independent lines of evidence.
