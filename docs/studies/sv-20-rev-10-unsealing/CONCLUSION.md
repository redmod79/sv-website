# Revelation 10 — The Little Book Opened

## Question
What is the "little book open" (biblaridion eneogmenon) in the angel's hand in Revelation 10? How does the Dan 12:5-7 oath scene structurally parallel Rev 10:5-6? How does the content inversion (duration to termination) identify the little book as Daniel's sealed prophecy now permanently opened? What does the sweet-then-bitter experience (Rev 10:9-10) correspond to historically? What is the recommission of Rev 10:11?

## Summary Answer
The little book open in the angel's hand is Daniel's sealed prophecy — specifically the time element of the vision — now permanently unsealed. Five converging evidence lines identify it: a six-station vocabulary arc from Hebrew sealing to Greek opening, a five-element oath scene replication with content inversion (duration to termination), the raz-to-mysterion disclosure chain, the Ezekiel scroll-eating pattern with deliberate modifications, and the chronos termination matching Daniel's sealed time content. The sweet-then-bitter experience structurally fits a prophetic message received with joy whose implications prove painful. The recommission ("thou must prophesy again") mandates a universal prophetic mission emerging from the opened book's content.

## Key Verses

> **Revelation 10:2** "And he had in his hand a little book open: and he set his right foot upon the sea, and his left foot on the earth."

> **Revelation 10:6** "And sware by him that liveth for ever and ever, who created heaven, and the things that therein are, and the earth, and the things that therein are, and the sea, and the things which are therein, that there should be time no longer."

> **Daniel 12:7** "And I heard the man clothed in linen, which was upon the waters of the river, when he held up his right hand and his left hand unto heaven, and sware by him that liveth for ever that it shall be for a time, times, and an half; and when he shall have accomplished to scatter the power of the holy people, all these things shall be finished."

> **Daniel 12:4** "But thou, O Daniel, shut up the words, and seal the book, even to the time of the end: many shall run to and fro, and knowledge shall be increased."

> **Revelation 10:7** "But in the days of the voice of the seventh angel, when he shall begin to sound, the mystery of God should be finished, as he hath declared to his servants the prophets."

> **Revelation 10:10-11** "And I took the little book out of the angel's hand, and ate it up; and it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter. And he said unto me, Thou must prophesy again before many peoples, and nations, and tongues, and kings."

> **Revelation 5:1** "And I saw in the right hand of him that sat on the throne a book written within and on the backside, sealed with seven seals."

> **Revelation 22:10** "And he saith unto me, Seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book: for the time is at hand."

> **Daniel 8:26** "And the vision of the evening and the morning which was told is true: wherefore shut thou up the vision; for it shall be for many days."

## Analysis

### The Central Question: What Is the Little Book?

Revelation 10 introduces an object that appears nowhere else in Scripture: a *biblaridion eneogmenon* — a little book in an open state. The word *biblaridion* (G974) occurs only four times, all in this chapter (Rev 10:2, 8, 9, 10). It is a double diminutive: *biblos* → *biblion* → *biblaridion*. The diminutive form suggests something smaller than a full scroll — a portion or extract.

The key grammatical feature is *eneogmenon*, a perfect passive participle of *anoigo* (G455). The canonical grammar reference for this construction (V-RPP) identifies it as describing a settled state resulting from a prior completed action. The paradigmatic example is Rev 5:6 where the Lamb stands *esphagmenon* — carrying the enduring condition of having-been-slain. Applied here: the little book carries the enduring condition of having-been-opened. The opening act is past. The open state persists throughout the chapter.

The passive voice means the book was opened by someone other than the angel who holds it. The angel displays it; he did not unseal it. This points back to the seal-breaking sequence of Revelation 5-8, where the Lamb progressively opens the seven-sealed scroll.

To identify this book, we need to ask: what book in Scripture was sealed and then opened? Is there one that matches?

### Evidence Line 1: The Sealed-to-Opened Vocabulary Arc

The series has traced a six-station vocabulary progression from maximum concealment to permanent disclosure (sv-11):

1. **Dan 8:26** — *satham* (H5640): "shut up the vision." First sealing command.
2. **Dan 12:4** — *satham* + *chatham* (H2856): "shut up the words, and seal the book." Maximum Hebrew sealing — both verbs combined.
3. **Rev 5:1** — *katasphragizo* (G2696): "sealed with seven seals." Maximum Greek sealing — a hapax legomenon with an intensive *kata-* prefix. Perfect passive participle (*katesphragismenon*), same construction as *eneogmenon*.
4. **Rev 10:2** — *eneogmenon*: permanently opened state. Perfect passive participle.
5. **Rev 22:10** — *me sphragises*: "seal NOT the prophecy." Prohibition of re-sealing.

The arc moves from Hebrew sealing verbs through Greek sealing verbs and then reverses toward opening and prohibition. The extremes use matching grammar: *katesphragismenon* (V-RPP, permanently sealed) becomes *eneogmenon* (V-RPP, permanently opened). One settled state replaced by another.

No other biblical book produces this pattern. Isaiah 8:16 seals teaching among disciples — preservation, not time-bound concealment. Isaiah 29:11-12 describes a sealed book that people cannot read due to spiritual blindness — no unsealing mechanism, no temporal limit, no "time of the end" expiration. Only Daniel's prophecy is sealed by divine command with a built-in expiration ("until the time of the end," Dan 12:4) and a promise that the sealing will produce understanding when it opens ("knowledge shall be increased," Dan 12:4). The vocabulary arc connects this specific sealed book to Revelation's opened one.

### Evidence Line 2: The Oath Scene Replication and Inversion

This is the strongest single evidence line. Daniel 12:5-7 and Revelation 10:5-6 share a five-element structural framework:

**Element 1 — The figure's position.** Dan 12:6: the man clothed in linen is "upon the waters of the river" (*mimma'al le-meymey ha-ye'or*). Rev 10:2, 5: the angel stands "upon the sea and upon the earth." Both figures occupy a position of authority above water. The scope expands: river → sea and earth.

**Element 2 — The hand-raising gesture.** Dan 12:7: "he held up his right hand and his left hand unto heaven" (*wayyarem yemino u-smolo el ha-shamayim*). Rev 10:5: "lifted up his hand to heaven." Daniel's figure raises BOTH hands; Revelation's angel raises the RIGHT hand only. The double hand-raising in Daniel intensifies the oath (cf. Deut 32:40 where God lifts a single hand in oath). Revelation returns to the single-hand form.

**Element 3 — The oath verb.** Dan 12:7: *wayyishaba* (Niphal Wayyiqtol 3ms of *shaba*, H7650) — "he swore." Rev 10:6: *omosen* (aorist active of *omnyo*, G3660) — "he swore." The shaba → omnyo bridge has 143 LXX co-occurrences, making these near-equivalent terms. The same oath act described in the same way.

**Element 4 — The oath mediator formula.** Dan 12:7: *be-chey ha-olam* — "by the living one of eternity." Rev 10:6: *en to zonti eis tous aionas ton aionon* — "by the one living unto the ages of the ages." Same prepositional structure. Same substantival participle identifying the eternal God. The Greek is a direct rendering of the Hebrew. Both angels swear by the same God using the same formula.

**Element 5 — The oath content.** Here the inversion occurs. Dan 12:7: *le-moed moedim va-chetsi* — "for a time, times, and a half." This announces a DURATION — 3½ prophetic time units. Rev 10:6: *chronos ouketi estai* — "time shall be no longer." This announces a TERMINATION — the cessation of measured time periods. Same frame, opposite content. The oath that once measured how long the waiting would last now declares that the waiting is over.

Both scenes also share a terminus formula. Dan 12:7: "all these things shall be finished" (*tikhleynah kol-elleh*). Rev 10:7: "the mystery of God should be finished" (*etelesthe to mysterion tou theou*). Both use "finished" as the endpoint — Daniel for the wonders, Revelation for the mystery.

The probability of five structural elements matching by coincidence — with one deliberate inversion — is negligible. This is intentional literary replication. And if Revelation 10's oath scene deliberately replicates and inverts Daniel 12's oath scene, then the book in the angel's hand must be the book that was sealed in Daniel. The oath identifies the book.

### Evidence Line 3: The raz-to-mysterion Disclosure Chain

Daniel 2:28 introduces the concept: "there is a God in heaven that revealeth secrets" (*raz*). The Aramaic word *raz* (H7328) occurs 9 times, all in Daniel. The translation relationship (TR-raz-mysterion) documents that every LXX occurrence of *raz* is rendered *mysterion* (G3466). They are loan-equivalents.

The chain moves through Scripture: Dan 2:28 (*raz* revealed) → Rom 16:25-26 (*mysterion* kept secret, now manifest) → Eph 3:3-5 (*mysterion* of Christ now revealed to apostles) → Col 1:26 (*mysterion* hidden from ages, now manifest) → 1 Pet 1:10-12 (prophets searched for the timing) → Rev 10:7 (*mysterion* of God FINISHED).

The verb in Rev 10:7 is *etelesthe* — aorist passive of *teleo*. This is a proleptic aorist: a future event described as already completed, expressing divine certainty. The root *telos* (G5056) connects to *tetelestai* ("it is finished") at the cross (John 19:30). The mystery that began with Daniel's revelation reaches the same definitive completion as Christ's redemptive work.

Amos 3:7 provides the hermeneutical key: "Surely the Lord GOD will do nothing, but he revealeth his secret unto his servants the prophets." Rev 10:7 echoes the exact formula: "as he hath declared to his servants the prophets" (*eueggelisen tois heautou doulois tois prophetais*). The mystery was declared to the prophets piece by piece. At the seventh trumpet, it is finished — not ended abruptly but brought to its intended completion (*telos*).

This chain links Daniel's revealed *raz* to Revelation's finished *mysterion*. The opened book is the vehicle by which the mystery reaches completion.

### Evidence Line 4: The Ezekiel Pattern Modified

Revelation 10:8-11 consciously reworks Ezekiel 2:8-3:14. Both follow the same three-step sequence: eat the scroll, experience bitterness, receive a prophetic commission. But every element is modified.

**The sweetness is the same.** Ezek 3:3: "it was in my mouth as honey for sweetness." Rev 10:10: "it was in my mouth sweet as honey." The simile is preserved nearly verbatim. God's word, even difficult word, tastes sweet when first received. The psalmist expressed the same truth: "How sweet are thy words unto my taste! yea, sweeter than honey to my mouth!" (Psa 119:103).

**The bitterness is relocated.** In Ezekiel, sweetness happens during eating (3:3) and bitterness comes afterward as Ezekiel's emotional response to his mission: "I went in bitterness, in the heat of my spirit" (3:14). The bitterness is the prophet's personal reaction. In Revelation, the angel PREDICTS the bitterness before John eats: "it shall make thy belly bitter" (10:9, *pikranei*, future active — the scroll is the grammatical agent). And the experience confirms it: "my belly was bitter" (10:10, *epikranthe*, aorist passive — the belly is acted upon). The bitterness is not an emotional aftermath. It is intrinsic to the content. The scroll itself causes it.

This is a structural intensification. The modification means the prophetic content carries both sweetness and bitterness within itself — receiving it brings joy, but understanding its full implications brings pain. The sweet-bitter duality is built into the nature of the message, not just the prophet's experience of delivering it.

**The commission scope expands.** Ezek 3:4: "go, get thee unto the house of Israel." The scope is narrow — one nation. Rev 10:11: "thou must prophesy again before many peoples, and nations, and tongues, and kings." The scope is universal. And the four-fold audience formula (peoples, nations, tongues, kings) draws from Daniel's own vocabulary: Dan 7:14 uses "all people, nations, and languages" in the same kind of list.

Rev 10:11 also uniquely substitutes "kings" (*basileusin*) for "kindreds/tribes" (*phylai*) found in all other Revelation audience lists (5:9; 7:9; 11:9; 13:7; 14:6; 17:15). This substitution may signal a political dimension to the recommissioned prophecy — the message addresses not just peoples in general but the powers that rule them.

**The recommission verb is unique.** *Prophetuesai* (V-AAN) is the ONLY aorist infinitive of *propheteuo* in the entire NT. All other infinitival uses are present tense (1 Cor 14:31, 14:39), describing ongoing activity. The aorist treats the recommission as a bounded, complete task — a specific prophetic mission, not general ongoing prophecy. Combined with *dei* (divine necessity: "thou MUST") and *palin* ("again" — presupposing prior activity that stopped), the grammar describes a divinely mandated, specific, renewed prophetic task directed at the whole world.

### Evidence Line 5: Chronos and the Time Content

The oath's content — *chronos ouketi estai* — must be interpreted within Revelation's usage pattern for *chronos* (G5550). From the canonical word study: every Revelation use of *chronos* denotes a measured duration or allotted span. Rev 2:21 — "space" to repent (allotted duration). Rev 6:11 — "a little season" (brief remaining period). Rev 20:3 — "a little season" (allotted span). None of these uses describe time-as-such or cosmic time. They all describe bounded periods.

When Rev 10:6 declares *chronos ouketi estai*, the contextual evidence points to the cessation of prophetic time periods — the kind of measured durations that Daniel's prophecy contained (the 2300 erev boqer, the 1260, the 1290, the 1335). The grammar supports this: *chronos* is anarthrous (qualitative — "time" as a category, not "the time"), and *ouketi* ("no longer") presupposes that this category previously existed. Something that existed (prophetic time periods) will cease to exist.

This aligns precisely with what Daniel's sealed prophecy contained. The sealed content, as established in sv-10 and sv-11, was the time element — the 2300 erev boqer, a number expressed in a deliberately unusual unit without a starting point. If the oath declares the end of prophetic time periods, and the book in the angel's hand is the source of those time periods, then the time content links the book to Daniel.

### The Sweet-Bitter Experience

The sweet-then-bitter structure describes a two-phase experience built into the prophetic content itself. Receiving the opened book's message produces initial joy (sweet as honey); understanding its full implications produces pain (belly was bitter).

What kind of prophetic content would produce this specific pattern? The text does not name the historical event. But the structure fits any prophetic message received with great expectation that proves more complex or painful than anticipated. The key textual feature is that the bitterness does not cancel the commission — it leads to a broader one. "Thou must prophesy AGAIN" follows the bitterness. The disappointment, whatever its specific cause, becomes the occasion for an expanded mission.

The modification from Ezekiel's pattern matters here. Ezekiel's bitterness was about the difficulty of his mission to rebellious Israel. John's bitterness is about the content of the message itself. The prophetic word contains something that is simultaneously good news (sweet) and deeply unsettling (bitter). This dual nature produces a sequence: receive with joy, then grapple with the harder implications, then go forward with a broader commission.

### The Recommission and the Maskilim

Rev 10:11's "thou must prophesy again" closes the chapter but opens a new phase. The *palin* ("again") presupposes that prophetic activity related to this content occurred before and then stopped. The recommission restarts it.

The connection to Daniel's maskilim (Dan 12:3, 10) operates through the *tsadaq* root. Dan 12:3: the maskilim "turn many to righteousness" (*matsdiqey*, Hiphil participle of *tsadaq*). Dan 8:14: the sanctuary is vindicated (*nitsdaq*, Niphal of *tsadaq*). Same root, complementary roles. The maskilim participate in vindication by helping the deceived understand which system was real. Rev 10:11's universal recommission provides the vehicle: prophesying "before many peoples, nations, tongues, and kings" is how the maskilim's understanding reaches the world.

The *dei* (divine necessity) that governs the recommission mirrors the *dei* that governs Revelation's entire prophetic framework. Rev 1:1: "things which must (*dei*) shortly come to pass." Rev 4:1: "things which must (*dei*) be hereafter." Rev 10:11: "thou must (*dei*) prophesy again." The same divine compulsion that drives the unfolding of prophetic events drives the proclamation of the prophetic message.

### The Theophanic Messenger

The angel of Rev 10:1 carries four theophanic markers: clothed with a cloud (Exo 13:21), rainbow on his head (Eze 1:28; Rev 4:3), face like the sun (Rev 1:16), feet like pillars of fire (Exo 13:21-22). These markers signal divine authority without necessarily identifying the angel as Christ himself. The description parallels the "man clothed in linen" of Dan 10:5-6 and 12:5-7 — the same figure who swore the oath in Daniel's scene. Whether this is a Christophany or a high-ranking angel operating with divine authority, the effect is the same: the opened book and the oath come with the full weight of heaven.

### The Seven Thunders as Counter-Evidence?

Rev 10:3-4 introduces a complication: content is sealed within a chapter about unsealing. The seven thunders speak, John starts to write, and a heavenly voice commands: *sphragison* — "seal up!" (aorist imperative, a single decisive act). This is the only sealing command in Revelation.

This does not undermine the unsealing of Daniel's prophecy. It demonstrates that God retains sovereign control over revelation even in the age of disclosure. Not everything is for immediate publication. The thunders' content remains sealed — an open question the text does not resolve. But the sealing of the thunders is a different act from the sealing of Daniel's prophecy: the thunders are sealed at the point of revelation (John hears but cannot record); Daniel's prophecy was sealed after revelation (Daniel received and recorded it, then was told to seal it for later generations).

### Synthesis: Five Lines Converge

The identification of the little book as Daniel's sealed prophecy rests on converging evidence, not any single proof:

1. The vocabulary arc traces exclusively through Daniel's sealing verbs into Revelation's opening verbs, using matching grammatical forms.
2. The oath scene replicates five structural elements of Dan 12:5-7 with one decisive content inversion, linked by the strongest oath-verb lexical bridge in the LXX.
3. The raz-mysterion chain begins in Daniel and terminates in Rev 10:7.
4. The Ezekiel scroll-eating pattern is consciously reworked with structural intensifications.
5. The *chronos* termination matches the time content that Daniel's prophecy contained.

No other biblical book satisfies all five. Isaiah's sealed content has no unsealing mechanism. The Pentateuch is never described as sealed. The Psalms are never sealed. Only Daniel's prophecy is sealed by divine command, given a temporal expiration, and connected through vocabulary, structure, and theme to Revelation 10's opened book.

## Word Studies

**G974 biblaridion** — Double diminutive (biblos → biblion → biblaridion). 4 total NT occurrences. Three clearly use G974 (Rev 10:2, 9, 10); at v. 8 the heavenly voice shifts to *biblion* (G975) in the critical text, though some textual traditions (e.g., Textus Receptus) read *biblaridion* there as well. The diminutive form signals a smaller document — a portion of a larger scroll. The shift to *biblion* in v. 8 may identify the little book as a section of the larger sealed scroll from Rev 5:1. (Canonical library entry: G974-bibliaridion.md)

**G455 anoigo** — "To open." 78 tokens; Revelation has the highest concentration (25 occurrences). The perfect passive participle cluster in Revelation describes settled open states: Rev 3:8 (open door), 4:1 (heaven opened), 10:2 and 10:8 (little book open), 19:11 (heaven opened). The Hebrew source *pathach* (H6605) has 85 LXX co-occurrences — one of the strongest translation bridges. (Canonical library entry: G455-anoigo.md)

**G3660 omnyo** — "To swear." 21 unique verses. The Greek oath verb bridging to *shaba* (H7650) with 143 co-occurrences. Rev 10:6 is the only Revelation occurrence — a unique angelic/eschatological oath. The aorist (*omosen*) treats the oath as a completed, decisive act. (Canonical library entry: G3660-omnyo.md)

**H7650 shaba** — "To swear." 175 unique verses. Niphal reflexive ("bind oneself by oath") in Dan 12:7. The etymological link to *sheba* (H7651, "seven") connects oath-making to the covenant number. Dan 12:7's *wayyishaba* is a divine oath of decree. (Canonical library entry: H7650-shaba.md)

**G5550 chronos** — "Time" in the sense of measured duration. Distinguished from *kairos* (appointed moment), *aion* (age), and *hora* (hour). All four Revelation uses denote bounded periods. The anarthrous use in Rev 10:6 is qualitative: "time/delay as a concept." (Canonical library entry: G5550-chronos.md)

**G1163 dei** — "It is necessary" (divine compulsion). Rev 10:11 uses *dei* + accusative subject + aorist infinitive. The necessity is currently operative (present tense). Combined with *palin* ("again"), it mandates a renewed prophetic mission. (Canonical library entry: G1163-dei.md)

**G3466 mysterion** — "Mystery." From *myo* (to shut the mouth). Loan-equivalent of Aramaic *raz* (H7328), with every Daniel LXX occurrence rendering *raz* as *mysterion*. Rev 10:7's *etelesthe* (proleptic aorist passive) describes the mystery's completion as certain. (Canonical library entry: G3466-mysterion.md)

## Difficult Passages

### The Seven Thunders (Rev 10:3-4)
The sealing of the thunders' content within a chapter about unsealing is the primary tension in the passage. If God's program moves from sealed to unsealed, why introduce new sealed content? The text provides no resolution. We know the thunders spoke articulate content (their "voices"), that John understood it (he "was about to write"), and that a heavenly voice prohibited its recording. The purpose of the sealing — protection, timing, or something else — is not stated. This is a genuine lacuna in the available evidence. Any claim about what the thunders said goes beyond the text.

### "Time No Longer" — Interpretive Range
The phrase *chronos ouketi estai* has three possible meanings: (a) prophetic time periods cease, (b) no more delay in fulfilling God's purposes, (c) time itself ends. Interpretation (c) faces the strongest objection: events continue to unfold in chronological time after the seventh trumpet (Rev 11:15ff). Interpretations (a) and (b) are not mutually exclusive — the end of prophetic time periods IS the end of delay. The contextual evidence (all Revelation *chronos* uses denote bounded durations; the connection to v. 7's mystery finishing at the seventh trumpet) favors (a), but the text does not provide an explicit definition, leaving room for exegetical debate.

### The Identity of the Angel
Rev 10:1's theophanic description (cloud, rainbow, sun-face, fire-feet) raises the question of whether this is Christ himself or an angel acting with divine authority. The text calls the figure *allon angelon ischyron* — "another mighty angel" — distinguishing him from the Lamb of Rev 5. But the theophanic markers overlap with Christ's description in Rev 1:15-16. The text does not resolve this. The analysis proceeds on the basis that the angel carries divine authority regardless of his precise identity, since the message and the book he carries are what the passage is about.

### Whether the Identification Requires the Rev 5 Connection
The identification of the little book as Daniel's sealed prophecy can stand on the Dan 12/Rev 10 oath parallel alone. But if the *biblion*/*biblaridion* shift in Rev 10:8 is merely stylistic, then the link between the little book and the seven-sealed scroll of Rev 5 weakens. The analysis treats the shift as likely deliberate (the heavenly voice uses the same word as Rev 5:1 with the same perfect passive participle), but this connection is supporting evidence, not the primary basis.

## Conclusion

The little book open in the angel's hand in Revelation 10 is Daniel's sealed prophecy, identified through five converging lines of textual evidence.

The vocabulary arc traces from *satham/chatham* (Dan 8:26, 12:4) through *katasphragizo* (Rev 5:1, maximum sealing) to *eneogmenon* (Rev 10:2, permanently opened) to *me sphragises* (Rev 22:10, prohibition of re-sealing). No other biblical book follows this sealed-then-unsealed trajectory.

The oath scene in Rev 10:5-6 replicates five structural elements of Dan 12:5-7 — figure above water, hands raised to heaven, oath by the eternal God, oath formula, terminus declaration — with one decisive content inversion: duration becomes termination. The lexical bridge between the oath verbs (*shaba* → *omnyo*, 143 LXX co-occurrences) and the identical mediator formula (*be-chey ha-olam* / *en to zonti*) confirm intentional literary dependence. This inversion identifies the book: what Daniel's oath measured, Revelation's oath terminates.

The *raz*-to-*mysterion* chain begins at Dan 2:28 and finishes at Rev 10:7. The mystery declared to the prophets reaches completion (*etelesthe*, the same *teleo* root as Christ's cross-cry) when the seventh angel sounds.

The sweet-then-bitter experience (Rev 10:9-10) modifies Ezekiel's prototype (Ezek 2:8-3:14) by making the bitterness intrinsic to the content rather than an emotional aftermath. This indicates prophetic truth that produces both joy in reception and pain in its fuller implications. The recommission that follows ("thou must prophesy again before many peoples, nations, tongues, and kings," Rev 10:11) uses Danielic audience vocabulary (Dan 7:14) and the only aorist infinitive of *propheteuo* in the NT, treating the mandate as a bounded, specific, divinely compelled task.

The *chronos* that "shall be no longer" (Rev 10:6) fits the termination of prophetic time periods — the kind of content Daniel's sealed prophecy contained. The 2300 erev boqer, the 1290, and the 1335 all represent measured *chronos*-type durations. Their cessation matches the angel's declaration.

What remains uncertain: the seven thunders' content, the exact nature of the *biblion*/*biblaridion* shift, and the precise identity of the theophanic messenger. These open questions affect secondary details, not the central identification.

Revelation 10 is the pivot point where Daniel's sealed prophecy crosses from concealment to disclosure. The book sealed "until the time of the end" (Dan 12:4) now stands permanently open (*eneogmenon*) in the angel's hand. The oath that measured the duration of oppression now declares that the waiting is over. And the recommission sends the opened content to the whole world.
