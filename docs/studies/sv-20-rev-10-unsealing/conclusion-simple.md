# Revelation 10 — What Is the Little Book Opened?
## A Plain-English Summary

### The Scene

Picture an enormous angel — face blazing like the sun, feet like columns of fire, one foot planted on the sea and the other on dry land — holding a small open book in his hand. That image opens Revelation 10, and it raises one immediate question: what is that book, and why does it matter that it is *open*?

This study set out to answer that question. The short answer: the little open book is the same prophecy that the angel told Daniel to *seal up* centuries earlier. And its being open changes everything.

---

### Why a "Little" Book?

The Greek word used here — *biblaridion* — is a double diminutive, like saying "booklet" when you could say "book." It appears only four times in the entire New Testament, all in this one chapter. It is smaller than a full scroll. Think of it as a portion, an extract — not everything, but something specific.

The most important word attached to it is translated "open" — but the original Greek carries a precise meaning: *permanently open, as a result of something that already happened*. The book was opened in the past. It stays open. The angel is not opening it before our eyes. He is displaying something that has already been unsealed.

> **Revelation 10:2** "And he had in his hand a little book open: and he set his right foot upon the sea, and his left foot on the earth."

The passive form of the verb also tells us the angel did not open the book himself. Someone else opened it. That points back to earlier chapters in Revelation, where the Lamb breaks the seals on a great sealed scroll (Revelation 5–8). The little open book is the result of that seal-breaking.

---

### What Was Sealed in the First Place?

To know what is now open, you have to find what was shut. The Bible has only one prophecy that was sealed by divine command, given a specific expiration date, and promised to produce understanding when it finally opened.

That is the book of Daniel.

> **Daniel 12:4** "But thou, O Daniel, shut up the words, and seal the book, even to the time of the end: many shall run to and fro, and knowledge shall be increased."

Daniel was told twice to seal his vision — the Hebrew uses two different sealing words stacked together, the biblical equivalent of a maximum-security vault. Then, in Revelation, the Greek word for *opening* is used with the same grammatical form — a settled, completed state — that was used for the *sealing*. One permanent condition (sealed) is replaced by another permanent condition (open).

No other book in the Bible follows this pattern. Isaiah's sealed teaching was sealed for preservation, not concealment with a future unlocking. The Psalms were never sealed. Only Daniel's prophecy was sealed by God himself with the explicit promise that it would eventually be understood.

---

### The Oath That Locks It In

The most striking evidence is a scene that Revelation 10 borrows almost word-for-word from Daniel 12 — then flips.

In Daniel 12, a figure standing above a river raises both hands to heaven and swears by the eternal God:

> **Daniel 12:7** "...he held up his right hand and his left hand unto heaven, and sware by him that liveth for ever that it shall be for a time, times, and an half..."

That oath was about *duration* — how long a specific period of suffering would last. It gave a measurement: three and a half time units.

Now look at Revelation 10. The same scene: a figure above water, hand raised to heaven, swearing by the same eternal God. But the oath content is inverted:

> **Revelation 10:6** "And sware by him that liveth for ever and ever...that there should be time no longer."

Same figure. Same posture. Same God. Same oath formula. But where Daniel measured *how long*, Revelation declares *no longer*. The waiting period that Daniel's prophecy measured is now declared over.

Five separate structural elements match between the two scenes. The probability that this is coincidence is essentially zero. It is a deliberate literary echo — and the echo identifies the book. The oath that measured the duration of Daniel's sealed time period now announces that the measuring is done.

---

### The Mystery That Reaches Its End

There is a word that runs like a thread from Daniel to Revelation: in Aramaic it is *raz* (secret, mystery); in Greek it is *mysterion*. Every time the Greek Old Testament translated Daniel's *raz*, it used *mysterion*.

The chain goes like this: Daniel 2 introduces the *raz* — God reveals secrets through his prophets. Paul writes about the *mysterion* hidden for ages but now beginning to be revealed. And then:

> **Revelation 10:7** "But in the days of the voice of the seventh angel, when he shall begin to sound, the mystery of God should be finished, as he hath declared to his servants the prophets."

"Finished" here is the same root word as what Jesus said on the cross: *tetelestai* — "It is finished." The mystery that began with Daniel's revealed secret reaches the same decisive completion. The little open book is the vehicle that carries this mystery from its sealed beginning to its declared end.

---

### Eating the Book: Sweet, Then Bitter

The angel tells John to take the book and eat it. This sounds strange, but it follows a well-known pattern from Ezekiel, where the prophet also ate a scroll and found it sweet as honey. Eating a scroll in this tradition means fully absorbing a prophetic message — making it part of yourself before you deliver it.

The difference is in the bitter part.

> **Revelation 10:10-11** "And I took the little book out of the angel's hand, and ate it up; and it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter. And he said unto me, Thou must prophesy again before many peoples, and nations, and tongues, and kings."

In Ezekiel, the bitterness came from the prophet's personal anguish at delivering a hard message. Here, the bitterness is built into the message itself — the scroll *causes* it. The content contains both good news and hard news simultaneously. Receiving it brings joy. Sitting with its full implications brings pain.

This pattern fits a prophetic message received with great expectation whose fuller meaning turns out to be more difficult than anticipated. The bitterness does not cancel the mission. Instead, it expands it. The recommission that follows is the widest possible scope: all peoples, all nations, all languages, and kings.

---

### "You Must Prophesy Again"

The final verse of the chapter is a commission, and the grammar is unusual. "Thou must prophesy *again*" — that word *again* implies prophetic activity that happened before and stopped. Now it restarts. And the word translated "must" (*dei*) carries the same weight of divine necessity as the phrase "these things must come to pass" in Revelation 1:1.

The commission is not vague ongoing activity. The Greek verb form used here — an aorist infinitive — treats this as a specific, bounded task. Go. Prophesy. Before everyone.

The four-part audience (peoples, nations, tongues, kings) echoes Daniel 7:14, where the same kind of list describes the scope of God's ultimate kingdom. The opened book produces a mission directed at the entire world — including the people who hold political power.

---

### What About the Seven Thunders?

One puzzle remains inside the chapter itself. The great angel roars, and seven thunders respond. John hears them speak and starts to write — then a heavenly voice stops him: "Seal up those things which the seven thunders uttered, and write them not."

This is the only sealing command in all of Revelation. It does not contradict the unsealing of Daniel's prophecy. It shows that even in an age of disclosure, God retains control over what is revealed and when. The thunder content stays hidden — not because revelation has ended, but because God's timing governs what is published. We do not know what the thunders said. Any answer to that question goes beyond what the text provides.

---

### Putting It Together

Five separate threads all arrive at the same conclusion: the little open book is Daniel's sealed prophecy.

- The vocabulary traces from Daniel's sealing words through Revelation's opening words, using identical grammatical forms.
- The oath scene in Revelation 10 replicates Daniel 12 element by element, then inverts the content — duration becomes termination.
- The *raz*/*mysterion* chain runs from Daniel 2 to Revelation 10:7, where it is declared finished.
- The scroll-eating scene reworks Ezekiel's pattern, with the bitterness intensified and built into the content itself.
- The "time no longer" declaration fits the end of the kind of measured prophetic time periods that Daniel's prophecy contained.

No other book in Scripture satisfies all five. Daniel's prophecy is the only one sealed by God's command, set to expire "at the time of the end," and promised to produce understanding when it opens. Revelation 10 is where it opens.

> **Revelation 22:10** "And he saith unto me, Seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book: for the time is at hand."

What Daniel shut, God opens. What was measured is now declared finished. What was received with joy must now be proclaimed — with full awareness of its harder implications — to the whole world.

---

### What This Means for the Series

This study is a pivot point. The series has been tracing Daniel's prophecy across time: what was sealed, what the time periods mean, what the sanctuary vision contains. Revelation 10 is where that sealed content crosses into permanent disclosure. Everything that follows in the series — the sounding of the seventh trumpet, the completing of the mystery, the proclamation to all nations — flows from this moment.

The little open book is not a minor image. It is the hinge. The sealing of Daniel's prophecy was always meant to end. Revelation 10 is where that ending is declared, displayed, and made the basis for a worldwide mission.
