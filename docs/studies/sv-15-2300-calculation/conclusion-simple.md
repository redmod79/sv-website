# When Do the 2300 Days End?
## A Plain-English Summary

The book of Daniel contains one of the most precisely calculated prophecies in the Bible. In chapter 8, an angel tells Daniel that a period of "two thousand and three hundred days" will pass before something called the sanctuary is set right. The question this study tackles is straightforward: when does that period end, and how do we know?

The answer, built from careful reading of the original Hebrew, works out to 1844 AD. Here is how.

---

## One Word Changes Everything

The calculation depends on linking two prophecies that appear in different chapters. Daniel 8 gives the 2300-day period. Daniel 9 gives a separate "seventy weeks" prophecy. The question is whether these two prophecies share the same starting point.

The answer is hidden in a single Hebrew word.

Daniel 9:24 begins: "Seventy weeks are *determined* upon thy people." The English word "determined" sounds like a decree — as if God had decided something. But the Hebrew word is *chathak*, and it does not mean "decree." It means to *cut off*, to sever a piece from a larger whole.

> **Daniel 9:24** "Seventy weeks are determined upon thy people and upon thy holy city..."

This word appears exactly once in the entire Hebrew Bible — right here. When a biblical writer uses a unique word instead of the ordinary word for "decree" (which Daniel himself uses just two verses later), the choice is deliberate. The seventy weeks are not merely decreed. They are *cut* from something larger. And the only larger time period in the surrounding chapters is the 2300 days of Daniel 8:14.

This means the seventy weeks are a portion sliced out of the 2300 days. They must start at the same moment.

---

## When Does the Clock Start?

Daniel 9:25 identifies the starting point: "from the going forth of the commandment to restore and to build Jerusalem."

History records several decrees related to Jerusalem. The one that fits is found in Ezra chapter 7 — the decree of the Persian king Artaxerxes in 457 BC. This decree did something the others did not: it restored judicial self-governance to the Jewish people, giving them the authority to appoint judges and enforce laws. It was not just a construction permit. It was a restoration of national sovereignty.

> **Mark 1:15** "The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand: repent ye, and believe the gospel."

The seventy-weeks prophecy provides its own verification. Sixty-nine of those seventy weeks (483 years) were supposed to lead to the appearance of the Messiah. Starting from 457 BC and counting 483 years brings you to AD 27 — the very year Jesus began his public ministry. When he said "the time is fulfilled," he was announcing that the prophetic clock had hit its mark. History confirms the date from a completely independent source: Luke 3:1 records that John the Baptist began his ministry in the 15th year of the Roman emperor Tiberius, which historians place in AD 27.

The starting point works. The method works. The calculation can be trusted.

---

## The Math

Once the starting point (457 BC) and the time unit (one prophetic day equals one literal year) are established, the arithmetic is simple.

457 BC + 2300 years = 1844 AD.

There is a small adjustment required because our calendar has no "year zero" — it jumps directly from 1 BC to AD 1. Accounting for this, the calculation still lands in the autumn of 1844.

A second method reaches the same answer from the other end. The seventy weeks (490 years) end in AD 34. Subtract those 490 years from the full 2300, and 1810 years remain. AD 34 + 1810 = autumn 1844.

Two routes. One destination.

---

## A Second Prophecy Arrives at the Same Year

Daniel 12 introduces another time period — 1335 days — that runs on a completely different track. It begins from a different historical event (the removal of the "daily," a symbol of God's appointed worship system) and counts forward 1335 years.

> **Daniel 12:12** "Blessed is he that waiteth, and cometh to the thousand three hundred and five and thirty days."

Starting from 508 AD, the 1335 years reach 1843 — within a year of 1844, with the small difference explained by calendar mechanics and the precision of the historical starting point.

The significance here is not just the number. It is that two prophecies, beginning from entirely different events in history, counting entirely different lengths, arrive at essentially the same year. One starts with a Persian king's decree about Jerusalem. The other starts with the removal of God's worship system. They are counting different things. They arrive together.

This is the mathematical equivalent of two independent witnesses telling the same story.

---

## What Happens at the End?

Daniel 8:14 describes what occurs when the 2300 days conclude:

> **Daniel 8:14** "And he said unto me, Unto two thousand and three hundred days; then shall the sanctuary be cleansed."

The English word "cleansed" comes from a translation error that has persisted for centuries. The Hebrew word is *nitsdaq*, which comes from the root *tsadaq* — meaning to be righteous, to be vindicated, to receive a favorable verdict. The word for actual ritual cleansing (*taher*) appears throughout Leviticus, but Daniel chose a different word entirely.

This is not a scrubbing. It is a verdict. The sanctuary is declared right.

The scene in Daniel 7 matches this precisely:

> **Daniel 7:9-10** "I beheld till the thrones were cast down, and the Ancient of days did sit... the judgment was set, and the books were opened."

A courtroom. A judge. Records being examined. This is a pre-advent judgment — it happens before the second coming, because Daniel 7:22 shows the judgment completing before the saints receive the kingdom.

Revelation picks up the same moment:

> **Revelation 14:7** "Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come."

The 2300 days end in 1844. What begins at that moment is a heavenly judgment — the opening of the books, the examining of records, the vindication of God's purposes.

---

## The Blessing on the 1335

Of all the time prophecies in Daniel — the 1260, the 1290, the 2300, the 1335 — only one receives a blessing. Only the 1335.

> **Daniel 12:12** "Blessed is he that waiteth, and cometh to the thousand three hundred and five and thirty days."

The word translated "waiteth" in Hebrew is not passive sitting around. It means to cling, to hold fast, to adhere deliberately over a long period. The people who reach this moment are those who have actively pressed toward it — who understood what was coming and did not let go.

The closest parallel in the Old Testament is Isaiah 30:18:

> "Blessed are all they that wait for him... the LORD is a God of judgment."

The blessing and the judgment are inseparable. To reach the 1335 is to arrive at the moment when God acts as judge. Those who have been holding on through centuries of persecution and obscurity finally see what they were waiting for.

## What This Means for the Series

This study establishes the numerical backbone of the entire Sanctuary Vision series. Every subsequent question — what the judgment actually involves, what it means for the sanctuary to be "vindicated," who the people are that understand these things — depends on this foundation being in place.

The calculation itself is not complicated once the pieces are assembled: a single Hebrew word (*chathak*) links two prophecies to the same starting point; the 70-weeks prophecy verifies the method and the date; the 1335 independently converges on the same year; and the terminus event is a heavenly judgment, not a physical cleansing.

Studies later in the series (particularly sv-17 and sv-18) will examine what this judgment looks like in practice — the antitypical Day of Atonement, the meaning of vindication, and the people Daniel calls the *maskilim* (those who understand). This study plants the stake in the ground: the 2300 days end in 1844, and what begins there is the most significant event in the prophetic calendar.
