# What Does Freedom Have to Do with Judgment?
## A Plain-English Summary

The ancient Israelite calendar has a surprise built into it that most readers walk right past. In the seventh month of every fiftieth year, the Jubilee trumpet sounded — debts cancelled, slaves freed, land returned to its original families. A total economic and social reset.

But here is the part people miss: the Jubilee was proclaimed *on* the Day of Atonement. Not after it. Not the next week. On the same day the high priest completed the cleansing ritual inside the sanctuary. The text is explicit about this.

> **Leviticus 25:9-10** "Then shalt thou cause the trumpet of the jubile to sound on the tenth day of the seventh month, in the day of atonement shall ye make the trumpet sound throughout all your land. And ye shall hallow the fiftieth year, and proclaim liberty throughout all the land unto all the inhabitants thereof."

Liberty was declared the moment cleansing was finished. That placement is not accidental. It tells you something about what the Bible thinks these two ideas have to do with each other.

---

## The Tishri Calendar: Four Stages, One Arc

Leviticus 23 places three observances in the seventh month (Tishri), and the Jubilee makes a fourth. Together they trace a single trajectory:

**Trumpets (Tishri 1)** — The alarm. Horns blow across the land. Israel is called to attention before God.

**Day of Atonement (Tishri 10)** — The judgment. Nine days later, the high priest cleanses the sanctuary. Every person must "afflict their soul" — fast, pray, genuinely participate. Those who refuse are cut off. No other festival carries this penalty.

**Jubilee (proclaimed on Tishri 10)** — The declaration. On the same day the cleansing is complete, the trumpet of liberty sounds. Three things happen: personal freedom is restored (*deror* — liberty), land goes back to its original family, and kinship ties are renewed. The Hebrew word *deror* carries the sense of unimpeded flow — removing whatever blocks free movement.

**Tabernacles (Tishri 15-22)** — The celebration. Five days later, the people take up palm branches and live in booths for a week. The text commands joy: "you shall rejoice before the LORD your God seven days." After the solemnity of judgment and the relief of liberty, rejoicing is the mandated response.

The arc reads: alarm, judgment, liberty, celebration. Each stage builds on what came before.

## Isaiah 61: From Liberty to Righteousness

Isaiah 61 compresses this entire calendar arc into a single chapter — and in the process, it builds a vocabulary chain that connects the Jubilee to the language of vindication.

It starts with the Jubilee word. The anointed speaker proclaims *deror* — "liberty to the captives" (v.1). This is the same word from Leviticus 25:10, and the phrase *qara deror* ("proclaim liberty") appears only seven times in the entire Old Testament. Six of those seven trace directly to the Jubilee formula.

Then the chapter introduces a binary — two outcomes from one event. Verse 2 announces "the acceptable year of the LORD" (*ratson* — a word from the sacrificial system meaning divine acceptance) and "the day of vengeance of our God" (*naqam* — judicial resolution, not rage). This is the same shape as the Day of Atonement: one occasion, two results. Participate and receive acceptance. Refuse and face judgment.

By verse 3, the recipients of this proclamation are called "oaks of righteousness" — *eiley hatsedeq*. That word *tsedeq* belongs to the same Hebrew root (*tsadaq*) as *nitsdaq* in Daniel 8:14, the verse that drives this entire study series. In just three verses, Isaiah has walked from Jubilee vocabulary to vindication vocabulary.

The chain keeps going. By verse 10, righteousness has become something you wear:

> **Isaiah 61:10** "He hath clothed me with the garments of salvation, he hath covered me with the robe of righteousness."

God does the dressing — the verb is causative. The recipient does not earn or create this garment. And by verse 11, this righteousness has gone public: God will cause it to "spring forth before all the nations." What began as personal liberty in verse 1 has become visible, universal vindication by the chapter's end.

## The Verb That Does Double Duty

One of the study's most concrete findings involves a single Hebrew verb: *avar* (H5674), used in its Hiphil (causative) stem.

In Leviticus 25:9, the Hiphil of *avar* causes the trumpet to "pass through" the land — the Jubilee announcement.

In Zechariah 3:4, the same Hiphil of *avar* causes iniquity to "pass from" the high priest — the cleansing declaration.

> **Zechariah 3:4** "Behold, I have caused thine iniquity to pass from thee, and I will clothe thee with change of raiment."

Same verb. Same grammatical form. Same causative function. The verb that announces liberty is the verb that removes sin. This is not a theological interpretation — it is a grammatical fact that any Hebrew concordance can confirm.

Zechariah 3 deepens the connection further. After the iniquity is removed, the priest receives a "pure turban" — and the word for "pure" is *tahor*, the exact result-word of the Day of Atonement in Leviticus 16:30: "that ye may be clean (*tahor*) from all your sins before the LORD." Day of Atonement vocabulary is sitting on the restored priest's head.

## Jeremiah 34: God Calls Liberty "The Right Thing"

A completely independent confirmation comes from Jeremiah. When the people of Jerusalem finally proclaimed *deror* — freeing their enslaved neighbors as the Jubilee law required — God responded:

> **Jeremiah 34:15** "And ye were now turned, and had done right in my sight, in proclaiming liberty every man to his neighbour."

God calls proclaiming *deror* "the right thing" (*yashar* — upright, straight, morally correct). What Isaiah builds through a chapter-long vocabulary chain — liberty connects to righteousness — Jeremiah states in one clause. A different prophet, a different century, the same connection.

And when the people later revoked that liberty and re-enslaved the freed workers, God's response was grimly ironic: "I proclaim a *deror* for you — to the sword, to the pestilence, and to the famine" (Jer 34:17). The same word that meant freedom now meant destruction. Refuse to give liberty, and liberty turns against you. The Day of Atonement binary — cleansed or cut off — appears even in the Jubilee vocabulary.

## The Garment Change

Two texts describe God dressing someone in new clothes after judgment:

In Zechariah 3:4-5, after the courtroom scene and the removal of iniquity, God clothes the high priest in festival garments and a pure turban. In Isaiah 61:10, after the liberty proclamation and the acceptance/judgment binary, God clothes the speaker in "garments of salvation" and a "robe of righteousness."

Both use the same causative verb for clothing — God is the one who dresses the recipient. Both occur after a judgment context. The garment names differ, but the pattern is identical: judgment resolves, then God re-clothes the person in garments that signify restored standing.

## What This Means for the Series

This study has identified a second vocabulary bridge connecting Day of Atonement language to vindication language — and it runs through the Jubilee.

The first bridge is Daniel 9:24, where the six purposes of the seventy weeks include both atonement (*kaphar* — DOA vocabulary) and "everlasting righteousness" (*tsedeq olamim* — vindication vocabulary). That bridge will get its own study later in the series.

The second bridge is Isaiah 61, where the Jubilee word *deror* — proclaimed on the Day of Atonement — connects through a traceable chain to *tsedeq* and *tsedaqah*, the very root behind *nitsdaq* in Daniel 8:14. The Hiphil-of-*avar* bridge (Lev 25:9 and Zech 3:4) supports the connection grammatically. Jeremiah 34:15 confirms it independently.

The series has now traced a progression: sin enters the sanctuary through the daily service, the Day of Atonement removes it, the DOA sorts people into cleansed or cut off, and the Jubilee declares the social reality of what cleansing accomplished — liberty, restoration, return. The vocabulary that describes this process leads directly to the vindication language of Daniel 8:14.

Cleansing produces liberty. Liberty is righteousness. And that righteousness, Isaiah says, will stand before all nations.
