# Does Hebrews Say Jesus Entered the Most Holy Place at His Ascension?
## A Plain-English Summary

The short answer is: **yes — but not in the way the question usually implies.** Understanding why requires clearing up a translation problem, a common misconception about "sitting down," and a piece of the Old Testament sanctuary story that most readers skip past.

---

## The Curtain Problem: One Greek Word, Two Hebrew Words

The book of Hebrews talks about Jesus entering "within the veil" (Hebrews 6:19). But which veil? The Old Testament tabernacle had two curtains — an outer screen at the entrance and an inner curtain dividing the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place.

In Hebrew, these were two completely different words. The inner curtain was called *paroketh*, and it is used 25 times in the Old Testament. Every single time, it refers to that inner dividing curtain — the one behind which the ark of the covenant sat and where God's presence dwelled. Not once does it mean the outer entrance screen.

Here is where things get complicated. When Jewish scholars translated the Old Testament into Greek (the Septuagint), they used the same Greek word — *katapetasma* — for both curtains. Two distinct Hebrew words became one Greek word. The Greek-speaking reader of Hebrews could not tell from *katapetasma* alone which curtain was meant.

The author of Hebrews knew this. That is why in Hebrews 9:3 he wrote "after the **second** veil." He added the number precisely because the Greek word by itself was ambiguous. If *katapetasma* automatically meant "inner veil," the word "second" would be pointless. But here is the implication for Hebrews 6:19: when he writes "within the veil" without any qualifier, he is leaning on the Old Testament background — where "within the veil" always meant the Most Holy Place.

> **Hebrews 6:19** "Which hope we have as an anchor of the soul, both sure and stedfast, and which entereth into that within the veil."

The point of the metaphor is beautiful. Our hope is like a ship's anchor — but instead of digging into the sea floor, it is lodged in the very presence of God, in the heavenly Most Holy Place. What keeps believers stable is not something visible. It is something beyond the veil.

## Did Jesus Enter the Most Holy Place at His Ascension?

Yes. But here is the detail most discussions miss: there is more than one reason a high priest might enter the Most Holy Place.

The Old Testament sanctuary had **three distinct phases of operation**, not two:

1. **Inauguration** — when the sanctuary was first set up and formally opened (Exodus 40, Leviticus 8–9)
2. **Daily service** — the ongoing morning and evening offerings throughout the year
3. **The Day of Atonement** — the annual judgment-day service at the end of the year (Leviticus 16)

Most debates assume only two options: either Jesus entered the Most Holy Place at His ascension (Day of Atonement at the ascension), or He did not enter the Most Holy Place at all until 1844. But there is a third option the argument overlooks. When Jesus ascended, He was entering as the **inaugurator** — not as the Day-of-Atonement priest.

Think of it this way: the earthly high priest entered the Most Holy Place at the inauguration (the glory of God filled the entire tabernacle in Exodus 40:34–35). He entered again on the Day of Atonement for a completely different purpose. **Two entries into the same space, two different functions.**

Hebrews uses the language of inauguration — not Day-of-Atonement language — to describe the ascension. The Greek word *enkainizo* (meaning "to formally inaugurate" or "to open with ceremony") appears in Hebrews 9:18 and 10:20. It is the same kind of word used when Moses anointed the tabernacle for the first time.

> **Hebrews 10:20** "By a new and living way, which he hath consecrated for us, through the veil, that is to say, his flesh."

Christ opened a permanent way through the veil. That is inauguration language — a one-time opening ceremony with ongoing results. The Day of Atonement, by contrast, was repeated every year.

---

## The Forerunner Who Opens the Door

Hebrews 6:20 calls Jesus a "forerunner" — the Greek word *prodromos*, which appears only once in the entire New Testament.

> **Hebrews 6:20** "Whither the forerunner is for us entered, even Jesus, made an high priest for ever after the order of Melchisedec."

A forerunner is someone who goes ahead of others. He enters first so that others can follow. This is the exact opposite of the Day-of-Atonement pattern. On the Day of Atonement, the high priest entered the Most Holy Place **alone** — Leviticus 16:17 makes it explicit: "There shall be no man in the tabernacle of the congregation when he goeth in." Nobody followed him in.

But a forerunner implies followers. And Hebrews confirms this:

> **Hebrews 10:19** "Having therefore, brethren, boldness to enter into the holiest by the blood of Jesus."

The door Christ opened is open for everyone who comes through His blood. The Day of Atonement closed the door behind the priest. The inauguration opened it permanently. The *forerunner* language points to inauguration, not to the annual Day of Atonement.

## What Does "Sitting Down" Mean?

One of the most misunderstood statements in Hebrews is that Jesus "sat down" at God's right hand. Some read this as meaning all priestly work is finished. But Hebrews itself will not allow that reading.

> **Hebrews 8:1–2** "Now of the things which we have spoken this is the sum: We have such an high priest, who is set on the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in the heavens; a minister of the sanctuary, and of the true tabernacle, which the Lord pitched, and not man."

The author calls this his thesis statement — "the sum of what we have said." And in the same breath he says Jesus is both **seated** and **a minister**. If sitting meant all ministry had stopped, this sentence would make no sense. The author sees no contradiction because he is describing two different things: sitting means the sacrifice is finished; ministering means the priestly work continues.

> **Hebrews 10:11–12** "And every priest standeth daily ministering and offering oftentimes the same sacrifices, which can never take away sins: but this man, after he had offered one sacrifice for sins for ever, sat down on the right hand of God."

The contrast is between the Levitical priest who **stands** because his sacrifices never finish the job, and Christ who **sat down** because His one offering was completely effective. The sitting is about the **completed sacrifice**, not about the **end of all ministry**.

And Hebrews 7:25 confirms that ministry is ongoing:

> **Hebrews 7:25** "Wherefore he is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him, seeing he ever liveth to make intercession for them."

"Ever liveth to make intercession" — present tense, continuous. Jesus is actively interceding right now, from His seated position at God's right hand.

The Old Testament itself anticipated this combination. Psalm 110 contains two statements about the same person: "Sit thou at my right hand" (v. 1) and "Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedek" (v. 4). The author of Hebrews quotes both throughout the letter, never treating them as two separate phases — sit first, priest later. They are simultaneous realities.

Zechariah 6:13 makes it explicit:

> **Zechariah 6:13** "Even he shall build the temple of the LORD; and he shall bear the glory, and shall sit and rule upon his throne; and he shall be a priest upon his throne."

One throne. One person. Sitting, ruling, and priesting — all at the same time. That is the king-priest pattern Jesus fulfills.

## Heaven Is Not Just a Bigger Tabernacle

One important thing Hebrews makes clear: the heavenly sanctuary is not simply a scaled-up version of the earthly one. When the author describes where Christ entered, he eventually stops using compartment language and says something much larger:

> **Hebrews 9:24** "For Christ is not entered into the holy places made with hands, which are the figures of the true; but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us."

"Heaven itself." "The presence of God." That is not a room. It is the unmediated, unveiled reality of God — which is exactly what the Most Holy Place with its mercy seat and cherubim was meant to symbolize. The earthly Most Holy Place was always a picture of something bigger. Christ went to the reality the picture pointed to.

This means the veil-crossing in Hebrews 6:19 represents passing from the visible realm into God's direct presence. The two-compartment structure of the earthly tabernacle was a divinely designed teaching tool — the barrier between compartments illustrated the barrier between humanity and God. Christ's sacrifice tore through that barrier.

---

## The Three Phases Still Hold

The three-phase structure of the earthly sanctuary — inauguration, daily service, Day of Atonement — maps onto the heavenly ministry:

- **Ascension** = Inauguration. Christ entered God's presence to formally open the heavenly ministry, just as Moses formally opened the tabernacle in Exodus 40.
- **Intercession** = Ongoing ministry. Christ "ever liveth to make intercession" (Hebrews 7:25), actively serving from His seated position throughout the present age.
- **Judgment** = The heavenly equivalent of the Day of Atonement. Hebrews 9:23 says the heavenly things themselves need purification — which implies a future heavenly event corresponding to the annual Day of Atonement. This connects to Daniel 8:14 and the pre-advent judgment.

The Day of Atonement antitype is not at the ascension precisely because the ascension is the inauguration. In the earthly system, inauguration came first. The Day of Atonement came last. That order holds in the antitype.

## What This Means for the Series

This study confirms and deepens several threads running through the series.

**The "sat down" argument collapses.** Critics sometimes argue that because Jesus "sat down," His priestly work is finished — no 1844, no ongoing sanctuary ministry. But Hebrews 8:1–2 puts "sat down" and "minister" in the same sentence as its thesis statement. The sitting ends the sacrificial work; it does not end the priestly ministry.

**The ascension was an inauguration, not the Day of Atonement.** This resolves the apparent conflict between "Jesus entered within the veil at the ascension" (which Hebrews clearly teaches) and "the antitypical Day of Atonement begins in 1844" (which Daniel 8:14 and the 2300-day prophecy point to). Both are true because they describe different phases of the same sanctuary ministry.

**The heavenly sanctuary is real, not just a metaphor.** Hebrews 9:23 says the heavenly things "themselves" need purification. Something actual happens in the heavenly sanctuary — not merely a theological concept. This underpins the entire Daniel 8:14 argument.

**Believers have real, present access to God.** Because Jesus entered as forerunner and inaugurated permanent access, the invitation stands now:

> **Hebrews 4:16** "Let us therefore come boldly unto the throne of grace, that we may obtain mercy, and find grace to help in time of need."

That throne is not closed. It is actively dispensing mercy — today, and until the forerunner's work is complete.
