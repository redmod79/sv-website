# What Are Daniel 9:24's Six Purposes?
## A Plain-English Summary

Daniel 9:24 is one of the most packed sentences in the Bible. An angel named Gabriel appears to Daniel and announces that God has set aside a period of 490 years — "seventy weeks" — for a specific set of things to happen. He lists six goals for that period. This study asks: what are those six goals, how do they fit together, and what do they tell us about the bigger picture in the book of Daniel?

---

## The Verse Itself

> "Seventy weeks are determined upon thy people and upon thy holy city, to finish the transgression, and to make an end of sins, and to make reconciliation for iniquity, and to bring in everlasting righteousness, and to seal up the vision and prophecy, and to anoint the most Holy." — Daniel 9:24

Six things. Two groups of three. The first three deal with clearing away the problem of sin. The last three describe what gets installed once sin is dealt with.

---

## The First Three: Clearing Away Sin

The first three purposes are:

1. **Finish the transgression** — bring rebellion to a complete end
2. **Make an end of sins** — terminate wrongdoing
3. **Make reconciliation for iniquity** — make atonement for guilt

What's striking is the specific words used. Hebrew has many words for sin, but these three — transgression (pesha), sin (chattat), and iniquity (avon) — are not chosen randomly. They appear together in only one other place in the entire Old Testament:

> "And Aaron shall lay both his hands upon the head of the live goat, and confess over him all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions in all their sins, putting them upon the head of the goat." — Leviticus 16:21

That verse describes the Day of Atonement — the one day each year when the Israelite high priest performed a special ceremony to cleanse the whole nation's accumulated sin. The same three sin-words, in a single sentence, used as a corporate confession over a sacrifice.

Daniel himself had just used similar language in his prayer earlier in chapter 9. He confessed that Israel had sinned, committed iniquity, and rebelled. Gabriel answers that prayer precisely: those three categories of failure will be finished, ended, and atoned for.

---

## The Last Three: What Gets Built in Their Place

Once the sin problem is cleared, three positive realities take its place:

4. **Bring in everlasting righteousness** — a permanent righteous standing, not just temporary forgiveness
5. **Seal the vision and prophet** — confirm that the prophecy is genuine by having it come true
6. **Anoint the most Holy** — consecrate the heavenly sanctuary for ministry

These are not vague spiritual feelings. They are specific events with specific meanings.

**Everlasting righteousness** is what distinguishes the Messianic atonement from the annual Day of Atonement ceremony. The earthly ceremony produced cleansing — the people were declared clean for another year. But Daniel 9:24 says the result of the Messiah's atonement is something stronger: righteousness that lasts forever. Paul describes this in Romans:

> "Whom God hath set forth to be a propitiation through faith in his blood, to declare his righteousness for the remission of sins that are past... that he might be just, and the justifier of him which believeth in Jesus." — Romans 3:25–26

**Sealing the vision and prophet** means authenticating the prophecy. When the 490-year timeline plays out exactly as predicted — and it did — the prophecy proves itself trustworthy. The precise historical fulfillment (beginning in 457 BC, reaching the Messiah's baptism in AD 27, his death in AD 31, and the end of the period in AD 34) stamps the whole vision as genuine.

**Anointing the most Holy** refers to the inauguration of the heavenly sanctuary. The phrase "most Holy" (Hebrew: qodesh qodashim) appears over forty times in the Old Testament and consistently refers to places, objects, and sacred space — never to a person. Every time Moses anointed something and it became "most holy," it was the tabernacle, the altar, or the furnishings. The pattern is:

> "And thou shalt take the anointing oil, and anoint the tabernacle, and all that is therein, and shalt hallow it... and it shall be an altar most holy." — Exodus 40:9–10

Daniel 9:24 follows this same pattern. The anointing consecrates a sanctuary for ministry to begin — in this case, the heavenly sanctuary where Christ entered after his ascension:

> "For Christ is not entered into the holy places made with hands, which are the figures of the true; but into heaven itself." — Hebrews 9:24

---

## The Word That Proves the 490 Years Connect to the 2,300

The verse opens with an unusual word: "determined" (Hebrew: nechtak). This is the only time this word appears anywhere in the entire Hebrew Bible. Its root meaning is "cut off." Both ancient Greek translations of Daniel recognized this — one rendered it "cut short," the other "decreed."

If 490 years are "cut off," they have to be cut from something. The prior chapter (Daniel 8) already introduced a longer time period: 2,300 evenings and mornings. The angel Gabriel had been sent to explain that period but didn't finish — Daniel fainted before the explanation was complete. Chapter 9 is Gabriel's return visit to finish the job.

The 490 years are the opening portion of the 2,300, cut off and given to Daniel as the testable segment. If the 490 years can be verified historically (and they can), then the whole 2,300-year framework is authenticated by the same prophecy.

---

## The Unique Vocabulary Bridge

One of the study's most precise findings is about vocabulary collision. Across the entire Old Testament, certain words travel in clusters. "Atonement" words (kaphar and its companions) belong to Leviticus — they show up alongside priests, blood, altars, and the mercy seat. "Righteousness" words (tsedeq and its companions) belong to Psalms and Isaiah — they show up alongside justice, truth, and legal verdicts.

These two vocabulary systems almost never overlap. Atonement language and righteousness language run on separate tracks.

Daniel 9:24 is where they meet — the only verse in Scripture where both kaphar (atonement) and tsedeq (righteousness) appear together. Isaiah points toward the same logic:

> "He shall see of the travail of his soul, and shall be satisfied: by his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many; for he shall bear their iniquities." — Isaiah 53:11

Bearing iniquity (the atonement side) produces justification for many (the righteousness side). Daniel 9:24 is where the Bible says this most directly: the act of atonement does not just cleanse — it produces everlasting righteousness.

---

## Daniel's Prayer Sets Up the Answer

Daniel prays during the evening sacrifice time. His prayer reads like a Day of Atonement confession: he confesses sin, iniquity, and rebellion on behalf of all Israel. He appeals to God's righteousness (tsedaqah appears multiple times in the prayer) while acknowledging Israel's failures. He asks for cleansing.

Gabriel's answer exceeds what Daniel asked for. Daniel prayed for forgiveness — the daily-level response to sin. Gabriel promises something the annual Day of Atonement ceremony itself never delivered: not just cleansing (the DOA's result in Leviticus 16:30), but everlasting righteousness. The answer is bigger than the question.

---

## The 490-Year Number Is Not Random

The number 490 echoes through Israel's history. The 70-year Babylonian exile that Daniel is praying about happened because Israel had failed to observe 490 sabbatical years (70 × 7 = 490; see 2 Chronicles 36:21). Four hundred ninety years of covenant failure produced exile and devastation. Gabriel answers with 490 years of Messianic accomplishment that repairs what the failure broke: finished transgression, ended sin, atonement for iniquity, everlasting righteousness, authenticated prophecy, and consecrated heavenly sanctuary. The answer is calibrated to the problem.

---

## What This Means for the Series

This study is the fourteenth in a series investigating Daniel 8:14 — the verse that says the sanctuary will be "vindicated" after 2,300 days. Daniel 9:24 sits at the center of that investigation because it is the hinge between problem and solution.

The series has established that Daniel 8 describes an attack on the heavenly sanctuary — an attack that throws down truth and removes the daily ministry. Daniel 8:14 promises that the sanctuary will be vindicated. But vindication requires a basis. You cannot declare something righteous unless righteousness has already been established.

Daniel 9:24 provides that basis. It is the verse that connects what the Day of Atonement does (kaphar — atone) to what the vindication declares (tsedeq — righteous). It shows that the Messiah's work does not just cleanse the record; it establishes everlasting righteousness. And it consecrates the heavenly sanctuary where that righteous verdict will eventually be formally declared.

Without Daniel 9:24, there is no direct path from Leviticus 16 to Daniel 8:14. With it, the progression is clear: atonement produces cleansing, cleansing produces righteous standing, righteous standing makes possible the final verdict. The six purposes of Daniel 9:24 are not a checklist — they are the theological engine that drives the entire series forward.
