# Verse Analysis

## Verse-by-Verse Analysis

### Genesis 15:6 — The Imputation Anchor
**Context:** Abram has no heir. God promises descendants like the stars. Abram believes the promise.
**Direct statement:** "And he believed in the LORD; and he counted it to him for righteousness."
**Original language:** The Hebrew parsing reveals three key words: he'emin (Hiphil of aman, H539 — he caused himself to be firm/trusting in God), vayyachsheveha (Qal of chashab, H2803 — he reckoned it), tsedaqah (H6666 — righteousness). The word used is tsedaqah — the feminine noun that leans toward personal quality or concrete act. The verb chashab is accounting language: to reckon, credit, impute. From the canonical word study (H2803-chashab.md), this is the OT imputation mechanism.
**Cross-references:** This verse is quoted three times in the NT: Rom 4:3, Gal 3:6, Jas 2:23. Paul builds his entire imputation argument on it. The OT parallels tool finds Psa 9:8 (firm, justice), Isa 56:1 (justice), and Psa 119:137 (righteousness of God). Psa 32:1-2 interprets the same event (David's blessing on the one to whom God "imputes not iniquity").
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the first occurrence of tsedaqah in the OT, and it appears in an imputation context. The forensic dimension begins here. But note: God did not reckon faith "instead of" righteousness — He reckoned the faith-response itself "for" righteousness. Abram trusted, and God credited that trust as a righteous standing.

### Genesis 6:9 — Noah, the Righteous Man
**Context:** The earth is corrupt and filled with violence. God is about to judge by flood.
**Direct statement:** "Noah was a just man and perfect in his generations, and Noah walked with God."
**Original language:** tsaddiyq (H6662) — the adjective "righteous/just." Noah is the first person called tsaddiyq in the Bible. tamiyim — "blameless/complete." The description combines righteous character with relational faithfulness ("walked with God").
**Cross-references:** Heb 11:7 says Noah was "heir of the righteousness which is by faith." Eze 14:14,20 names Noah alongside Daniel and Job as righteous men. Gen 7:1 — God says to Noah, "thee have I seen righteous (tsaddiyq) before me in this generation."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Noah shows that tsaddiyq involves actual conduct — he walked with God while the rest of the world did not. Yet Hebrews credits this to faith. The character and forensic dimensions overlap here.

### Genesis 18:19 — Abraham's Household Keeping the Way of the LORD
**Context:** God is about to reveal His plans for Sodom to Abraham.
**Direct statement:** "He will command his children and his household after him, and they shall keep the way of the LORD, to do justice (tsedaqah) and judgment (mishpat)."
**Original language:** tsedaqah + mishpat — the hendiadys pair that appears 46 times across the OT (from the canonical word study H6666-tsedaqah.md). Here it describes the expected behavior of Abraham's household within the covenant relationship.
**Cross-references:** Mic 6:8 echoes this trio of justice, mercy, and walking with God. Deu 6:25 makes obedience the means by which covenant righteousness is counted.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse roots righteousness in the covenant relationship. God chose Abraham so that Abraham would pass on the practice of doing tsedaqah and mishpat. The relational dimension — righteousness as covenant faithfulness — is clear here.

### Exodus 23:7 — God Will Not Justify the Wicked
**Context:** Laws for the courtroom. Instructions to judges about fairness and justice.
**Direct statement:** "The innocent and righteous slay thou not: for I will not justify the wicked."
**Original language:** atsdiyq (Hiphil Imperfect 1st Singular of tsadaq, H6663) — "I will declare righteous." God says He will NOT apply the Hiphil (declarative) of tsadaq to the rasha (wicked). The Hiphil stem makes this clearly forensic: declaring a verdict.
**Cross-references:** This creates an apparent contradiction with Rom 4:5, where God "justifieth the ungodly." Pro 17:15 calls it "abomination to the LORD" to justify the wicked or condemn the just.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse sets up the deepest tension in the righteousness theme. If God will not declare the wicked righteous, how can Rom 4:5 say He justifies the ungodly? The resolution comes through Isa 53:11 and Rom 3:25-26 — the righteous servant bears iniquities so that the ungodly can be transformed and declared righteous without breaking God's own principle.

### Deuteronomy 6:25 — Righteousness Counted for Obedience
**Context:** Moses instructing Israel on keeping God's commandments.
**Direct statement:** "And it shall be our righteousness, if we observe to do all these commandments before the LORD our God, as he hath commanded us."
**Original language:** tsedaqah (H6666) — the same word used in Gen 15:6 for imputed righteousness is here used for obedience-based righteousness. The verb "observe to do" is shamar + asah — keeping and doing.
**Cross-references:** This seems to teach a righteousness by obedience that conflicts with Rom 3:20 ("by the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified"). But note the context: this is covenant language. Within the covenant, faithfulness to the terms is counted as right standing. This is not earning salvation by merit but maintaining the relationship God established by grace (He brought them out of Egypt first — Deu 6:23).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse shows the relational dimension most clearly. Covenant faithfulness — doing what God commanded — is called righteousness within the covenant relationship. It does not cancel Gen 15:6 but adds a second dimension.

### Deuteronomy 25:1 — The Courtroom Model
**Context:** Civil law instructions for resolving disputes between men.
**Direct statement:** "When there is a lawsuit between men, and they come unto judgment, then they shall judge them; then they shall justify the righteous, and condemn the wicked."
**Original language:** Five key courtroom words converge in one verse: riyb (H7379, lawsuit), mishpat (H4941, judgment), shaphat (H8199, to judge), Hiphil of tsadaq (H6663, to declare righteous/acquit), Hiphil of rasha (H7561, to declare wicked/condemn). Both tsadaq and rasha appear in Hiphil — making both declarative. The court does not make someone righteous or wicked; it declares what they already are.
**Cross-references:** This judicial model carries directly into the NT through dikaioo (G1344), whose Louw-Nida domain is 56.005 — Courts: Judge, Condemn, Acquit. Rom 8:33-34 uses the same courtroom structure: "Who shall lay anything to the charge? God that justifieth. Who condemns?"
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the clearest forensic text. The Hiphil verb pair (justify/condemn) is a courtroom verdict. When Paul says God "justifies," he is using this judicial vocabulary.

### Psalm 32:1-2 — The Blessedness of Imputed Righteousness
**Context:** A psalm of David, attributed by Paul to the man "unto whom God imputeth righteousness without works" (Rom 4:6).
**Direct statement:** "Blessed is he whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is covered. Blessed is the man unto whom the LORD imputeth not iniquity."
**Original language:** chashab (H2803) again — the same imputation verb from Gen 15:6, but now in the negative: God does NOT reckon iniquity.
**Cross-references:** Rom 4:6-8 quotes this psalm to show that imputed righteousness involves both the positive (crediting righteousness) and the negative (not counting sin).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Imputation works in two directions: God credits righteousness and does not credit sin. This is the double accounting of forensic justification.

### Psalm 51:4 — God Vindicated When He Judges
**Context:** David's confession after his sin with Bathsheba.
**Direct statement:** "Against thee, thee only, have I sinned, and done this evil in thy sight: that thou mightest be justified (tsadaq) when thou speakest, and be clear when thou judgest."
**Original language:** tsadaq here is Qal — God IS righteous in His verdict. This is not the Hiphil (declaring righteous) but the Qal (being righteous). God's judgments are themselves righteous.
**Cross-references:** Paul quotes this in Rom 3:4 to show that human sin cannot impugn God's righteousness. God is vindicated even when humans fail.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse applies tsadaq to God Himself. God's own righteousness is at stake when He judges. This connects to the vindication theme: Dan 8:14's nitsdaq involves God's character being vindicated through the sanctuary.

### Psalm 103:17-18 — Covenant Righteousness
**Context:** David praising God's merciful character.
**Direct statement:** "The mercy of the LORD is from everlasting to everlasting upon them that fear him, and his righteousness (tsedaqah) unto children's children; to such as keep his covenant."
**Original language:** checed (H2617, covenant loyalty) + tsedaqah (H6666) — the relational vocabulary pair. From the canonical word study (H2617-checed.md), checed appears 241 times and is central to the relational dimension of righteousness. Here, God's covenant loyalty and His righteousness run together and are directed toward those who keep His covenant.
**Cross-references:** Mic 6:8 asks for checed alongside mishpat. Hos 6:6 says God desires checed, not sacrifice.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Righteousness is covenant-shaped. God's tsedaqah flows to those within the covenant who respond with faithful obedience. The relational dimension.

### Proverbs 17:15 — The Abomination of False Verdicts
**Context:** Wisdom teaching about justice.
**Direct statement:** "He that justifieth the wicked, and he that condemneth the just, even they both are abomination to the LORD."
**Original language:** The Hiphil of tsadaq (declare righteous) and the Hiphil of rasha (declare wicked) appear together — the same antonym pair as Deu 25:1. A false verdict in either direction is abomination.
**Cross-references:** This connects to Exo 23:7 and intensifies the tension with Rom 4:5. It also connects to Isa 5:20,23 ("woe unto them which justify the wicked for reward").
**Relationship to other evidence:** Confirms that the tsadaq/rasha pair is courtroom language. The forensic verdict must match reality. Declaring the guilty innocent is as wicked as declaring the innocent guilty.

### Isaiah 50:8 — Courtroom Vindication of the Servant
**Context:** The third Servant Song. The Servant endures suffering and trusts God to vindicate him.
**Direct statement:** "He is near that justifieth me; who will contend with me?"
**Original language:** matsdiyqi (Hiphil Participle of tsadaq, H6663) — "the one who declares me righteous." riyb (H7378) — "who will bring a lawsuit (riyb) against me?" The courtroom language is explicit: vindication by God, challenge to any accuser.
**Cross-references:** Rom 8:33-34 directly echoes this passage: "Who shall lay anything to the charge of God's elect? It is God that justifieth. Who is he that condemneth?" Paul transposes Isaiah's Servant language into the believer's experience.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The Servant trusts in God's forensic vindication. The NT applies this same vindication to all believers through Christ. The courtroom pattern continues from Deu 25:1 through Isaiah into Romans.

### Isaiah 53:11 — The Righteous Servant Justifying Many
**Context:** The fourth Servant Song. The Servant has suffered, died, and is now satisfied.
**Direct statement:** "By his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many; for he shall bear their iniquities."
**Original language:** Three tsadaq forms converge: tsaddiyq (H6662, adjective — the Servant IS righteous), yatsdiyq (Hiphil of H6663 — He SHALL DECLARE RIGHTEOUS many), and the mechanism is "he shall bear their iniquities" (yisbol avonotam). The Hiphil is causative-declarative: the righteous Servant causes many to be declared righteous.
**Cross-references:** 2 Cor 5:21 — "He made him to be sin for us... that we might be made the righteousness of God in him." Rom 5:19 — "by the obedience of one shall many be made righteous."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the hinge verse of the entire study. It resolves the Exo 23:7 problem: God CAN justify the ungodly because the righteous Servant bears their iniquities. It connects all three dimensions: the Servant is righteous in character (tsaddiyq), He issues a forensic declaration (yatsdiyq), and the mechanism involves a relational substitution (bearing iniquities for others).

### Isaiah 61:10 — The Robe of Righteousness
**Context:** The anointed Servant speaks of restoration and joy.
**Direct statement:** "He hath clothed me with the garments of salvation, he hath covered me with the robe of righteousness (tsedaqah)."
**Original language:** tsedaqah (H6666) — righteousness as a garment. The clothing metaphor shows God providing righteousness as something that covers the person. The passive language ("he hath clothed me") indicates God is the one who dresses the person in righteousness.
**Cross-references:** Zech 3:3-5 dramatizes this — Joshua's filthy garments removed, clean garments given. Rev 19:8 gives the NT counterpart, but with dikaiomata (righteous acts).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This garment imagery bridges forensic and character dimensions. God provides the covering (forensic), but what is worn is actual righteousness (character). The garment is not a fiction. It is real righteousness placed on the person.

### Isaiah 64:6 — Self-Righteousness as Filthy Rags
**Context:** Israel confessing its sin and unworthiness before God.
**Direct statement:** "All our righteousnesses are as filthy rags."
**Original language:** tsedaqah in the plural (tsidqoteynu — "our righteousnesses") — these are human attempts at righteous acts. They are compared to filthy garments (literally, menstrual cloths). Note: this is not saying righteousness itself is worthless — it is saying human-generated righteousness, apart from God, is polluted.
**Cross-references:** Php 3:8-9 — Paul considers his law-keeping as "dung" compared to "the righteousness which is of God by faith." Rom 10:3 — Israel was "going about to establish their own righteousness" and failed.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse exposes the bankruptcy of self-righteousness while simultaneously confirming that true righteousness must come from God. It does not contradict Deu 6:25 (covenant obedience as righteousness) but condemns the attempt to be righteous apart from God's covenant relationship.

### Jeremiah 23:6 — The LORD Our Righteousness
**Context:** Messianic prophecy of the coming righteous Branch.
**Direct statement:** "This is his name whereby he shall be called, THE LORD OUR RIGHTEOUSNESS (YHWH Tsidqenu)."
**Original language:** YHWH + tsedeq (H6664) + 1cp suffix — "the LORD is our righteousness." The Messiah's name itself identifies Him as the source of righteousness for His people.
**Cross-references:** 1 Cor 1:30 — "Christ Jesus, who of God is made unto us wisdom, and righteousness, and sanctification, and redemption." Php 3:9 — "the righteousness which is of God by faith."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Righteousness comes from God through the Messiah. This is not just forensic status — the Messiah embodies righteousness. The name "YHWH Tsidqenu" means the LORD Himself becomes our right standing.

### Jeremiah 31:31-34 — The New Covenant
**Context:** God promises a new covenant to replace the one Israel broke.
**Direct statement:** "I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts... I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more."
**Original language:** The covenant involves both forgiveness (forensic — sin not remembered) and internal transformation (relational/character — law written on hearts).
**Cross-references:** Heb 8:8-12 quotes this passage in full. Rom 8:4 says "the righteousness of the law might be fulfilled in us, who walk... after the Spirit."
**Relationship to other evidence:** The new covenant combines all three dimensions. Forensic: sins forgiven. Relational: "I will be their God." Character: the law written within, producing obedience from the inside out.

### Ezekiel 18:5-9 — The Ethical Catalog of the Righteous
**Context:** God refutes the proverb "the fathers have eaten sour grapes and the children's teeth are set on edge." Each person is accountable for their own conduct.
**Direct statement:** "If a man be just (tsaddiyq), and do that which is lawful (mishpat) and right (tsedaqah)... he is just, he shall surely live."
**Original language:** tsaddiyq + mishpat + tsedaqah — the triad of righteous character, just judgment, and righteous action. The catalog that follows (vv. 6-8) lists specific behaviors: not worshipping idols, not defiling a neighbor's wife, not oppressing, restoring pledges, feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, not charging usury.
**Cross-references:** Eze 18:5-9 parallels the specific ethical requirements of Mic 6:8 and Deu 6:25. NT parallels: Jas 2:14-26 (faith shown by works), 1 John 3:7 ("he that doeth righteousness is righteous").
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the fullest OT description of what righteous conduct looks like in practice. It proves that "righteous" is not a purely forensic label — it describes someone who actually does right things. The character dimension is detailed here.

### Ezekiel 18:24 — Losing Righteousness
**Context:** Same chapter as the ethical catalog. God addresses whether the righteous can fall.
**Direct statement:** "When the righteous turneth away from his righteousness, and committeth iniquity... all his righteousness that he hath done shall not be mentioned."
**Original language:** tsaddiyq turning from tsedaqah. The word "turneth away" (shuv) indicates a settled reversal — not a single stumble but a turning from the path.
**Cross-references:** Heb 10:26-29 (willful sinning after knowledge of truth), 2 Pet 2:20-22 (worse than before), Gal 5:4 (fallen from grace).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse directly addresses the "lost" dimension. Righteous standing can be forfeited through deliberate, sustained turning away. Previous righteousness is "not mentioned" — it is not banked. The relational and character dimensions matter: the person has broken the covenant relationship and abandoned righteous conduct.

### Daniel 8:14 — The Niphal Verdict
**Context:** A heavenly being asks how long the sanctuary will be trampled. The answer comes.
**Direct statement:** "Unto two thousand and three hundred days; then shall the sanctuary be cleansed (nitsdaq)."
**Original language:** nitsdaq — Niphal Perfect 3ms of tsadaq (H6663). This is the ONLY Niphal of tsadaq in the entire OT. From the grammar reference (passages/dan-8-14.md), the Niphal is a simple passive: the sanctuary receives vindication. It does not vindicate itself. KJV's "cleansed" comes from the Vulgate and Theodotion, not from the Hebrew root. The Hebrew root is tsadaq (righteous/vindicated), not taher (ritually clean). Every other occurrence of tsadaq in the OT stays within the righteous/justify/just domain.
**Cross-references:** Dan 7:9-14 describes the same event from a judicial angle (the court is set, the books are opened). Dan 9:24 bridges atonement (kaphar) to everlasting righteousness (tsedeq olamim).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse is the destination the series is building toward. Nitsdaq uses courtroom vocabulary (tsadaq) applied to the sanctuary. The sanctuary receives a passive verdict of vindication — declared righteous. All three dimensions apply: the sanctuary's function (character), its covenant standing (relational), and its judicial status (forensic) are all vindicated.

### Daniel 9:24 — The Bridge Verse
**Context:** Gabriel explains the 70-weeks prophecy to Daniel.
**Direct statement:** "To finish the transgression, and to make an end of sins, and to make reconciliation (lekhapper) for iniquity, and to bring in everlasting righteousness (tsedeq olamim)."
**Original language:** Six infinitives span from atonement vocabulary (lekhapper — Piel Infinitive Construct of kaphar, H3722) to righteousness vocabulary (tsedeq olamim, H6664 + H5769). From the canonical word study (H3722-kaphar.md), kaphar means to atone, cover, make amends. tsedeq olamim is "everlasting righteousness" — a permanent righteous standing that endures through the ages.
**Cross-references:** Lev 16:30 uses kaphar → taher (atonement leads to cleansing). Dan 9:24 goes further: kaphar → tsedeq (atonement leads to everlasting righteousness). Rom 3:25-26 completes the picture: hilasterion → dikaios (propitiation → just and justifier).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the ONLY verse that bridges both atonement and righteousness vocabulary in the same sentence. It shows that atonement produces righteousness — the progression the series traces.

### Habakkuk 2:4 — The Just Shall Live by Faith
**Context:** God tells Habakkuk to wait for the vision's fulfillment.
**Direct statement:** "The just shall live by his faith."
**Original language:** tsaddiyq (H6662) + emunah (H530) — the righteous person + faithfulness/steadfastness. From the canonical word study (H530-emuwnah.md), emunah carries the sense of firmness, reliability, and faithful persistence. This is not "faith" as a one-time act but "faithfulness" as a way of life.
**Cross-references:** Quoted three times in the NT: Rom 1:17, Gal 3:11, Heb 10:38. Paul uses it for justification by faith; Hebrews uses it for persevering faithfulness. Both dimensions exist in the original.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse bridges the forensic and relational dimensions. The just (forensic standing) shall live (ongoing life) by faithfulness (relational commitment). Being declared righteous leads to living faithfully.

### Micah 6:8 — The Three Requirements
**Context:** God reminds Israel of what He really wants from them.
**Direct statement:** "He hath shewed thee, O man, what is good; and what doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly (mishpat), and to love mercy (checed), and to walk humbly with thy God?"
**Original language:** mishpat (H4941, justice) + checed (H2617, covenant loyalty) + walking with God. From the canonical word study (H2617-checed.md), checed means "covenant loyalty/faithfulness." This verse combines the relational dimension (love checed, walk humbly with God) with the character dimension (do mishpat).
**Cross-references:** Parallels Gen 18:19 (do tsedaqah and mishpat). NT echoes in Luk 6:35 (compassion, goodness), 1 Tim 6:11 (compassion), Jas 3:17 (merciful, full of good fruits).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Mic 6:8 shows what righteousness looks like in daily life. It is not about ritual performance but about doing justice, loving covenant loyalty, and walking with God. The relational and character dimensions dominate.

### Luke 1:6 — Zacharias and Elisabeth: Righteous Before God
**Context:** The introduction of John the Baptist's parents.
**Direct statement:** "They were both righteous (dikaios) before God, walking in all the commandments and ordinances (dikaioma, G1345) of the Lord blameless."
**Original language:** dikaios (G1342) + dikaioma (G1345) — righteous persons walking in righteous ordinances. From the canonical word study (G1345-dikaioma.md), dikaioma here means "righteous regulations/ordinances." Their righteousness is described as obedient conduct.
**Cross-references:** This is the NT equivalent of Eze 18:5-9 — a concrete description of what righteous people actually look like. Also echoes Deu 6:25 (obedience counted as righteousness).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Confirms that "righteous" in the Bible includes actual conduct, not just declared status. These two are called dikaios because they walked in God's commands. The character dimension is visible in their lives.

### Matthew 5:20 — Righteousness That Exceeds
**Context:** The Sermon on the Mount. Jesus establishes a higher standard.
**Direct statement:** "Except your righteousness shall exceed the righteousness of the scribes and Pharisees, ye shall in no case enter into the kingdom of heaven."
**Original language:** dikaiosyne (G1343) — righteousness. Jesus says the required righteousness must "exceed" (perisseuse) that of the Pharisees — not less righteousness but more. What follows in Matt 5:21-48 shows that the required standard goes deeper than external compliance to internal reality.
**Cross-references:** Phil 3:8-9 — Paul's own Pharisaic righteousness compared to the righteousness of God by faith. Matt 23:23-28 — Jesus condemns the Pharisees for externalism while neglecting justice, mercy, and faithfulness.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This challenges purely forensic readings. Jesus does not say "your imputed status must exceed theirs" but "your righteousness must exceed theirs." The character dimension matters. The Pharisees had external compliance. Jesus demands internal transformation that produces genuine righteous conduct.

### Matthew 22:11-14 — The Wedding Garment
**Context:** The parable of the king's wedding feast. A guest is found without proper attire.
**Direct statement:** "How camest thou in hither not having a wedding garment? And he was speechless."
**Original language:** endyma gamou — "wedding garment." This is a provided garment (the king hosts the feast and would supply garments), yet this guest refused it.
**Cross-references:** Isa 61:10 (robe of righteousness), Zech 3:3-5 (garment exchange), Rev 19:8 (fine linen = dikaiomata of saints).
**Relationship to other evidence:** The garment must be received (forensic — provided by the King) but also worn (character — the person puts it on). Refusing the provided garment is fatal. This connects to Eze 18:24: the person who had the garment but turned away from it.

### Job 29:14 — Wearing Righteousness
**Context:** Job recalls his former days of honor and service.
**Direct statement:** "I put on righteousness (tsedeq), and it clothed me: my judgment (mishpat) was as a robe and a diadem."
**Original language:** tsedeq (H6664) + mishpat (H4941) — righteousness and justice as clothing. Job describes his righteous conduct as something he actively put on. It then clothed him — became his identity.
**Cross-references:** Rev 19:8 — dikaiomata (righteous deeds) compose the wedding garment. Isa 61:10 — God provides the garment.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Here the garment comes from the person's own doing — the character dimension. Job put on righteous conduct. But compare Isa 61:10 and Rev 19:8 where the garment is also "granted" by God. The full picture: God provides what the believer lives out.

### Zechariah 3:1-5 — The Garment Exchange
**Context:** A courtroom vision. Joshua the high priest stands before the Angel of the LORD. Satan stands to accuse.
**Direct statement:** "Take away the filthy garments from him... I have caused thine iniquity to pass from thee, and I will clothe thee with change of raiment."
**Original language:** The filthy garments represent iniquity. The removal of garments IS the removal of iniquity. The new clothing is provided by God. This is a courtroom scene: the defendant (Joshua), the accuser (Satan), the Judge (the Angel of the LORD), and the verdict (garments exchanged, iniquity removed).
**Cross-references:** Rom 8:33-34 — "Who shall lay anything to the charge? God that justifieth. Who condemns?" Isa 61:10 — robe of righteousness. Rev 12:10 — the accuser of our brethren cast down.
**Relationship to other evidence:** All three dimensions appear. Forensic: a courtroom with accuser and judge, a verdict pronounced. Relational: Joshua represents Israel in covenant relationship (v. 7 — "if thou wilt walk in my ways"). Character: filthy garments removed, clean garments given — actual transformation, not just relabeling.

### Leviticus 16:30 — kaphar to taher
**Context:** The Day of Atonement instructions.
**Direct statement:** "For on that day shall the priest make an atonement (kaphar) for you, to cleanse (taher) you, that ye may be clean from all your sins before the LORD."
**Original language:** kaphar (H3722, to atone) → taher (H2891, to cleanse). The earthly Day of Atonement moves from atonement to ritual cleansing. From the canonical word study (H3722-kaphar.md), kaphar is covering/atonement — the process. taher is ritual purity — the result.
**Cross-references:** Dan 9:24 extends this: kaphar → tsedeq olamim (atonement → everlasting righteousness). The earthly type stops at ritual cleansing; the antitype reaches vindication.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This establishes the starting point of the progression: kaphar → taher → tsadaq. Day of Atonement cleanses. Dan 9:24 moves from cleansing to righteousness. Dan 8:14 declares the end result — vindication (nitsdaq).

### Romans 3:10 — None Righteous
**Context:** Paul establishing universal human sinfulness.
**Direct statement:** "There is none righteous, no, not one."
**Original language:** dikaios (G1342) — righteous. Paul quotes Psa 14:1-3. This is a statement about the natural human condition — no one achieves righteousness by their own effort.
**Cross-references:** Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned." Isa 64:6 — "all our righteousnesses are as filthy rags."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This establishes why imputed righteousness is necessary. No human can generate sufficient righteousness. It does not say righteousness is impossible — it says human-generated righteousness is impossible. God must provide it.

### Romans 3:20 — Not by Deeds of Law
**Context:** Paul arguing that the law reveals sin but cannot produce righteousness.
**Direct statement:** "By the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified in his sight: for by the law is the knowledge of sin."
**Original language:** dikaioo (G1344) in future passive — "shall be justified." The law's function is to reveal sin (knowledge = epignosis), not to produce righteousness.
**Cross-references:** Gal 2:16 — "a man is not justified by the works of the law, but by the faith of Jesus Christ."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Law-keeping cannot produce the forensic verdict. This does not say law-keeping is unimportant (Rom 8:4 says the righteousness of the law IS fulfilled in believers) — it says law-keeping cannot be the basis of the verdict.

### Romans 3:21-22 — The Righteousness of God by Faith
**Context:** The turning point in Paul's argument — from human inability to God's provision.
**Direct statement:** "But now the righteousness of God without the law is manifested, being witnessed by the law and the prophets; even the righteousness of God which is by faith of Jesus Christ."
**Original language:** dikaiosyne theou (G1343 + G2316) — "the righteousness of God." This phrase can mean "righteousness from God" (source), "God's own righteous character" (quality), or "God's righteous saving action" (activity). All three nuances are present in Romans.
**Cross-references:** Phil 3:9 — "the righteousness which is of God by faith." 2 Cor 5:21 — "that we might be made the righteousness of God in him."
**Relationship to other evidence:** "The righteousness of God" is the answer to human inability. It comes through faith in Jesus Christ, was witnessed by the OT (Gen 15:6, Hab 2:4), and is available to all who believe.

### Romans 3:24-26 — Justified Freely by Grace
**Context:** The heart of Paul's gospel — how God justifies sinners.
**Direct statement:** "Being justified freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus: whom God hath set forth to be a propitiation through faith in his blood, to declare his righteousness... that he might be just, and the justifier of him which believeth in Jesus."
**Original language:** Three dik- family words converge: dikaiosyne (G1343, "his righteousness"), dikaios (G1342, "just"), dikaioo (G1344, "justifier"). Present passive participle dikaiounmenoi — "being justified" (ongoing availability, divine passive). hilasterion (G2435) — mercy seat/propitiation. From the canonical word study (G2435-hilasterion.md), this connects to the OT mercy seat where atonement was made.
**Cross-references:** Lev 16:30 (Day of Atonement), Isa 53:11 (the righteous servant justifying many), Dan 9:24 (kaphar → tsedeq olamim).
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is where the sanctuary and the courtroom meet. The hilasterion (mercy seat) is the ground where God shows Himself to be both dikaios (just) AND dikaioo (the justifier). He does not violate Exo 23:7 because the propitiation satisfies justice. He does not merely overlook sin. He deals with it through Christ's blood, then justifies the believer.

### Romans 4:3-5 — The Imputation Argument
**Context:** Paul argues that Abraham was justified by faith, not by works.
**Direct statement:** "Abraham believed God, and it was counted unto him for righteousness... to him that worketh not, but believeth on him that justifieth the ungodly, his faith is counted for righteousness."
**Original language:** elogisthe (Aorist Passive of logizomai, G3049) — "it was reckoned" (divine passive). dikaioo (G1344) — "the one who justifies." The present active participle ton dikaiounta — God is the one "justifying the ungodly (asebes)."
**Cross-references:** Gen 15:6 (the original event), Jas 2:21-23 (Abraham justified by works when he offered Isaac — faith completed by works).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Paul's argument is about the ground of justification — faith, not works. But Rom 4:5 says God "justifieth the ungodly" — creating the tension with Exo 23:7. The resolution is in the context: God justifies the ungodly through the propitiation of Christ (Rom 3:25), not by ignoring their ungodliness. Faith receives what Christ provides.

### Romans 4:6-8 — Imputing Righteousness Without Works
**Context:** Paul quotes David (Psa 32:1-2) alongside Abraham.
**Direct statement:** "David describeth the blessedness of the man, unto whom God imputeth righteousness without works."
**Original language:** logizomai (G3049) — "imputeth/reckons." This is the positive side: God credits righteousness. Verses 7-8 are the negative: iniquities forgiven, sins covered, sin not imputed.
**Cross-references:** Psa 32:1-2 (the original), Gen 15:6 (Abraham parallel).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Forensic imputation has two movements — crediting righteousness and not counting sin. Both are acts of divine accounting.

### Romans 4:22-25 — Delivered and Raised for Our Justification
**Context:** Paul concludes the Abraham argument.
**Direct statement:** "It was imputed to him for righteousness... Who was delivered for our offences, and was raised again for our justification (dikaiosis, G1347)."
**Original language:** dikaiosis (G1347) — one of only two occurrences. From the canonical word study (G1347-dikaiosis.md), this is the act/process of being declared righteous. The -sis suffix indicates the act itself (vs. dikaioma, the result). Christ was "delivered for our offenses" (death for sin) and "raised for our justification" (resurrection proves the payment was accepted).
**Cross-references:** Rom 5:18 (the other dikaiosis occurrence — "justification of life"). 1 Cor 15:17 — "if Christ be not raised, your faith is vain; ye are yet in your sins."
**Relationship to other evidence:** The resurrection is essential to justification. If Christ remained dead, there would be no acquittal. His rising proves that God accepted the sacrifice and that the verdict of vindication is real.

### Romans 5:1 — Peace Through Justification
**Context:** Paul draws the conclusion from the justification argument.
**Direct statement:** "Therefore being justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ."
**Original language:** dikaioo (G1344) in aorist passive participle — "having been justified" (completed act, divine passive). The result is peace (eirene) with God — the relational dimension. Justification restores the relationship.
**Cross-references:** Rom 8:1 — "no condemnation." Eph 2:13-14 — "made nigh... he is our peace."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Forensic justification has relational consequences. The verdict of acquittal restores peace between God and the sinner. All three dimensions connect: the verdict (forensic) restores the relationship (relational) and produces a new way of living (character — Rom 5:2-5 describes hope, patience, experience).

### Romans 5:9 — Justified by His Blood
**Context:** Paul connecting justification to salvation from future wrath.
**Direct statement:** "Being now justified by his blood, we shall be saved from wrath through him."
**Original language:** dikaioo (G1344) in aorist passive participle — "having been justified." The blood of Christ is the basis of justification. Saved from wrath is the eschatological result.
**Cross-references:** Rom 3:25 — propitiation "through faith in his blood." Heb 9:22 — "without shedding of blood is no remission."
**Relationship to other evidence:** The blood grounds the forensic verdict. Without the sacrifice, there is no basis for the acquittal.

### Romans 5:18-19 — The Adam-Christ Parallel
**Context:** Paul comparing Adam's transgression with Christ's righteous act.
**Direct statement:** "As by the offence of one judgment came upon all men to condemnation; even so by the righteousness of one the free gift came upon all men unto justification of life. For as by one man's disobedience many were made sinners, so by the obedience of one shall many be made righteous."
**Original language:** V. 18: dikaiomatos (G1345, genitive — "of one righteous act") → dikaiosis zoes (G1347 + G2222 — "justification of life"). katakrima (G2631 — "condemnation") vs. dikaiosis (G1347 — "justification") — the antonym pair from the canonical word study (G2631-katakrima.md). V. 19: kathistemi (G2525) — "were made/constituted" — both sinners and righteous are constituted by the action of another.
**Cross-references:** 1 Cor 15:21-22 — "As in Adam all die, even so in Christ shall all be made alive."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This passage shows that justification is not just a verdict but involves being actually constituted righteous (kathistemi). The "obedience of one" produces this. The forensic verdict (dikaiosis) is grounded in Christ's actual obedient life (dikaioma — righteous act). Character and forensic dimensions merge.

### Romans 6:7 — Freed from Sin
**Context:** Paul arguing that the believer has died with Christ to sin.
**Direct statement:** "He that is dead is freed from sin."
**Original language:** dedikaiотai (G1344, perfect passive of dikaioo) — literally "has been justified from sin." Strikingly, KJV translates dikaioo as "freed" here. This is the only place where dikaioo is rendered "freed" in the KJV. The perfect tense indicates a completed action with ongoing results. The passive is the divine passive.
**Cross-references:** Rom 8:1-2 — "no condemnation... the law of the Spirit of life hath made me free from the law of sin and death."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Even dikaioo — the courtroom word — can carry a "liberation/freedom" nuance. Being justified is not just a verdict; it is a release from sin's claim. This connects the forensic verdict to actual freedom.

### Romans 8:1-4 — No Condemnation, Walking in the Spirit
**Context:** Paul concluding his argument about the believer's new life.
**Direct statement:** "There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus... that the righteousness (dikaioma, G1345) of the law might be fulfilled in us, who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit."
**Original language:** katakrima (G2631) — condemnation (the same word as Rom 5:18). dikaioma tou nomou (G1345 — "the righteous requirement of the law"). The righteous standard of the law IS fulfilled in believers who walk by the Spirit. This is not merely imputed to them — it is "fulfilled in us."
**Cross-references:** Jer 31:33 — "I will put my law in their inward parts." Gal 5:22-23 — the fruit of the Spirit IS the fulfillment of the law.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The character dimension shows up powerfully. God's purpose in justification is not merely verdict but transformation: the law's righteous requirement fulfilled in the believer's life. Forensic (no condemnation) enables relational (in Christ Jesus) which produces character (walking in the Spirit).

### Romans 8:33-34 — The Courtroom Challenge
**Context:** Paul's triumphant conclusion about the believer's security.
**Direct statement:** "Who shall lay any thing to the charge of God's elect? It is God that justifieth. Who is he that condemneth? It is Christ that died, yea rather, that is risen again, who is even at the right hand of God, who also maketh intercession for us."
**Original language:** egkalesei (G1458, "shall bring a charge" — legal term for formal accusation) → dikaioo (G1344, present active participle — "the one justifying") → katakrino (G2632, "condemn" — the verdict antonym). From the canonical word study (G1344-dikaioo.md), God is named as the active justifier in the present tense — ongoing action.
**Cross-references:** Isa 50:8 — "He is near that justifieth me; who will contend (riyb) with me?" Zech 3:1-5 — Satan standing to accuse, the Lord rebuking. Rom 8:1 — "no condemnation."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the fullest NT courtroom scene for justification. Accuser, defendant, judge, verdict — all present. God is the judge who justifies, Christ is the advocate who intercedes, and no accuser can overturn the verdict. Pure forensic language, echoing Isa 50:8 directly.

### Romans 10:3 — Self-Righteousness vs. God's Righteousness
**Context:** Paul explaining why Israel stumbled.
**Direct statement:** "They being ignorant of God's righteousness, and going about to establish their own righteousness, have not submitted themselves unto the righteousness of God."
**Original language:** dikaiosyne theou (G1343 — "God's righteousness") vs. idian dikaiosynen ("their own righteousness"). Two kinds of righteousness in contrast.
**Cross-references:** Isa 64:6 — human righteousness as filthy rags. Phil 3:9 — Paul rejecting his own righteousness for God's.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Self-generated righteousness cannot stand. Only the righteousness that comes from God is sufficient. This reinforces the forensic dimension — righteousness must be received, not achieved — while also showing that refusing God's provision is fatal.

### 1 Corinthians 6:11 — Washed, Sanctified, Justified
**Context:** Paul reminding the Corinthians of their transformation.
**Direct statement:** "But ye are washed, but ye are sanctified, but ye are justified in the name of the Lord Jesus, and by the Spirit of our God."
**Original language:** Three aorist passive verbs: apelousasthe (washed), hegiasthete (sanctified), edikaioothete (justified — G1344, aorist passive). All three are divine passives. The order — washed, sanctified, justified — may be liturgical or experiential rather than strictly logical.
**Cross-references:** Tit 3:5-7 — "washing of regeneration... being justified by his grace." Rev 7:14 — "washed their robes."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Justification appears alongside washing and sanctification as aspects of the same conversion experience. The three dimensions operate together: cleansing (relational restoration), sanctification (character transformation), justification (forensic verdict).

### 2 Corinthians 5:21 — Made the Righteousness of God
**Context:** Paul explaining the ministry of reconciliation.
**Direct statement:** "He hath made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in him."
**Original language:** genometa (Aorist Middle Subjunctive of ginomai, G1096 — "that we might become"). This is not "that we might be declared righteous" but "that we might become the righteousness of God." dikaiosyne theou — the same phrase as Rom 3:21-22.
**Cross-references:** Isa 53:11 — the righteous servant bearing iniquities. Rom 5:19 — "made righteous."
**Relationship to other evidence:** The verb "become" (ginomai) pushes beyond purely forensic language. Believers do not merely receive a verdict — they become something. The character dimension is present: actual transformation into righteousness, not just labeled as righteous. Yet this happens "in him" — it is Christ-centered, not self-generated.

### Galatians 2:16 — Not Justified by Law-Works
**Context:** Paul confronting Peter's hypocrisy and establishing justification by faith.
**Direct statement:** "A man is not justified by the works of the law, but by the faith of Jesus Christ... for by the works of the law shall no flesh be justified."
**Original language:** Three forms of dikaioo (G1344) in one verse — present passive, aorist passive subjunctive, future passive — all passive (divine passive). The negative: law-works do not produce the verdict. The positive: faith in Jesus Christ does.
**Cross-references:** Rom 3:20,28 — the same point. Acts 13:39 — "by him all that believe are justified."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Reinforces the forensic dimension. The verdict comes through faith, not through law-keeping. But note: Paul is not saying law-keeping is wrong — he is saying it cannot be the ground of the verdict.

### Galatians 5:4-6 — Faith Working Through Love
**Context:** Paul warning against returning to law-based justification.
**Direct statement:** "Ye are fallen from grace... for in Jesus Christ neither circumcision availeth any thing, nor uncircumcision; but faith which worketh by love."
**Original language:** pistis di agapes energoumene (G4102 + G26 + G1754 — "faith working/active through love"). This is the formula that holds justification and works together. Faith is not passive; it works through love. Yet it is faith that justifies, not the works.
**Cross-references:** Jas 2:22 — "faith wrought with his works." 1 John 3:7 — "he that doeth righteousness is righteous."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This bridges forensic and character dimensions. The faith that receives the verdict (forensic) is a living, active faith that works through love (character). "Fallen from grace" shows that the forensic standing can be lost by abandoning faith for law-based merit.

### Philippians 3:8-9 — Not My Own Righteousness
**Context:** Paul recounting his credentials and rejecting them.
**Direct statement:** "I count all things but loss... that I may win Christ, and be found in him, not having mine own righteousness, which is of the law, but that which is through the faith of Christ, the righteousness which is of God by faith."
**Original language:** dikaiosyne (G1343) appears three times: "my own righteousness from law" vs. "the righteousness through faith of Christ" vs. "the righteousness from God (ek theou) upon faith."
**Cross-references:** Rom 10:3 — establishing own righteousness vs. God's. Isa 64:6 — human righteousness as filthy rags.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the clearest personal statement of forensic righteousness. Paul rejects self-generated righteousness and receives God-given righteousness through faith. The source matters: from God, not from self.

### Titus 2:11-12 — Grace Teaching Righteous Living
**Context:** Paul instructing Titus on sound doctrine.
**Direct statement:** "The grace of God that bringeth salvation hath appeared to all men, teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly, in this present world."
**Original language:** dikaios (G1346, the adverb — "righteously"). Grace does not just pardon; it teaches righteous living. The character dimension: grace produces conduct.
**Cross-references:** Rom 6:1-2 — "Shall we continue in sin, that grace may abound? God forbid." Rom 8:4 — righteousness of the law fulfilled in us.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Grace and righteous conduct are not opposed. Grace is the teacher that produces soberly, righteously, and godly living. The forensic gift (salvation) leads to the character result (righteous living).

### Hebrews 10:26-29 — No More Sacrifice for Willful Sin
**Context:** Warning passage after explaining Christ's sacrifice.
**Direct statement:** "If we sin wilfully after that we have received the knowledge of the truth, there remaineth no more sacrifice for sins."
**Original language:** hamartanonton (Present Active Participle of hamartano, G264 — "while sinning"). The present tense indicates ongoing, continuous, willful sinning. This is not an isolated failure but a sustained pattern of deliberate rebellion against known truth. hekousios — "willfully/deliberately."
**Cross-references:** Eze 18:24 — turning away from righteousness. 2 Pet 2:20-22 — worse than before. Heb 6:4-6 — "impossible to renew."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Righteous standing can be lost through persistent, willful rebellion against known truth. The key word is the present participle — ongoing action, not a single lapse. This is settled apostasy, not stumbling.

### Hebrews 11:4,7 — Righteousness by Faith in the OT
**Context:** The "faith chapter" — examples of those who lived by faith.
**Direct statement:** V. 4: "By faith Abel offered unto God a more excellent sacrifice, by which he obtained witness that he was righteous." V. 7: "By faith Noah... became heir of the righteousness which is by faith."
**Original language:** dikaios (G1342) and dikaiosyne (G1343) — both in the context of faith. Abel was "witnessed as righteous" through faith's offering. Noah "inherited the righteousness by faith."
**Cross-references:** Gen 4:4 (Abel's offering), Gen 6:9 (Noah called just). Rom 4:3 (Abraham believed).
**Relationship to other evidence:** Even OT examples of righteous conduct are grounded in faith. Abel's offering, Noah's ark-building — these were acts of faith that expressed their trust in God. The forensic and character dimensions worked together even before the cross.

### James 2:20-26 — Faith Without Works Is Dead
**Context:** James arguing against empty profession of faith.
**Direct statement:** "Was not Abraham our father justified by works, when he had offered Isaac his son upon the altar? Seest thou how faith wrought with his works, and by works was faith made perfect?... Ye see then how that by works a man is justified, and not by faith only."
**Original language:** dikaioo (G1344) — "justified." James uses the same verb as Paul but in a different sense. For Paul, "justified" means "declared righteous by God" (the initial verdict). For James, "justified" means "shown to be righteous" (the visible demonstration). Jas 2:22 — "faith wrought with (sunergei) his works" — faith and works cooperate.
**Cross-references:** Gen 15:6 (quoted by both Paul and James) and Gen 22 (offering of Isaac, cited by James).
**Relationship to other evidence:** James does not contradict Paul. Paul speaks of the ground of justification (faith, not works). James speaks of the evidence of justification (genuine faith produces works). Dead faith — faith without works — is not real faith. This confirms that the character dimension is essential: genuine faith always produces righteous conduct.

### 1 John 1:7-9 — Confession and Cleansing
**Context:** John's opening instructions on fellowship with God.
**Direct statement:** "If we walk in the light... the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin... If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just (dikaios, G1342) to forgive us our sins, and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness (adikia, G93)."
**Original language:** dikaios (G1342) — God is "just/righteous" in forgiving. adikia (G93) — "unrighteousness." From the canonical word study (G93-adikia.md), adikia is the primary Greek antonym of dikaiosyne. God is righteous IN forgiving — meaning forgiveness is not a violation of justice but an expression of it (because of the blood of Christ).
**Cross-references:** Rom 3:26 — "just and the justifier." Zech 3:4 — "I have caused thine iniquity to pass from thee."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse addresses how righteousness is maintained. Believers stumble, but confession and God's faithful cleansing restore them. The mechanism: walking in light (character) + confession when we fail (relational) + God's righteous forgiveness (forensic). This is not Eze 18:24 (settled turning away) but ongoing repentance within the covenant.

### 1 John 3:7 — He That Does Righteousness Is Righteous
**Context:** John distinguishing the children of God from the children of the devil.
**Direct statement:** "Little children, let no man deceive you: he that doeth righteousness is righteous, even as he is righteous."
**Original language:** dikaiosyne (G1343 — "righteousness") + dikaios (G1342 — "righteous"). The one who does (poion, present active participle — ongoing action) righteousness IS righteous. The doing proves the being.
**Cross-references:** Eze 18:5-9 — detailed catalog of righteous conduct. Jas 2:24 — justified by works. Matt 5:20 — righteousness must exceed that of the Pharisees.
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the strongest character-dimension verse in the NT. Righteous identity is not separated from righteous conduct. You are righteous if you do righteousness. This does not contradict forensic justification (the verdict enables the doing) but insists that genuine righteousness always appears in conduct.

### 1 John 3:10 — Manifest by Conduct
**Context:** Continuation of the children of God vs. children of the devil theme.
**Direct statement:** "Whosoever doeth not righteousness is not of God, neither he that loveth not his brother."
**Original language:** dikaiosyne (G1343) and agape (G26, love). Doing righteousness and loving one's brother are the twin tests of belonging to God.
**Cross-references:** Gal 5:6 — "faith which worketh by love." 1 John 4:8 — "God is love."
**Relationship to other evidence:** Reinforces 1 John 3:7. Righteousness is visible. The character dimension cannot be separated from genuine standing with God.

### 2 Peter 2:20-22 — The Way of Righteousness
**Context:** Peter warning about false teachers who escaped the world but returned to it.
**Direct statement:** "It had been better for them not to have known the way of righteousness, than, after they have known it, to turn from the holy commandment delivered unto them."
**Original language:** dikaiosyne (G1343) — "the way of righteousness." The phrase "way of righteousness" describes a path, a way of life — the relational and character dimensions. They knew it, walked in it, then turned away.
**Cross-references:** Eze 18:24 — righteous turning away. Heb 10:26-29 — willful sin. Heb 6:4-6 — impossible to renew.
**Relationship to other evidence:** Confirms that righteousness involves ongoing walking (relational faithfulness) and that turning away from it has severe consequences. The standing is real but can be forfeited.

### Revelation 7:9,13-14 — Robes Washed White
**Context:** The great multitude before the throne.
**Direct statement:** "These are they which came out of great tribulation, and have washed their robes, and made them white in the blood of the Lamb."
**Original language:** eleukonan (G3021, "made white") — the robes are literally whitened in blood. This paradox — made clean through blood — echoes the atonement theme.
**Cross-references:** Isa 1:18 — "though your sins be as scarlet, they shall be as white as snow." Rev 19:8 — fine linen = dikaiomata. Zech 3:3-5 — garment exchange.
**Relationship to other evidence:** The garment imagery continues. The robes are washed (forensic cleansing through the blood) and made white (character transformation). The garment represents real righteousness obtained through Christ's sacrifice.

### Revelation 19:8 — Fine Linen Is the Righteous Acts of Saints
**Context:** The marriage supper of the Lamb. The Bride is prepared.
**Direct statement:** "To her was granted that she should be arrayed in fine linen, clean and white: for the fine linen is the righteousness (dikaiomata) of saints."
**Original language:** edothe (Aorist Passive of didomi, G1325 — "it was granted") — divine passive: God grants the garment. dikaiomata (G1345, PLURAL of dikaioma — "righteous acts/deeds"). From the canonical word study (G1345-dikaioma.md), the plural form means concrete righteous deeds, not abstract righteousness (dikaiosyne). The fine linen is composed of actual righteous acts. Yet these acts are "granted" by God (divine passive).
**Cross-references:** Job 29:14 — putting on righteousness. Isa 61:10 — God clothing with the robe of righteousness. Eph 2:10 — "created in Christ Jesus unto good works, which God hath before ordained that we should walk in them."
**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the climactic garment text. It holds forensic and character dimensions together. The garment is granted (forensic — divine passive, God provides) and is composed of righteous deeds (character — actual conduct). The righteous acts are real, but they are enabled by God. The wedding garment is not a fiction covering unchanged sinners — it is genuine righteousness that God made possible and the saints lived out.

## Patterns Identified

- **Pattern 1: Tsadaq/dikaioo is primarily courtroom vocabulary.** Every stem of tsadaq operates in forensic contexts. Deu 25:1 is the model: lawsuit → judgment → the court declares the righteous righteous and the wicked wicked. Exo 23:7 and Pro 17:15 set the divine boundary. Isa 50:8 and Rom 8:33-34 apply it. Dikaioo carries this forward — Louw-Nida 56.005 classifies it under Courts: Judge, Condemn, Acquit. The voice distribution (passive ~30x, active ~9x) shows justification as something received from God, not achieved. Supported by: Deu 25:1, Exo 23:7, Pro 17:15, Isa 50:8, Psa 51:4, Rom 3:24-26, Rom 4:5, Rom 8:33-34, Gal 2:16, Dan 8:14.

- **Pattern 2: Righteousness has a relational/covenant dimension that cannot be reduced to forensic verdict.** tsedaqah and mishpat appear together 46 times in the OT. Righteousness is covenant faithfulness — doing what the covenant requires, walking with God, loving checed. This is not merit-based earning but faithful participation in the covenant relationship. Supported by: Gen 18:19, Deu 6:25, Psa 103:17-18, Mic 6:8, Jer 31:31-34, Luk 1:6, Hab 2:4.

- **Pattern 3: Righteousness involves actual conduct — the character dimension.** The Bible consistently describes the righteous as those who DO righteous things. Eze 18:5-9 gives the most detailed catalog. 1 John 3:7 states it plainly: "he that doeth righteousness is righteous." The garment imagery (Job 29:14, Rev 19:8) confirms that righteousness is worn in the form of actual deeds. This is not self-righteousness (Isa 64:6) but God-enabled conduct (Rev 19:8 — "it was granted"). Supported by: Gen 6:9, Eze 18:5-9, 1 John 3:7, 1 John 3:10, Matt 5:20, Job 29:14, Rev 19:8, Tit 2:11-12, Gal 5:22-23.

- **Pattern 4: Righteousness is received through faith and imputation, not earned by human effort.** Gen 15:6 establishes the mechanism: believe → God reckons → righteousness. Rom 3:20 says law-works cannot produce the verdict. Rom 4:4-5 contrasts working (earning) with believing (receiving). Phil 3:9 rejects self-generated righteousness for "the righteousness which is of God by faith." The passive voice of dikaioo confirms: the sinner receives, God acts. Supported by: Gen 15:6, Psa 32:1-2, Rom 3:20-22,24, Rom 4:3-8, Gal 2:16, Phil 3:8-9, 2 Cor 5:21, Tit 3:7.

- **Pattern 5: Righteous standing can be lost through deliberate, sustained turning away.** Eze 18:24 is the clearest statement: the righteous who turns away loses all previous righteousness. Heb 10:26 specifies the condition: willful, ongoing sinning (present participle) after receiving knowledge. 2 Pet 2:20-22 says the end is worse than the beginning. The key is the settled, persistent nature of the turning — not isolated failure (which 1 John 1:7-9 addresses through confession). Supported by: Eze 18:24, Heb 10:26-29, 2 Pet 2:20-22, Gal 5:4, Eze 33:15.

- **Pattern 6: The three dimensions of righteousness interconnect and cannot be separated.** Forensic justification (verdict) leads to relational restoration (peace with God, Rom 5:1) which produces character transformation (walking in the Spirit, Rom 8:4). Conversely, character evidence (doing righteousness, 1 John 3:7) confirms forensic status, and relational faithfulness (covenant keeping, Deu 6:25) is counted as righteousness. The garment imagery holds all three together: God provides (forensic), the believer receives within relationship (relational), and the garment is composed of actual deeds (character). Supported by: Rom 5:1 + 8:1-4, Gen 15:6 + 18:19 + Eze 18:5-9, Isa 61:10 + Rev 19:8, 1 Cor 6:11, Jer 31:31-34.

## Word Study Integration

The Hebrew and Greek word studies reveal connections that English translation obscures.

**The tsadaq root family (530+ OT occurrences)** functions as a single semantic domain. tsaddiyq (adjective, 210x) describes the righteous person. tsedaqah (noun, 165x) names the quality or act of righteousness. tsedeq (noun, 119x) names the abstract standard. tsadaq (verb, 41x) expresses the action of being righteous or being declared righteous. The verb's five stems encode different dimensions:

- **Qal** (22x): to BE righteous — the state. "Shall mortal man be more just than God?" (Job 4:17)
- **Hiphil** (12x): to DECLARE righteous — the verdict. Deu 25:1, Isa 53:11, Exo 23:7.
- **Niphal** (1x): to BE DECLARED righteous / receive vindication — Dan 8:14 only. The sanctuary receives the verdict passively.
- **Piel** (5x): to prove right by comparison — "Sodom hath justified thy sister" (Eze 16:52).
- **Hithpael** (1x): to clear oneself — "How shall we justify ourselves?" (Gen 44:16).

The Niphal of Dan 8:14 is deliberately unique. The grammar reference (stem-niphal.md) classifies it as simple passive — the sanctuary does not vindicate itself but receives vindication from an external judge. This single occurrence carries the weight of the entire series.

**The Greek dik- family (234+ NT occurrences)** maps tightly to the tsadaq family through the LXX. tsaddiyq → dikaios with PMI 32.40. tsedaqah → dikaiosyne with 129 co-occurrences. tsadaq → dikaioo with PMI 26.99. The translation relationship study (TR-tsadaq-dikaioo.md) confirms this is one of the tightest Hebrew-Greek mappings in the entire LXX. When Paul writes dikaioo, he uses the very word the LXX translators chose for tsadaq.

English translation hides a key distinction: **dikaioma vs. dikaiosyne.** Both are translated "righteousness" in some contexts, but dikaioma (G1345) means a concrete righteous act, requirement, or regulation, while dikaiosyne (G1343) means the abstract quality or state. Rev 19:8 uses dikaiomata (plural) — righteous deeds — not dikaiosyne. The wedding garment is made of actual deeds, not abstract status. But Luk 1:6 uses dikaiomata for "ordinances" — the regulations Zacharias and Elisabeth walked in. The word carries both senses: the righteous requirement AND the righteous deed done in response.

**The imputation vocabulary** (chashab H2803 → logizomai G3049) is accounting language. Gen 15:6 uses chashab; Rom 4:3 uses logizomai. Both mean to reckon, credit, count. This is the mechanism of forensic justification — God credits righteousness to the believer's account. logizomai appears 8 times in Rom 4 alone, always in the context of reckoning righteousness.

**The antonym pair** tsaddiyq/rasha (81 co-occurrences) and its verbal equivalent tsadaq/rasha (Hiphil forms in Deu 25:1) establishes the binary courtroom framework: righteous or wicked, acquitted or condemned, justified or sentenced. The Greek equivalent is dikaiosis vs. katakrima (Rom 5:18) — justification vs. condemnation. There is no middle category. In the courtroom, you walk out vindicated or condemned.

**checed (H2617, 241 occurrences)** anchors the relational dimension. Paired with tsedaqah and emeth, checed describes the covenant loyalty that God extends and that He expects in return. Mic 6:8 asks for love of checed alongside mishpat (justice). This is not forensic verdict language — it is relationship language. Yet the relational dimension of righteousness is inseparable from the forensic and character dimensions.

## Cross-Testament Connections

The OT and NT share a single vocabulary for righteousness through the LXX bridge. Several key connections stand out:

**Gen 15:6 → Rom 4:3 / Gal 3:6 / Jas 2:23:** The Genesis imputation event is quoted verbatim in all three NT letters. Paul and James both cite the same verse to make different but complementary points (Paul: the ground is faith; James: genuine faith shows works).

**Isa 50:8 → Rom 8:33-34:** The Servant's courtroom vindication language reappears in Paul's triumphant conclusion. "He is near that justifieth me; who will contend?" becomes "God that justifieth. Who condemns?" The vocabulary transfers completely.

**Isa 53:11 → 2 Cor 5:21 / Rom 5:19:** The righteous Servant bearing iniquities to justify many becomes "he made him to be sin for us, that we might become the righteousness of God" and "by the obedience of one shall many be made righteous." The mechanism stays constant: substitutionary bearing of iniquity produces righteous standing.

**Hab 2:4 → Rom 1:17 / Gal 3:11 / Heb 10:38:** "The just shall live by his faith" crosses from OT to NT three times, anchoring the justification-by-faith doctrine.

**Deu 25:1 → Rom 8:33-34:** The earthly courtroom model (declare righteous / declare wicked) becomes the heavenly courtroom model (God justifies / who condemns?).

**Lev 16:30 → Dan 9:24 → Rom 3:25-26 → Dan 8:14:** The progression from atonement vocabulary (kaphar/taher) to righteousness vocabulary (tsedeq/dikaios) crosses both testaments. The earthly Day of Atonement (Lev 16) reaches cleansing. Dan 9:24 bridges to everlasting righteousness. Rom 3:25-26 shows the mercy seat (hilasterion) producing God's righteous justification. Dan 8:14 pronounces the verdict.

**Eze 18:5-9 → 1 John 3:7:** The OT ethical catalog for the righteous person finds its NT echo: "he that doeth righteousness is righteous." Both insist that righteous conduct defines the righteous person.

**Zech 3:1-5 → Rev 7:14 / 19:8:** The courtroom garment exchange — filthy garments removed, clean garments given — develops into the white robes washed in the Lamb's blood and the fine linen of the saints' righteous deeds. The garment imagery spans both testaments.

## Difficult or Complicating Passages

### Romans 4:5 vs. Exodus 23:7 — God Justifying the Ungodly
**The problem:** Exo 23:7 says God will NOT justify the wicked. Rom 4:5 says God "justifieth the ungodly." This seems like a direct contradiction.
**Analysis:** The resolution lies in Isa 53:11 and Rom 3:25-26. God does not simply ignore wickedness. He deals with it through the righteous Servant who bears iniquities (Isa 53:11). The propitiation (hilasterion, Rom 3:25) satisfies justice. So God can justify the ungodly without violating His own principle because the basis of the verdict has changed — not the ungodly person's merit but Christ's righteous act (dikaioma, Rom 5:18). God remains just (dikaios) while being the justifier (dikaiounton, Rom 3:26). The ungodly who believe are not declared righteous while remaining unchanged — they are transformed (2 Cor 5:21, "that we might become the righteousness of God").

### James 2:24 vs. Romans 3:28 — Justified by Works vs. by Faith
**The problem:** Paul says "a man is justified by faith without the deeds of the law" (Rom 3:28). James says "by works a man is justified, and not by faith only" (Jas 2:24). They appear to flatly contradict.
**Analysis:** Paul and James use "justified" in different senses. Paul means the initial verdict — how a sinner is acquitted before God. The ground is faith. James means the visible demonstration — how a person shows their faith is real. James explicitly says "faith wrought with his works, and by works was faith made perfect" (Jas 2:22). For James, "justified" means "shown to be righteous" — vindicated as genuine. Abraham believed (Gen 15:6, cited by Paul) and then demonstrated his faith by offering Isaac (Gen 22, cited by James). Both are true because they address different moments: the verdict (Paul) and the evidence (James).

### Deuteronomy 6:25 vs. Romans 3:20 — Righteousness by Obedience?
**The problem:** Deu 6:25 says "it shall be our righteousness, if we observe to do all these commandments." Rom 3:20 says "by the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified." Does obedience count as righteousness or not?
**Analysis:** Deu 6:25 speaks within the covenant relationship. Israel was already redeemed from Egypt by grace (Deu 6:23). Obedience is not earning the covenant but faithfully living within it. Rom 3:20 speaks of the initial ground of justification — law-keeping cannot produce the verdict of acquittal. These address different questions: Deu 6:25 asks "what does covenant faithfulness look like?" (answer: obedience). Rom 3:20 asks "what is the basis of acquittal?" (answer: not law-works but faith). The relational dimension (Deu 6:25) and the forensic dimension (Rom 3:20) are complementary, not contradictory.

### 1 John 1:8 vs. 1 John 3:6,9 — Cannot Sin vs. Have Sin
**The problem:** 1 John 1:8 says "if we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves." But 1 John 3:6 says "whosoever abideth in him sinneth not" and 3:9 says "whosoever is born of God doth not commit sin... he cannot sin."
**Analysis:** The verb tenses matter. In 1 John 3:6,9, the present tense means habitual, ongoing action. "Does not sin" means "does not practice sin as a way of life." "Cannot sin" means "cannot go on sinning as a settled pattern." 1 John 1:8 acknowledges that believers still stumble — anyone who claims to have no sin at all is self-deceived. The solution: walking in the light (1 John 1:7) includes honest acknowledgment of sin (1 John 1:9) while not making sin a pattern of life (1 John 3:6,9). The distinction is between stumbling (addressed by confession) and habitual sinning (incompatible with being born of God).

### Ezekiel 18:24 and Hebrews 10:26-29 — Can the Righteous Really Be Lost?
**The problem:** Some readings of forensic justification imply that once declared righteous, a person cannot lose that standing. But Eze 18:24 says the righteous who turns away will die in their sin. Heb 10:26-29 says there remains no more sacrifice for willful sinners.
**Analysis:** Both passages describe a specific kind of turning — not an isolated failure but a settled, sustained rebellion. Eze 18:24: the righteous "turneth away" (shuv — a complete reversal). Heb 10:26: the present participle hamartanonton describes ongoing, continuous, deliberate sinning. The warnings are not about believers who stumble and repent (1 John 1:7-9 covers that). They are about those who receive the truth and then comprehensively reject it. The relational dimension explains this: righteousness is covenant faithfulness. Breaking the covenant by settled rebellion ends the righteous standing. Previous acts of righteousness do not provide immunity from a present reality of willful sin.

## Preliminary Synthesis

The weight of evidence points toward a three-dimensional understanding of biblical righteousness:

**First, the forensic (declared) dimension is firmly established.** The tsadaq/dikaioo vocabulary is courtroom language (Louw-Nida 56.005, Deu 25:1, Rom 8:33-34). Righteousness is received through imputation by faith (Gen 15:6, Rom 4:3-5), not earned by law-works (Rom 3:20, Gal 2:16). The voice distribution of dikaioo (~30 passive, ~9 active) confirms that justification is something done TO the sinner BY God.

**Second, the relational (covenant faithfulness) dimension is equally biblical.** tsedaqah paired with mishpat 46 times describes covenant behavior. Deu 6:25 counts obedience as righteousness within the covenant. Mic 6:8 names the requirements: justice, covenant loyalty (checed), and walking with God. Hab 2:4 links the just person to ongoing faithfulness (emunah). Luk 1:6 calls Zacharias and Elisabeth righteous because they walked in God's commandments.

**Third, the character (conduct) dimension is not optional.** Eze 18:5-9 defines the righteous by what they do. 1 John 3:7 says plainly: the one who does righteousness IS righteous. Rev 19:8 says the wedding garment IS the righteous deeds of the saints. Matt 5:20 requires exceeding righteousness. Grace itself teaches righteous living (Tit 2:11-12).

**The three dimensions are connected, not competing.** Forensic justification (the verdict) restores the covenant relationship (relational) and produces transformed conduct (character). Conversely, conduct confirms standing (Jas 2:24) and covenant faithfulness is itself counted as righteousness (Deu 6:25). No single dimension is sufficient alone. Forensic without character is a "dead faith" (Jas 2:26). Character without forensic is self-righteousness (Isa 64:6, Phil 3:9). Relational without forensic has no ground. Forensic without relational has no context.

**For the series:** Dan 8:14's nitsdaq draws on the full range of this vocabulary. The sanctuary receives a passive verdict (forensic), within the context of God's covenant relationship with His people (relational), and the verdict addresses character (the sanctuary's holiness and proper function). A purely forensic reading of nitsdaq misses two dimensions. The learner needs all three to understand what the vindication verdict means.
