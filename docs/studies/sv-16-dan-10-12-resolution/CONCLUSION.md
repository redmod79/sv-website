# Daniel 10-12: The Resolution of the Vision

## Question
Daniel 10-12 relationship to earlier visions. biyn chain resolution. 1290/1335 as sealed signposts. Pastoral purpose of 12:13.

## Summary Answer
Daniel 10-12 is not a separate vision. It is the completion of the vision that began in Daniel 8. The biyn chain — 19 occurrences of the verb biyn (H995) across Dan 8-12 — tracks the process of understanding from Daniel's initial desire (8:15) through failure (8:27) through resolution of the mar'eh at 10:1, a second failure on new content at 12:8, and finally to the discriminating promise that the maskilim will understand at 12:10. The 1290 and 1335 are sealed time periods Daniel cannot grasp — signposts designed for the wise in the time of the end. Dan 12:13 closes the book with a triple promise: rest (death), stand (resurrection), lot (inheritance).

## Key Verses

> **Daniel 10:1** "In the third year of Cyrus king of Persia a thing was revealed unto Daniel, whose name was called Belteshazzar; and the thing was true, but the time appointed was long: and he understood the thing, and had understanding of the vision."

> **Daniel 8:27** "And I Daniel fainted, and was sick certain days; afterward I rose up, and did the king's business; and I was astonished at the vision, but none understood it."

> **Daniel 12:8** "And I heard, but I understood not: then said I, O my Lord, what shall be the end of these things?"

> **Daniel 12:10** "Many shall be purified, and made white, and tried; but the wicked shall do wickedly: and none of the wicked shall understand; but the wise shall understand."

> **Daniel 12:3** "And they that be wise shall shine as the brightness of the firmament; and they that turn many to righteousness as the stars for ever and ever."

> **Daniel 12:13** "But go thou thy way till the end be: for thou shalt rest, and stand in thy lot at the end of the days."

> **Daniel 10:12** "Then said he unto me, Fear not, Daniel: for from the first day that thou didst set thine heart to understand, and to chasten thyself before thy God, thy words were heard, and I am come for thy words."

> **Daniel 12:1** "And at that time shall Michael stand up, the great prince which standeth for the children of thy people: and there shall be a time of trouble, such as never was since there was a nation even to that same time: and at that time thy people shall be delivered, every one that shall be found written in the book."

## Analysis

### The biyn Chain: Structural Backbone of Daniel 8-12

The verb biyn (H995, "to separate mentally, to distinguish, to understand") appears 19 times in Daniel — 11.7% of its 162 OT occurrences, the highest density of any biblical book (canonical H995 study). This is not random distribution. These 19 occurrences form a carefully constructed narrative arc that tracks the process of understanding itself across five chapters.

The chain begins with desire. At Dan 8:15, Daniel has just witnessed the chazon — the ram, the goat, the horn, the "how long?" question, the 2300-evening-morning answer. He responds: va-avaqeshah biynah, "I sought understanding" (biynah, H998, noun). This is active, intensified seeking — the Piel of baqash. Daniel wants to know what the vision means.

That desire triggers a commission. At Dan 8:16, a voice commands Gabriel: haven et-hallaz ha-mar'eh, "cause this man to understand the mar'eh" (Hiphil.Impv.2ms). Two things matter here. First, the commission targets the mar'eh specifically — not the chazon broadly. The mar'eh is the 2300 evening-morning time prophecy, identified by Dan 8:26 as "the vision of the evening and the morning" (sv-05, sv-12). Second, the Hiphil stem is causative: Gabriel must produce understanding in Daniel. This is a mission with a measurable outcome.

The mission fails. Gabriel explains the chazon's symbols — ram is Medo-Persia, goat is Greece, horn is the persecuting power (8:20-25). But he does NOT explain the mar'eh. Dan 8:27 records the result: ein mevin, "no one understanding" (Hiphil.Ptcp, negated). The verb biyn appears in negated participial form — a settled state of non-understanding. The commission from 8:16 is unfulfilled.

Daniel takes his own initiative. At 9:2, he "understood by books" (biyn, Qal.Perf.1s) the number of years in Jeremiah's prophecy about the 70-year captivity. Daniel can understand prophetic time periods when they come from Scripture. This makes the failure to understand the 2300 more pointed. It is not that Daniel lacks ability. The mar'eh was deliberately sealed.

Gabriel returns. At 9:22, vayyaskileni (Hiphil of sakal — "he caused me to understand") and biynah (noun) appear together. Gabriel came to deliver understanding. At 9:23, the dual imperative: biyn ba-dabar ve-haven ba-mar'eh — "understand the word AND gain insight into the mar'eh" (grammar reference dan-9-22-23.md). Both objects — word and mar'eh — are targets of understanding. Gabriel then delivers the 70-weeks prophecy (9:24-27), which provides the starting point the mar'eh lacked. The 70 weeks are "cut from" the 2300 (sv-14, chathak). The starting point — 457 BC — now makes the 2300 calculable.

The resolution arrives at 10:1. u-vin (Qal.Perf.3ms) et-ha-dabar, u-biynah lo ba-mar'eh — "he understood the word, and understanding was to him in the mar'eh." The grammar reference (dan-10-1.md) confirms: this is completed action (Qal perfect) plus a stative nominal clause expressing settled possession. The Hiphil mission from 8:16 — "cause this man to understand the mar'eh" — has succeeded. Daniel now possesses understanding. The shift from Hiphil (8:16, causative — someone must produce understanding) to Qal (10:1, simple — Daniel now has it) marks the transition from dependence on Gabriel's instruction to personal possession of insight.

But the chain does not end there. At 10:11, haven reappears (Hiphil.Impv.2ms) — "understand the words that I speak." At 10:12, lehavin (Hiphil.InfCon) — Daniel's heart was "set to understand." At 10:14, lehavinekha (Hiphil.InfCon + 2ms suffix) — the speaker came "to cause you to understand." The biyn vocabulary continues because the chazon is still unfolding. The mar'eh track closed at 10:1. The chazon track remains open (10:14: "the vision [chazon] is yet for many days").

Then comes the second failure. At 12:8, after hearing the time-times-and-half answer and the condition about shattering the holy people's power, Daniel confesses: lo avin (Qal.Impf.1s), "I do not understand." The imperfective aspect signals ongoing, unresolved inability. Daniel understood the mar'eh (10:1). He does NOT understand the new content — the 1260 and the additional time periods about to be given (1290, 1335). These are sealed (12:9). They are not for him.

The chain arrives at its destination in 12:10. Two uses of biyn in a single verse, divided by a moral line: lo yavinu kol-resha'im — "none of the wicked shall understand" (Qal.Impf.3mp, negated); ve-ha-maskilim yavinu — "but the wise shall understand" (Qal.Impf.3mp, affirmed). The chain that began with Daniel's personal desire to understand ends with a promise — and a restriction. Understanding is not for everyone. It is for the maskilim, the wise ones who have been purified. The wicked cannot access it. Character determines comprehension.

### Daniel's Mourning: Grief from Understanding

Dan 10:1 says Daniel understood. Dan 10:2-3 says he mourned for three weeks. The sequence matters. Daniel does not mourn because he is confused. He mourns because he grasps the scope of what the 2300 years contain.

The KJV renders tsaba gadol as "the time appointed was long." But the canonical word study (H6635) shows tsaba means host/warfare/service in nearly all of its 492 OT occurrences. The phrase means "great warfare." Daniel understood that the vision involves centuries of conflict — the horn's attack on God's people, the removal of the tamid, the casting down of truth (sv-06). This is not abstract knowledge. It is the kind of understanding that breaks a person.

His mourning includes three-week fasting: no lechem chamudoth ("pleasant bread" — bread of desirables, same chamad root as the chamudoth address), no flesh, no wine, no anointing (10:3). Dan 10:12 adds a detail: lehit'annoth — "to humble yourself" (Hithpael of 'anah). This is the same verb used for the Day of Atonement's "afflict your souls" (Lev 16:29,31). Daniel's self-affliction parallels the DOA requirement — the sorting event that separates participants from refusers. The DOA pattern (sv-04) creates a binary: those who humble themselves are cleansed; those who refuse are cut off. Dan 12:10's binary (maskilim understand, wicked do not) echoes this same structure.

The 21-day mourning runs exactly parallel to the 21-day cosmic conflict described in 10:13. Daniel prays on earth; a heavenly messenger is resisted by the prince of Persia for 21 days; Michael intervenes. The concurrence is not coincidental. It shows the connection between human prayer and heavenly action. Daniel's words "were heard" from day one (10:12, nishme'u — divine passive). The delay was not in heaven's response but in the cosmic opposition that had to be overcome.

Heaven's response to Daniel's grief is the most detailed prophecy in Scripture — Dan 11's ruler-by-ruler sweep through centuries of history. This is not information for its own sake. It is pastoral care. God shows Daniel: I know every ruler, every battle, every shift of power. Nothing will happen that I have not foreseen. The sovereign foreknowledge demonstrated in Dan 11 is the ANSWER to Daniel's grief about the tsaba gadol.

### The Triple Strengthening and the chamudoth Address

Heaven does not simply deliver information. It prepares the recipient. Three times in Dan 10, Daniel is physically touched and strengthened (10:10 — set on knees; 10:16 — lips touched, speech restored; 10:18 — strengthened by one like a man). Three times Daniel is addressed as ish chamudoth, "man of preciousnesses" (9:23, 10:11, 10:19). The canonical word study (H2530) shows this address is unique to Daniel — no other prophet receives it.

The triple pattern — three touches, three titles, three "fear not" assurances — reveals something about how heaven handles revelation. Prophecy is not data transfer. It carries weight. Daniel collapses physically (10:8-9,15,17). He loses strength. He cannot breathe (10:17, ruach). Each collapse is met with a touch and a word of comfort. The progression builds Daniel's capacity to receive what is coming.

The double imperative in 10:19 — chazaq chazaq, "be strong, be strong" — is followed by shalom lekha, "peace to you" (H7965). The canonical chazaq study (H2388) shows the Piel form (strengthening another) in 10:18 and the Hithpael (self-strengthening) in 10:19. Daniel receives strength externally, then internalizes it. Only then does he say: "Let my lord speak; for thou hast strengthened me."

### The chazon/mar'eh Resolution

The two-track vision system (sv-05, sv-12) reaches its pivot at Dan 10:1-14. The mar'eh — the 2300 evening-morning time prophecy — has been the target of Gabriel's mission since 8:16. The mission failed at 8:27. Gabriel resumed it at 9:22-23. Dan 10:1 records the resolution: biynah lo ba-mar'eh, "understanding was to him in the mar'eh."

But 10:14 immediately shows that the chazon remains open: "yet the chazon is for many days." The chazon is the broad prophetic panorama — the ram, the goat, the horn, the sanctuary being cast down, the vindication (8:14). The mar'eh (time element) has been understood. The chazon (the full sweep of events) continues to unfold. Dan 11-12 IS the chazon's continuation — a detailed explanation of what the symbolic panorama of Dan 8 meant in concrete historical terms.

This confirms the structural argument: Dan 11 begins with Persia (11:2), just as Dan 8 began with the ram (= Persia, 8:20). The same starting point, the same sequence of powers, vastly more historical detail. The biyn chain and the chazon/mar'eh tracking system together prove that Dan 8-12 is one continuous, interconnected narrative — not four separate visions.

### The 1290 and 1335: Sealed Signposts

After Daniel confesses he does not understand (12:8), two additional time periods emerge. The 1290 days (12:11) are counted from "the time that the daily sacrifice [tamid] shall be taken away, and the abomination that maketh desolate set up." The 1335 days (12:12) carry a unique blessing: ashrey ha-mechakkeh, "blessed is the one who waits and comes to" them.

These periods extend beyond the 1260 baseline established in 12:7 (sv-15). The 1260-year period (538-1798 AD) is the foundational period of the horn's dominance. The 1290 adds 30 years (reaching 1828). The 1335 adds 75 beyond the 1290 (reaching 1843/1844).

The 1335's convergence with the 2300's terminus is the key. sv-15 established that 457 BC + 2300 = 1844 AD. The 1335, counted from a different starting point, arrives at the same date. Two independent time calculations reaching the same terminus is a verification — it confirms the prophetic framework. The beatitude attached to the 1335 is unique in Daniel — no other time period in the book receives a blessing. "Blessed is he that waiteth and cometh" to this date. The one who waits faithfully to 1844 receives a blessing because that is when the sanctuary begins to be vindicated (nitsdaq, 8:14) — the event the entire series has been building toward.

These time periods are part of the sealed content Daniel cannot understand. They are designed for the maskilim of the time of the end (12:10). Daniel could calculate the 2300 (he had the starting point from the 70 weeks). But the 1290 and 1335 require additional information — starting points Daniel did not possess. They are signposts for a generation Daniel would not live to see.

### The Purification Triad and Discriminating Understanding (12:10)

Dan 12:10 uses three purification verbs: yitbararu (Hithpael of barar — reflexive purifying), yitlabbenu (Hithpael of laban — reflexive whitening), yitsarfu (Niphal of tsaraph — passive refining). These are the same three roots that appear in Dan 11:35: "some of them of understanding shall fall, to try them [tsaraph], and to purge [barar], and to make them white [laban]."

The recycling is deliberate. 11:35 describes the maskilim's historical experience — they suffer, they are refined through persecution. 12:10 describes the eschatological reality — "many shall be purified, and made white, and tried." The same vocabulary connects the two. The historical refining (11:35) produces the eschatological identity (12:10). Those who endure the process become the ones who understand.

But the stems shift. In 11:35, the verbs are in Hiphil/Piel (active/causative) — external forces refine the maskilim. In 12:10, the verbs are Hithpael/Niphal (reflexive/passive) — the many purify themselves and are refined. The shift suggests maturation. What began as something done TO them becomes something they participate in actively. The refining is not just suffered. It is embraced.

Then comes the discrimination. "The wicked shall do wickedly: and none of the wicked shall understand (lo yavinu); but the wise (maskilim) shall understand (yavinu)" (12:10). The biyn chain's final verdict splits humanity into two categories based on character. Understanding is not available to everyone. The wicked are defined by their inability to understand — not because the content is too complex, but because their character blocks access. This echoes Lev 16's DOA sorting: those who afflict their souls participate; those who refuse are cut off (sv-04). The same moral binary operates at the eschatological level.

### The matsdiqey-nitsdaq Connection: The Human Dimension of Vindication

Dan 12:3 uses two key terms for the maskilim: ha-maskilim (Hiphil.Ptcp of sakal — "the wise ones") and matsdiqey ha-rabbim (Hiphil.Ptcp.Cst of tsadaq — "those causing the many to be righteous"). The second term — matsdiqey — shares the root tsadaq (H6663) with nitsdaq in 8:14.

The connection runs deep. nitsdaq (Niphal, 8:14) = the sanctuary receives vindication. matsdiqey (Hiphil, 12:3) = the maskilim cause many to be righteous. Same root. Different stems. Complementary functions. The Niphal is passive/receptive — the sanctuary is vindicated by God. The Hiphil is causative — the maskilim bring others into that righteousness. sv-01 showed the tsadaq family has three dimensions: forensic (declared), relational (covenant faithfulness), and character (conduct). The maskilim operate in all three — they understand the verdict (forensic), they maintain covenant loyalty under persecution (relational), and they live what they teach (character).

The maskilim's role in Dan 11:33 reinforces this. "They that understand among the people shall instruct many." The maskilim both understand and teach. They are not passive recipients of sealed knowledge. They are active agents who help the deceived recognize which system was real — the heavenly ministry (tamid) or the earthly counterfeit. This is the human dimension of the divine verdict. God declares the sanctuary vindicated (8:14). The maskilim bring people into that vindication (12:3). Matt 13:43 confirms Jesus validated this concept: "Then shall the righteous shine forth as the sun in the kingdom of their Father."

### Dan 12:1-3: Four Layers of Divine Response

Dan 12:1-3 provides four layers of eschatological comfort, answering Daniel's grief about the great warfare:

First, Michael stands up (12:1). ya'amod (Qal.Impf.3ms) — a specific future rising. ha-'omed (Qal.Ptcp.ms) — a continuous protective stance. Michael's title reaches its peak: "the great prince" (ha-sar ha-gadol), the third and highest of his three titles in Dan 10-12. He stands for "the children of thy people" — Israel. The deliverance is conditional: "every one that shall be found written in the book." The book of life runs from Exo 32:32-33 (Moses interceding) through Dan 12:1 to Rev 20:12 (final judgment). Being written in the book = being delivered through the time of trouble. Jer 30:7 provides the closest parallel: "the time of Jacob's trouble; but he shall be saved out of it."

Second, the time of trouble (12:1). "Such as never was since there was a nation even to that same time." This is not generic distress. It is unprecedented, climactic, and final. It marks the boundary between history and eschatological resolution.

Third, resurrection (12:2). "Many of them that sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt." This is the clearest OT statement of individual bodily resurrection with dual outcomes. Isa 26:19 gives restoration-resurrection. Job 19:25-27 gives personal hope. Ezk 37:1-14 gives national metaphor. Dan 12:2 adds what none of them have: the negative outcome. Some awake to eternal life. Some awake to eternal contempt. John 5:28-29 expands this: "all that are in the graves shall hear his voice."

Fourth, the maskilim's reward (12:3). They shine as the firmament's brightness. They turn many to righteousness. They are like stars forever and ever. This is the eternal consequence of faithfulness during the great warfare — the maskilim who endured persecution (11:33-35), who were purified (12:10), who taught others the truth (11:33), who participated in vindication (matsdiqey, 12:3) now receive their reward. The reward is not rest. It is radiance. They shine because they gave light. They endure forever because what they taught — the vindication of God's sanctuary — is an eternal truth.

### The Pastoral Purpose of 12:13

Dan 12:13 is the final verse of the book. It contains three promises in sequence: tanuach (Qal.Impf.2ms of nuach — "you shall rest"), ta'amod (Qal.Impf.2ms of 'amad — "you shall stand"), le-goralekha (goral + 2ms suffix — "to/for your lot").

The verb nuach (H5117, canonical study) carries the Deuteronomic promise of rest. Job 3:13,17 uses it for death-rest: "then had I been at rest," "there the weary be at rest." Exo 33:14 — "My presence shall go with thee, and I will give thee rest" (the cross-testament parallels tool confirmed this as the strongest OT parallel to Dan 12:13). Daniel's nuach is death-rest. He will die. He will sleep. The tsaba gadol will continue without him.

The verb 'amad (H5975, canonical study) threads through Dan 10-12 with different functions: physical posture (10:11), Michael's protective rising (12:1), and now Daniel's resurrection (12:13). The same verb that describes Michael "standing up" for God's people (12:1) describes Daniel "standing" at the end of days. Michael rises to deliver. Daniel rises to receive.

The noun goral (H1486, canonical study) bridges the literal land-lot tradition (Joshua's tribal allotments) to eschatological destiny. In Joshua, goral determined which tribe received which territory — it was a divinely guided allotment. In Dan 12:13, Daniel's goral is his divinely allotted portion at the end of days. The canonical study shows goral collocates with nachalah ("inheritance") 9 times — the lot determines the inheritance. Daniel will rise to receive his allotted inheritance.

The three terms form a sequence: go → rest (die) → stand (rise) → receive your lot (inheritance). This is pastoral closure on every level. For Daniel the man: you will die, but you will not be lost. You will rise. For Daniel the prophet: the things you do not understand are not for you; they are for the maskilim. But you have done your part. For Daniel the servant of God: the great warfare will be resolved. The sanctuary will be vindicated. You will be there — standing in your lot — when it is finished.

The tone is not merely informational. It is tender. lekh la-qets — "go to the end." This echoes Deu 31:6's charge: "Be strong and of a good courage." Gabriel (or the angelic speaker) is releasing Daniel from the burden of understanding everything. The sealed content is not his responsibility. His responsibility was to be faithful — and he was.

## Word Studies

The tsadaq root (H6663) connects 8:14 and 12:3 at the deepest level. nitsdaq (Niphal) = the sanctuary receives vindication from God. matsdiqey (Hiphil) = the maskilim cause many to be righteous. The English KJV obscures this: "cleansed" (8:14) and "turn to righteousness" (12:3) do not look related. In Hebrew, they share a root. The sanctuary's vindication and the maskilim's teaching ministry are two halves of one event — the divine verdict and the human response to it.

The biyn-sakal overlap at 12:10 is significant. Hebrew distinguishes biyn (analytical discernment) from sakal (practical wisdom/insight). The LXX flattens both to syniemi (canonical TR-biyn-syniemi, TR-sakal-syniemi). But Dan 12:10 deliberately uses BOTH in one verse: maskilim (sakal) yavinu (biyn). The wise act with practical insight AND separate mentally / discern. Both capacities are needed. The Greek reader misses this distinction.

The purification triad — barar (H1305, clarify/purify), laban (H3835, make white), tsaraph (H6884, refine by fire) — appears as a set in only two verses in the entire OT: Dan 11:35 and Dan 12:10. Nowhere else. This exclusive pairing confirms deliberate vocabulary recycling. The historical experience of the maskilim (refined through persecution) prefigures and prepares them for their eschatological role (purified and understanding).

The nuach-'amad-goral sequence in 12:13 draws from three distinct semantic fields: rest/settlement (nuach), standing/rising (amad), and lot/inheritance (goral). Together they create a complete eschatological sequence: death → resurrection → reward. Each word carries weight from its broader OT usage (nuach from the Sabbath and Deuteronomic rest traditions, 'amad from the standing-before-God tradition, goral from the land-allotment tradition). The combination is unique to this verse.

## Difficult Passages

**Dan 10:1 — timing of understanding.** The verse says Daniel understood, and three weeks of mourning follow. But does "understood" refer to understanding gained before the mourning (making the mourning a response to understanding) or after it (making the mourning a quest for understanding)? The grammar allows both readings. The prior study (dan10-mourning-dan11-response) argued for the "understood before mourning" reading based on the verse's prologue position. The text does not absolutely settle this — either reading is grammatically possible. However, the prologue position and the completed-action form (Qal.Perfect) favor the reading that Daniel already understood when the mourning began. He mourned because he understood, not in order to understand.

**Dan 12:2 — "many" vs. "all."** The text says "many" (rabbim) shall awake, not "all" (kol). John 5:28-29 says "all that are in the graves." Does Daniel describe a limited resurrection (a subset at the time of trouble) or a general resurrection (using rabbim as an inclusive Semitic idiom)? The text does not fully resolve this. The word rabbim can function as "the great multitude" in Semitic usage, but it remains grammatically distinct from kol. If Dan 12:2 describes a specific pre-advent resurrection of both righteous and wicked at the time of trouble, it would explain the "many" rather than "all." But the dual-outcome structure (some to life, some to contempt) has been read throughout history as describing the general resurrection. The tension is genuine.

**Dan 12:8 — what exactly doesn't Daniel understand?** The content immediately preceding 12:8 is the time-times-and-half answer (12:7) and the condition about completing the shattering of the holy people's power. The 1290 and 1335 come AFTER 12:8, in verses 11-12 — as further sealed content given in response to Daniel's question. So Daniel's "I understood not" refers primarily to the 1260 and the shattering condition, not the 1290/1335 (which haven't been given yet). His question "what shall be the end of these things?" prompts more sealed content rather than an explanation. This complicates the common reading that Daniel "didn't understand the 1290/1335" — he didn't understand what he had already heard, and the answer added even more that he could not grasp.

**Dan 12:13 — resurrection or prophetic legacy?** EGW interprets "stand in thy lot" as including Daniel's prophetic testimony being heard again in the time of the end. The Hebrew technically allows this: 'amad can mean "be established" or "take one's position," and goral can mean "destiny" beyond personal resurrection. But the most natural reading, given the sequential structure (go → rest → stand), treats nuach as death, 'amad as resurrection, and goral as inheritance after resurrection. A "dual reading" (personal resurrection AND enduring prophetic influence) is possible but not demanded by the text. The text itself focuses on Daniel personally: "thou shalt rest... thou shalt stand." The second-person singular pronouns keep this personal.

## Conclusion

Daniel 10-12 is the resolution of what began in Daniel 8. The chazon/mar'eh tracking system proves it. The biyn chain traces it. The vocabulary connections confirm it.

The mar'eh — the 2300 evening-morning time prophecy — reaches resolution at Dan 10:1. Daniel understood. The starting point from the 70 weeks (sv-13, sv-14), the calculation to 1844 (sv-15), the significance of vindication (sv-01, sv-14) — these are now in Daniel's grasp. The mission Gabriel received at 8:16 ("cause this man to understand the mar'eh") is complete.

The chazon — the broad prophetic panorama — continues. Dan 10:14 says so explicitly: "the chazon is yet for many days." Dan 11-12 unfolds the chazon in concrete historical detail. The same powers from Dan 8 (Persia, Greece, the horn) appear again, with centuries of additional detail. God's sovereign foreknowledge over every ruler and every event is heaven's answer to Daniel's grief about the great warfare.

The biyn chain tracks the whole process. 19 occurrences across five chapters. From desire (8:15) through failure (8:27) through human initiative (9:2) through resumed divine instruction (9:22-23) through resolution (10:1) through continued teaching (10:11-14) through second failure on new content (12:8) to the discriminating conclusion (12:10). The chain's destination is not Daniel. It is the maskilim of the time of the end — purified through trial (11:35, 12:10), understanding what Daniel could not (12:10), teaching others (11:33, 12:3), and participating in the vindication itself (matsdiqey, 12:3, same tsadaq root as nitsdaq, 8:14).

The 1290 and 1335 are sealed signposts. Daniel could not understand them (12:8-9). They extend beyond the 1260 baseline — the 1290 by 30 years, the 1335 by 75 years beyond the 1290. The 1335 converges with the 2300's terminus at 1844 (sv-15). Two independent time systems reaching the same date is a built-in verification. The unique beatitude on the 1335 ("blessed is he that waiteth") marks the convergence point as a moment of blessing — the beginning of what the series has been studying: the vindication of the sanctuary.

Dan 12:13 closes the book with pastoral precision. Go. Rest. Rise. Receive your lot. Daniel will die and sleep in death. The great warfare will rage on without him. But at the end of days — le-qets ha-yamin — he will stand. He will receive his divinely allotted portion. The Exodus promise of rest (Exo 33:14, "I will give thee rest") finds its eschatological fulfillment. The tsaba gadol will end. The sanctuary will be vindicated. And Daniel, the man of preciousnesses, will be there.
