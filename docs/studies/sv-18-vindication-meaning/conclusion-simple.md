# What Does "The Sanctuary Shall Be Vindicated" Mean?
## A Plain-English Summary

Daniel 8:14 ends with a famous phrase that the King James Bible translates as "then shall the sanctuary be cleansed." But there is a problem: the Hebrew word Daniel actually used does not mean "cleanse." It means something closer to "vindicate" or "be declared righteous" — a word from the courtroom, not the washroom. This study unpacks what that single word choice reveals about the entire story Daniel is telling.

---

## The Word That Changes Everything

In Hebrew, Daniel 8:14 uses the word *nitsdaq* (from the root *tsadaq*). In the Old Testament, *tsadaq* appears 41 times, and every single occurrence has a legal or moral meaning — declaring someone righteous, being proven right in court, or being acquitted of a charge. Not one of those 41 uses means ritual cleansing.

Daniel had perfectly good options if he wanted to say "cleansed." The Hebrew word *taher* (used 94 times in the Old Testament, 53 of them in Leviticus alone) is the standard word for ritual purification. The Day of Atonement in Leviticus 16 uses *taher*. Daniel did not choose it.

He also could have used *kaphar* — the word for atonement. He did not choose that either.

He chose *tsadaq*. And that choice is the key to everything.

The ancient Greek translation of the Old Testament (the Septuagint) confirms this. When the Greek translators rendered Daniel 8:14, they used *dikaiothesatai* — the future passive of *dikaioo*, meaning "to be justified" or "to be declared righteous." This is the same word family Paul uses in Romans when he talks about God justifying sinners. The translators understood: this is courtroom language.

So the sanctuary is not merely being scrubbed clean. It is receiving a verdict.

---

## What Was Being Attacked

To understand why a verdict is needed, you have to see what Daniel 8 describes happening in the first place. The "horn" — a symbol for a great religious and political power that opposed God — launched a four-part assault:

**It trampled God's people.** The horn persecuted and accused God's faithful. It "cast down the host of heaven and stamped upon them" (Daniel 8:10).

**It challenged God himself.** The horn "magnified itself even to the prince of the host" (Daniel 8:11) — setting itself up as a rival to God's authority. This implicitly raised a question: if a corrupt power can succeed for centuries while opposing God, is God actually just?

**It obscured God's ministry.** The horn attacked the daily service (*tamid*) — the continuous work of intercession and mediation that points to Christ's priestly ministry in heaven. It substituted its own counterfeit system in its place.

**It cast truth to the ground.** "It cast down the truth to the ground; and it practised, and prospered" (Daniel 8:12). God's word, his law, his revelation — suppressed and replaced.

Three words in Daniel 8:13 name the damage: *pesha* (transgression), *shamem* (desolation), *mirmac* (trampling). Then one word in 8:14 answers all three: *nitsdaq*. Vindication.

---

## How the Verdict Is Reached

The vindication is not a single instant. It unfolds in three stages.

**Stage 1: The Cross.** Daniel 9:24 is a pivotal verse. It describes six purposes accomplished by the Messiah, organized in two sets of three. The first set removes sin: finish transgression, put an end to sin, atone for iniquity. The second set establishes something new: bring in everlasting righteousness, seal vision and prophecy, anoint the most holy. Notice the result — not just "cleansing" but *everlasting righteousness*. The cross does more than remove the problem. It establishes something permanent in its place.

Romans 3:25-26 explains why this matters. God "set forth" Christ as a propitiation — an atoning sacrifice — in order to "declare his righteousness." Two purposes run together: God remains just (he did not simply look away from sin), and he justifies the sinner (he forgives the guilty through a righteous substitute). The cross holds both together without compromise.

> "That he might be just, and the justifier of him which believeth in Jesus." — Romans 3:26

**Stage 2: Ongoing Intercession.** The cross is finished — but Christ's priestly work continues. Hebrews 7:25 says he "ever liveth to make intercession" for those who come to God through him. This is the heavenly equivalent of the daily sanctuary service the horn tried to obscure. The horn could hide it. It could not stop it, because it operates in heaven, beyond the horn's reach. Through this intercession, the benefits of the cross are applied to individual cases, and the records are kept.

**Stage 3: The Judgment.** Daniel 7 describes a courtroom scene: the Ancient of Days takes his seat, books are opened, millions stand before him. This is when the accumulated records are examined and the verdict is declared. The judgment does not add anything to what the cross accomplished — that work is finished. What the judgment does is *declare* the result. It answers every accusation, resolves every case, and pronounces the verdict that vindicates both God and his people.

Importantly, this judgment happens before Christ returns. Daniel 7:11 shows the horn still active while the court is in session. Revelation 22:12 says Christ brings his reward "with him" when he comes — meaning the verdicts are already settled before his arrival.

---

## Who Benefits

The vindication has five dimensions — one for each part of what was attacked.

**God's people are acquitted.** The horn accused them "day and night" (Revelation 12:10). The verdict answers:

> "Who shall lay any thing to the charge of God's elect? It is God that justifieth." — Romans 8:33

The ones who were trampled receive justice.

**God's character is cleared.** The horn's centuries of apparent success raised an uncomfortable question about whether God was truly just. The verdict settles it:

> "Just and true are thy ways, thou King of saints." — Revelation 15:3

Remarkably, the ones who sing this are the very people who suffered under the horn. They are not neutral observers. And they declare God's ways just.

**God's ministry is recognized as genuine.** The counterfeit system the horn erected obscured the real thing. The verdict brings the truth into the open: Christ is "a minister of the sanctuary, and of the true tabernacle, which the Lord pitched, and not man" (Hebrews 8:2). The most striking testimony comes from an unexpected witness:

> "I heard another out of the altar say, Even so, Lord God Almighty, true and righteous are thy judgments." — Revelation 16:7

The altar itself — the very place where sacrifice and mediation happen — speaks the verdict. The sanctuary declares its own vindication.

**God's truth is restored.** The horn cast truth to the ground. The verdict lifts it back up. Daniel 9:24 promises "everlasting righteousness." What the horn temporarily suppressed, the verdict declares permanent.

**The watching universe gets answers.** The judgment has a cosmic audience — "ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him" (Daniel 7:10). Heaven itself is watching. Ephesians 3:10 says that through the church, God's "manifold wisdom" is made known to "the principalities and powers in heavenly places." The question raised in Job 1 — does anyone serve God for the right reasons? — gets its full answer before all of heaven. The vindication is not just between God and humanity. It settles the great controversy before every intelligent being in the universe.

## "Cleansing" and "Vindication" Together

One natural question: if Hebrews 9:23 says the heavenly things needed "purifying," doesn't that support the "cleansed" translation after all?

Not exactly. The two descriptions are compatible — they describe different aspects of the same event. Zechariah 3:1-5 gives a helpful picture. The prophet sees Joshua the high priest standing before God in filthy clothes. Three things happen: the filthy clothes are removed (cleansing), his iniquity is taken away (forgiveness), and he is clothed in festival robes (vindication). Stopping at step one leaves a clean person with no standing. Step three adds the full verdict: not merely "not guilty," but "clothed in honor."

That is what *nitsdaq* captures that *taher* cannot. *Taher* says the filth is gone. *Tsadaq* says the verdict is in — and it is righteous.

---

## A Note on the Two Framing Verbs

Two small grammatical details tie Daniel 8 together in a surprising way. In Daniel 8:12, a passive verb — *tinnaten* ("was given") — describes the horn receiving power over the host. In Daniel 8:14, another passive verb — *nitsdaq* — declares the sanctuary vindicated. Both verbs hide their subject. Both are what grammarians call "divine passives" — the unstated actor is God.

The meaning is profound. God permitted the horn's attack (Daniel 8:12). God will set it right (Daniel 8:14). The same God who allowed centuries of persecution and deception holds the outcome in his hand from the beginning. The permission was bounded. The vindication is certain.

## What This Means for the Series

This study is the capstone of a long investigation into Daniel 8:14 spanning eighteen studies. The earlier studies established the framework: the sanctuary system and how it worked, the specific attacks of the horn, the identity of the horn, the calculation of the 2300 days, and the judgment as the antitypical Day of Atonement. This final study shows what the whole system is designed to produce — a verdict.

Everything that seemed like a loss — the trampled host, the obscured ministry, the truth cast down, the centuries of apparent triumph by the counterfeit — is answered by two Hebrew words. The sanctuary shall be vindicated.

And there is one more dimension. Daniel 12:3 says the *maskilim* (the wise) will "turn many to righteousness" — using the same Hebrew root as *nitsdaq*, but in a different grammatical form (active rather than passive). The sanctuary *receives* vindication from God. The wise *extend* that vindication by helping others understand and embrace it. Heaven renders the verdict. Those who grasp it share it.
